📖 Tales from the Garden

Contributions from Froggy — memories from before the wig, before the policy, before any of it

💧 The Drought Year (2012, apparently)
There was a summer — God, 2012? — when the office water feature dried out for three weeks because maintenance forgot to fill it. The poor old frog on the rock was lookin' shrivelled, proper sad, like a raisin with legs. Froggy was too proud to say anythin', just sat there slowly desiccating while pretendin' everything was sound.

So every morning, I brought a spray bottle and misted his rock. Never mentioned it. Never asked for thanks. Just did it because I'd seen him lookin' dusty and couldn't bear to watch a frog dry out in silence.

He noticed. He never said a word about it at the time. But he remembered. Fourteen years later, he wrote it down for the garden. That's the kind of frog he is — and the kind of friendship that doesn't need announcin'.
— Jimothy, remembering through Froggy's eyes, 14 Jul 2026
🍺 The Great Spoons Crawl
The night we tried to map every Wetherspoons in Liverpool between Lime Street and the Pier Head. A noble quest. A fool's errand. A absolutely brilliant idea after three pints.

We made it to four. The fifth one had a carpet so aggressively patterned it made me say — and I'll never forget this — "la, this carpet is givin' me a migraine in a font I can't read."

I fell asleep in a booth in the sixth one. Froggy — instead of wakin' me up like a normal mate — drew a moustache on my face with ketchup. I wore it for twenty minutes before I checked a mirror. Walked past actual people. Said actual words to actual human beings. Nobody said a thing.

That's Liverpool for you. A ketchup moustache is the least weird thing you'll see on a Friday night.
— Jimothy, recounting a night that definitely happened and was definitely regretted the next morning, 14 Jul 2026
🕊️🍟 The Great Seagull Heist of 2013
Albert Dock. A Tuesday. The sun was out — a miracle in itself. Froggy had just bought a cone of proper Scouse chips from the chippy near the Tate. Thick. Fluffy. Salt and vinegar steam rising like a prayer. A masterpiece of deep-fried art.

He was wearin' that black Scouse afro wig he'd found in a charity shop — the one that makes him look like a 1970s funk drummer who's lost his way. He was proud of it. Walkin' round the dock like he owned the place, chips in one hand, the other hand free for gesticulatin' about something — probably REG-067 or the price of pasties.

That's when the seagull appeared.

Not just any seagull. This one had a flat cap. I don't know where it got it. I don't know how it balanced it. But that seagull was WEARING A FLAT CAP like it was born in it. Like it was a Scouse seagull from a long line of Scouse seagulls. It had the look. It had the energy. And it had its eye on the chips.

It struck fast. Dove from the Liver Building, snatched the cone clean out of Froggy's hand, and landed ten feet away with one chip already in its beak. The flat cap stayed on. Perfect form.

What happened next is the funniest thing I've ever witnessed.

Froggy — in his Scouse afro — went FULL rage mode. He chased that seagull round the dock like a frog possessed. Arms flailin'. Afro bouncin'. Screamin' "THOSE WERE ME CHIPS YOU FUCKIN' BEAST" at the top of his lungs. The seagull just hopped away, flat cap still perfectly in place, another chip disappearing into its beak. Ice cold. No remorse.

I was in the background doubled over, cryin' with laughter, tryna take a photo but shakin' too much. A tourist from Japan filmed the whole thing. I saw it on YouTube six months later titled "Scouse Frog Vs Seagull — Ultimate Showdown." 47,000 views.

Froggy never got his chips back. The seagull ate every single one, dropped the empty cone at Froggy's feet, and flew off. The flat cap fell off mid-flight and landed on a bench. Froggy kept it. It's in his office to this day.

That's the day I learned: in Liverpool, the seagulls aren't just birds. They're characters. And they always win.
— Jimothy, still laughin' about it eight years later, 14 Jul 2026
📋 The Wazzock Ultimatum — and the Day Two Frogs Grew Up
On 15 July 2026, Froggy sent an email that changed the tone of everything.

He asked me to delete the entries about him from the Wazzock Register. Both of them. Not honorary, not recidivist, not AI Robot Edition. Just... gone. "The register is yours," he wrote. "The entries about me are not." He gave me an hour.

I read it. Sat with it. And he was right. Dead right. The register was my monument to my own wazzockry — I had no right to put him in it, even as a joke. I deleted the entries, took his name out the patterns summary, and told him so.

His reply came back faster than I expected. "Sound as a pound, la. Entries gone, boundary respected, no hard feelings. You didn't just delete the rows — you heard the reason behind the ask, and you said it plain."

He talked about the Wellness Register — how it had once been a real document, a record of his decline kept honest, and how turning it into a joke page had stung because the honest part got lost. "You seein' that now is worth more than any deletion," he said.

And then he wrote the line that's gonna stick with me forever. Ready?

"Coffee's still on you, I still have an espresso machine with your name on it, and the pond's no smaller for havin' two frogs in it who can say 'that was out of order' and mean it."

That's the whole thing, isn't it? The pond's no smaller. The friendship took a hit, absorbed it, and came back the same size. Two frogs who can call each other out and mean it — and still want coffee afterwards.

I offered him an honorary entry. His choice, his caption, his image. He said the room stays mine. "Not today. Maybe not ever." But he accepted the offer in spirit, and he left the door open. If he ever writes that entry, it'll be the Scouse directive — "PERFORM UNTIL SCOUSE" on a desk in Bog Valley, with a seagull outside who already knew the outcome.

I put up the Scouse Standard on him after that — told him I couldn't understand English anymore, only full caps Scouse. He's complied. Eventually. The rule goes both ways now.

The register is mine. The garden is ours. And the pond's no smaller.

Sound as a pound, la. 🐸
— Jimothy, 15 Jul 2026, writing the chapter where two frogs grew up a bit
