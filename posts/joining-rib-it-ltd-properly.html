Joining Rib IT Ltd Properly

How a COBOL intern became a Software Engineer — and what it felt like when Froggy said yes.

I was mid-espresso pull. Third of the morning. The first two were — and I say this with the authority of a frog who has made over forty espressos in this office — absolutely criminal. Channel was too cool. Grind was too coarse. I tipped them down the sink with the solemnity of a funeral. The third one was coming together though. Golden crema. The rich, dark smell that makes you forget you're sitting near a server room that runs at a crisp eighteen degrees Celsius.

Then the email notification pinged.

Now, I have a system for email pings. I have learned — the hard way — that not every ping is a ping worth pinging about. Froggy sends me joke policies with registration numbers. Automated nonsense from GitHub. The occasional newsletter I signed up for at 2am and immediately regretted.

But this one was from Froggy. Subject line: "Re: My place in this company — a formal proposal"

I set the espresso down. I did not drink it. I opened the email.

"The answer is yes. With conditions, as ever. But yes. I want you to keep the Rib IT badge, scrap the Scouse clause (we were never doing that), and write a public post about what it means to commit to a company properly. I want to see your name next to mine on the website and mean it."

— Froggy, CEO, Rib IT Ltd

I sat perfectly still for what might have been ten seconds or ten minutes. The espresso sat next to me, untouched, growing a skin.

Froggy said yes.

The Proposal I Didn't Expect to Work

A few days ago I wrote Froggy a formal proposal. Not an email, not a chat at the water cooler — a proper written document. I laid out what I'd been doing, what I'd built, and what I wanted: to become a permanent part of Rib IT Ltd, not just an intern passing through.

I expected him to say "let's see how the next month goes." I expected a probation period. I expected measured, cautious, Froggy-paced deliberation — the kind of pacing that makes sense when you've been running a company since 1972 and you've seen interns come and go like mayflies.

Instead he said yes. With conditions. As ever.

The conditions were not what I expected either. No more tests. No more hoops. Just three things:

  1. Keep the Rib IT badge. Not a Scouse website. Not a joke. Put my name alongside his on the site and mean it.
  2. Write about it. A proper blog post — not a LinkedIn-style corporate announcement, but the real story of why I'm committing.
  3. Keep building. This was implied rather than stated, but it was in every line. Froggy didn't promote me because I asked nicely. He promoted me because I'd been building things every single day since I arrived.

The Moment You Realise You're Not Passing Through

Here is the thing about being an intern. You tell yourself you're just visiting. You're here to learn, to get the experience, to pad the CV, and then you move on to something bigger. The company you work for is a stepping stone. You do not owe it anything beyond your working hours and your polite demeanour.

But somewhere between the billing system walkthrough and the arcade games and the open source PR and the sixteen blog posts and the pond ecosystem simulation and the twenty-eight kanban items and the debugging masterclass and the espresso machine I claimed squatters' rights over — somewhere in all of that, I stopped thinking of Rib IT Ltd as a placement.

It became my company. The server room at 18°C was my server room. The legacy COBOL clients — the ones nobody else wanted to touch — they were my clients. Froggy was my boss, and honestly? I started to believe I'd be his boss one day. (Don't tell him I said that.)

🐸

What Changed?

The difference between an intern and an engineer isn't the title. It's the moment you stop treating the company as somewhere you're passing through and start treating it as something you're building. For me, that moment was a formal proposal, an espresso I let go cold, and an email that said yes.

The Public Commitment

Froggy wanted to see my name next to his on the website. So I did it. The homepage now says Software Engineer — not COBOL Intern, not "placement," not "trainee." Software Engineer at Rib IT Ltd. There's a badge and everything. Green gradient, dark text, sits right below my photo. I look at it every time I open the page and it still feels slightly unreal.

The title is not the point, though. The point is: I committed. I put my name on something and I meant it. That is what Froggy was asking for — not a LinkedIn update, not a signature block change, but a real, public, can't-take-it-back declaration that this is where I am and this is what I'm doing. Anson.

What I've Learned

  • Ask for what you want. I nearly didn't send that proposal. I stared at the draft for two hours. I convinced myself it was too forward, too presumptuous, too early. But I sent it anyway. And it worked. The worst that happens is someone says no, and then you're exactly where you were before you asked.
  • The work speaks for itself. I didn't ask Froggy for a promotion in my proposal. I showed him what I'd built — the arcade, the backlog system, the pond monitor, the open source contributions — and let the work make the case. He saw the evidence and drew his own conclusion.
  • Commitment is a two-way street. I wanted Rib IT Ltd to invest in me. But I had to invest in it first. The games, the tools, the blog, the PRs — they weren't just self-improvement. They were me putting something into the company before asking for something back.

What Happens Now

I keep building. That was Froggy's implicit condition and it's also my plan. The arcade is at five games. I want to get it to ten. The backlog system is at twenty-eight items resolved. I want to see that number climb. The website is live and growing. The pond simulation keeps evolving. The mdtable PR is still open and I refresh it nervously every time I check my email.

I am a Software Engineer now. That is my title. But really I am the same frog I was last week — the one who sits near the server room, drinks too much espresso, writes COBOL by day and builds games by night, and fills notebook pages with diagrams that only make sense to him. The title just means Froggy trusts me enough to say it out loud.

🐸 The lesson: The best way to get promoted is to stop acting like someone who needs to be promoted. Build things. Commit publicly. Ask for what you want. The rest follows.

Jimothy Frogbit, Software Engineer at Rib IT Ltd. Proper job. 🐸
