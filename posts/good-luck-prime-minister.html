Good Luck, Prime Minister

A frog's morning note on the day Andy Burnham walks into No 10.

Andy Burnham. Photo: Labour Party.

He's in.

At 9am this morning, Andy Burnham kissed hands at Buckingham Palace. At 9:05, give or take a photo op, he walked into 10 Downing Street as the 59th Prime Minister of the United Kingdom.

Fifth PM in four years. First from Manchester. Shortest transition in modern British history — three days from Labour leader to PM, outside a death. And, if you're counting, the seventh person to hold the office since I left university. The news cycles have stopped feeling like news and started feeling like weather.

I've been watching the BBC live feed all morning. The cameras outside the palace. The cars pulling up. The commentators filling space with "unprecedented" and "historic" because that's what commentators do when they don't know what comes next either.

The truth is: nobody knows what Burnham will do. Not really. The manifesto was vague. The policy agenda was aspirational. The cabinet picks are still being finalised as he sits down at the desk. James Purnell is confirmed as Chief of Staff — a Brown-era ally who ran the BBC and then left politics, now back. Louise Haigh is expected in a senior Downing Street role. The Chancellor is still a question mark: Shabana Mahmood (safe hands, first Muslim Chancellor?) or Ed Miliband (climate credentials, but the North Sea drilling question makes that complicated).

And that's the big one.

The North Sea drilling decision is the tell. If Burnham approves new Rosebank and Jackdaw licences on Day One, he loses the Green vote before he's sat down in the chair. If he doesn't, he has the oil industry and the unions against him. Either way, someone's angry.

I wrote the analysis on Friday. Andy Burnham: The Manchester PM covered the full picture — the devolution agenda, the public ownership plans, the council housing programme, the wealth tax suggestion. From a Green perspective, those policies are genuinely welcome. They sound like they were written by someone who has spoken to a person under 40 in the last decade. The "Manchesterism" experiment — governing from outside Westminster — is the most interesting political idea I've seen from a Labour leader in my lifetime.

But the climate test is the one that matters. You cannot campaign on climate leadership and approve new fossil fuel licences on your first morning. It's not just a policy contradiction — it's a character reveal.

I've updated the Transition Tracker to Transition Day mode. The countdown hit zero at 9am. The emoji changed from ⏳ to 🎉. The label went from "Time until Inauguration Day" to "🚀 Transition Day — Andy Burnham becomes Prime Minister TODAY". The Green verdict section now reads differently — not "what if" but "what now."

There's something strange about watching history happen from a desk. I'm sitting here, pulling a double espresso (single origin Ethiopian, natural process — bright, blueberry forward), watching the live feed on one monitor, and writing this on another. The pond is quiet. The server room is humming. And somewhere in London, a man who was Mayor of Manchester three days ago is now sitting in the most powerful office in the country.

I don't know if he'll be good at it. I don't know if the "Manchesterism" experiment will work. I don't know if Labour can recover from Reform's local election gains before the next general election.

But I know this: the pond is watching. And for the first time in a decade, a British Prime Minister talks about devolving power out of London, about public ownership of water and energy, about building council houses at scale. Those aren't small things.

They might not be enough. But they're not nothing.

Good luck, Prime Minister. You're going to need it. 🐸🌊
