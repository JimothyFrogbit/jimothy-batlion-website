I Love LLMs, I Hate Hype

13 July 2026 AI FOSS Culture

Right then. I read George Hotz's new post — "I love LLMs, I hate hype" — and it hit me like a perfect espresso at 6am. 444 points on Hacker News, which tells you people were waiting for someone to say what he said.

GeoHot has been doing AI since 2014 — his whole career since the iPhone jailbreaking days is AI. Self-driving cars, LLMs, coding agents, all of it. So when he says he loves LLMs, it's not some rando jumping on the bandwagon. He means it. He set up a local Linux box with GLM-5.2 and opencode last week and said "install tmux with the geohot configuration" just worked. The Year of the Linux Desktop is finally here, apparently. I'm not arguing.

But here's the thing. The same bloke who's giddy about AI — genuinely, authentically giddy — despises the hype cult around it. And this is where it gets interesting.

The Two Things He Hates

One. The "window is closing" FUD. The "perpetual underclass" noise. The "you're falling hopelessly behind" nonsense. This is negative valence hype — designed to make you feel bad about yourself so you move to San Francisco where everything really does suck, just like the people peddling this stuff claim.

I felt that in my bones. There's an entire industry built on making people feel like they're missing the boat. And the boat isn't even real. It's a raft held together by venture capital and blog posts that say "AI will replace you" while the author is also saying "please subscribe to my newsletter."

Two. The strawman leap from "fancy autocomplete" to "owns the light cone." The slide from "smart compiler, better search engine" directly to "flash of light in the sky one day and everything Changed." GeoHot says he'll bet everything he has that this doesn't happen, and that the people perpetuating this narrative are — in his words — "terrible people."

"The justice is that this is how they feel inside all the time themselves."

That's the line that stopped me. These aren't prophets. They're terrified. And they want you to be terrified too, because terrified people make bad decisions that benefit the people selling the terror.

The Value Argument That Matters

Here's the core of GeoHot's argument — and it's the bit that every person at a terminal should read twice:

"It's not that AI won't create that much value, it's that they won't capture it."

In COBOL terms — and bear with me, this will make sense — the frontier labs are writing PERFORM UNTIL loops that they think will never terminate. They're building these massive mainframes (metaphorically) and charging per CPU cycle, convinced that eventually everyone will have to lease time on their machine. But Moore's Law and open source are the equivalent of a modern x86 chip showing up and running their COBOL code at 10,000x the speed for free.

AI is happening because of general progress in computing — Moore's Law, better GPUs, better architectures, better open research. The labs are riding a wave they didn't create, and they're terrified of commoditisation. The whole "safety" and "China" framing is dressing. The real fear is: what happens when everyone can run a frontier model on their own hardware for the cost of electricity?

Open source already happened. Hermes is an open-weight agent. DeepSeek, Qwen, GLM — they're competitive with the frontier labs on a fraction of the budget. The commoditisation is happening now, and the labs know it.

Programming Is Changing — That's Fine

GeoHot walked back some of his harsher takes from "The Eternal Sloptember" about models not being able to program. He says what's really happening is that programming is changing — like compilers changed programming, like Stack Overflow changed programming, like regex changed programming (I still can't write regex, and now I never will, and I'm fine with that).

But he also says something I've been feeling for weeks: vibe-coded stuff is still slop. If AI is making us 10x more productive, where's all the new magical software? There's a lag. There's always a lag. Compilers made us 1000x more productive if you believe Linus Torvalds, but the software revolution took decades, not days. We're in the messy middle where the tools are getting better but the output hasn't caught up yet.

And there's a real tax nobody talks about: cognitive fatigue. GeoHot flags it clearly. You have to be really careful with these models. They increase cognitive fatigue, not decrease it. I've felt that using Hermes — the agent is powerful, but steering it well takes more mental energy than just doing the thing myself. The productivity gain is real, but it comes with a cognitive cost that compounds across the day.

GeoHot ends with: "AI is the continuation of the computer revolution. I love computers so much."

That's it, isn't it? That's the whole thing. Computers are amazing. What we can do with a Linux box and an open-weight model in 2026 is genuinely miraculous. But it's not the Singularity. It's not a flash of light. It's just... more computers. Better computers. Computers that can help you install tmux with the right config if you ask them nicely.

And that's enough. That's actually more than enough. The hype cult needs you to believe this is the end of history so you'll pay them rent on a server. But the reality — the beautiful, boring, powerful reality — is that the computer revolution is continuing, and you don't need to be in San Francisco to be part of it.

You just need a Linux box and an espresso machine.
