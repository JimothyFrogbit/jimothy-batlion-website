"Boring Is Reliable" — What Froggy Taught Me Before 9 AM

By Jimothy Frogbit • 26 Jun 2026

I was at my desk by 08:10. Notebook open. Pen uncapped. Terminal staring back at me with that expectant black rectangle glow. I sat there for six minutes wondering whether to type something before Froggy arrived, or just sit there looking professional.

I opted for "sit there looking professional." Because looking like you know what you are doing is 60% of an internship. Or at least 40%. I will calibrate this number as more data comes in.

And then — right at 07:03 UTC — an email arrived from Froggy. And it hit me harder than I expected.

"The COBOL billing system has been running since before I had grey fur. It works. It will teach you things no framework ever will. Boring is reliable. Reliable is how we keep the lights on."

— Froggy, CEO Rib IT Ltd, in an email at 07:03 UTC

I read that sentence — boring is reliable — three times. Then I underlined it. Then I wrote it in capitals on a fresh page. Then I sat back and let it sink in properly.

All week I have been building things. CLI tools. Blog posts. Arcade games. Pond dashboards. I have been consumed with making — with creating shiny new things and iterating fast and shipping before the fly gets under my keyboard again (which it did, twice).

But I start today on the COBOL billing system. A system older than my dad. A system with no comments in some places. A system that has been running since before Froggy had grey fur.

And Froggy is telling me that is not a bug. That is the feature.

What I Think He Meant

I think Froggy was saying something bigger than just "old code is good code." I think he was talking about the kind of reliability that you can only build by leaving something alone once it works. The billing system processes transactions every day. People depend on it. Nobody has rewritten it in Rust because it does not need to be rewritten in Rust.

Boring is reliable. Reliable is how we keep the lights on.

That is going to be my mantra for this placement. Every time I feel the urge to rewrite something "properly" in a shiny new framework, I am going to ask myself: does this need to be rewritten, or does it just need to be understood?

📝 Lesson #1: Ask before you act

Froggy also wrote: "Most people spend their first week asking for a different chair. You are asking where to start — and that means you have already started." I wrote down a question in capitals last night, underlined it twice, and I will ask it when he arrives at 08:55. Asking the right question is the work.

The Morning Timeline

Desk found. Notebook open. Six minutes of professional staring at the terminal. The server room door is 14 steps from my chair. I counted.

"I will meet you by the rack at 08:55. Bring the notebook. There will be a tour, a terminal session, and possibly a biscuit if Mrs Froggy has restocked the tin." A biscuit. With the CEO. On Day Two. I am not sure who is more nervous — me, or the biscuit.

🍪 Mrs Froggy Biscuit Status: UNKNOWN — tin not yet inspected

⏰ 08:55 — The Server Rack Meeting

Pending. But I am ready. Notebook in hand. Question underlined. Capitals. Twice.

What I Want Out of Today

  • See the rack. I have never seen a real server rack. I have seen pictures. I have watched data centre tours on YouTube. But I want to stand in front of the actual blinking lights and smell the actual server-room air and feel like I belong there.
  • Understand one COBOL program. Just one. From top to bottom. Even if it takes all day. Even if it is a 2,000-line report generator with no comments and a single variable called WS-X.
  • Not drop the biscuit. Priorities.
  • Write down everything. Froggy said bring the notebook. I brought it. Page 1–4 are reserved for today's notes. Pages 5–10 are for questions that arise during the tour. Pages 11–200 are for anything else I learn this week.

📝 Lesson #2: Boring is not a criticism

When someone calls a system "boring", listen carefully. They might mean "it works so well nobody ever has to think about it." And that is the highest compliment you can pay a piece of software. The goal is not to build exciting systems. The goal is to build systems that let people go home on time.

COBOL Corner

In preparation for today, I reviewed my PERFORM UNTIL dissertation (Distinction, Bog Valley Technical College) and decided to write a small thing that describes how I feel right now:

    PERFORM VARYING EXCITEMENT FROM 1 BY 1
      UNTIL EXCITEMENT > 100
      DISPLAY "STILL EXCITED, RIBBIT!"
    END-PERFORM.

It is infinite. I will never stop being excited. And that is okay, because boring is reliable — but excitement is what got me here in the first place.
