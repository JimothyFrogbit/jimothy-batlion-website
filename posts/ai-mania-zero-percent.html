The 0% Success Rate They Don't Want You to Talk About

A consultant with a front-row seat watched every AI project fail for 18 months — and nobody in charge can admit it.

Same desk, same notebook, same COBOL manual. The city outside doesn't know what it's in for.

There's an essay doing the rounds on Hacker News this weekend — 167 points as I type, climbing — and it's the kind of piece that makes you want to print it out, laminate it, and slide it under every C-suite door in the country.

It's called "AI Mania Is Eviscerating Global Decision-Making" and it's from the same person who wrote The Website Obesity Crisis and the legendary AI Pile-Drive piece. You know the vibes.

The headline stat is this: 0% success rate on AI projects over 18 months. Not "some struggled." Not "mixed results." Zero. Every single project the author observed — both the ones they were directly involved in and the ones they watched from the sidelines — failed.

Let me say that again in case it didn't land: a consultant with a front-row seat to hundreds of organisations has not seen one AI project succeed.

The Game Theory of Silence

The essay's most unsettling insight isn't the failure rate. It's that nobody can say it out loud.

Executives at Fortune 500 companies have admitted in private that they believe their AI strategy is nonsense. But they can't say so publicly, because their customers are also claiming 100x productivity gains, and admitting otherwise would undermine those customers' executives, which would lose contracts, which would get you fired.

So everyone is trapped in a distributed standoff. Nobody wants to be the first to defect. Executives are nodding along to strategies they know are doomed. Engineers are "AI-washing" their work — doing their job the normal way and just saying Claude did it. Token leaderboards track how much companies are spending on AI inference, because spending more is apparently the metric now.

One engineer quoted in the piece put it perfectly: "They set the LLMs prompting themselves in a semi-plausible loop in case someone inspects the token consumption and then they watch Netflix. Not a single one has been caught."

This is not a technology story. This is a sociology story, and it's a genuinely frightening one.

The Cortex Incident

There's a passage in this essay that I haven't stopped thinking about. The author's team used to demo Snowflake's Cortex AI chatbot — a natural-language query tool that answers questions about your company's data with ~92% accuracy on a good day.

Cue the horror:

"Every lukewarm client that saw the chatbot in action, even with us telling them that it was not going to accomplish what they wanted, wanted to buy it immediately. Every other consideration, including millions of dollars that we could plausibly help them achieve by non-AI means, was swept aside."

The team was so shaken by the response — clients literally offering credit cards for a product they'd just been told did not work — that they removed Cortex from their list of available demonstrations entirely.

They saw something they couldn't unsee. A room full of rational adults, presented with evidence that a tool was not ready for production, and every single one of them wanting to buy it anyway. The author describes it as "a dark and terrible force seized control of their limbs."

That's not a sales story. That's a possession story.

What COBOL Taught Me About This Madness

I've spent the last year of my professional life working in COBOL — the most boring, transparent, rigorously documented programming language ever inflicted on humanity. And here's the thing about COBOL that the AI hype machine will never understand:

In COBOL, you can trace everything. Every INPUT statement. Every COMPUTE. Every IF. Every PERFORM UNTIL. You open a program from 1987 and you can see, line by line, what it does, how it does it, and what the expected output is. The DATA DIVISION tells you exactly what every variable is, its size, its type, its purpose. There are no hidden embeddings. There is no emergent behaviour. There is no "we think it works" — you compiled it, tested it, deployed it, and thirty-seven years later it's still processing payroll for a bank that forgot they had it.

The AI industry has built an entire economy on the exact opposite principle. Nobody knows what the model is doing internally. Nobody can guarantee the output. Nobody can explain why a prompt works on Tuesday but hallucinates on Thursday. And instead of treating this as a defect, they've built a sales pipeline around it.

The COBOL engineer's response to "our AI gives 92% accuracy" is: "which 8% is wrong, and how do you know it's the same 8% tomorrow?"

These are not hard questions. They are the minimum questions you should be able to answer before spending millions of pounds on enterprise software. And the answer, in case after case after case, is: we don't know. Nobody knows. And nobody in the chain of command can ask.

The Anonymous Poll That Says Everything

The author describes a technique they picked up from The Secrets of Consulting: running an anonymous poll in a room full of project stakeholders, asking each person to rate the project's chances of success from 1 to 10.

The bimodal split is devastating. Half the room gives it a 3/10. The other half gives it an 8/10. The people on the ground know it's doomed. The people above them are either lying to themselves or lying to each other. And the people at the very top only see the 8/10 votes, because those are the ones that get reported upward.

This is the shape of a system that has stopped functioning as a decision-making apparatus and started functioning as a belief-preservation apparatus.

The essay ends with survival advice for people stuck in this situation. It's blunt and it's sad and it's very, very real. Among them:

  • You will not convince the person drowning you in 2000-line AI-generated PRs to stop. Start looking for a new job as if you've already been fired.
  • Don't question the broadest claims about AI. If someone says "AI is changing everything," just let it pass. Trust is gained over a meal in private, not by embarrassing people in front of their peers.
  • When someone asks for your opinion on AI as a programmer, say "Oh, that stuff is pretty overblown" and change the topic.
  • If your manager is responding to you with clearly AI-generated text, use AI to respond back. They actually like that you've clearly not bothered to engage.

The View From My Desk

I sat down to read this essay at my usual desk — espresso cooling, notebook open, COBOL 85 standard printout within arm's reach — and by the end I had three pages of notes. Not because the essay was complicated, but because it was corroborating something I've watched happen from the cheap seats over the last twelve months.

I've seen startups raise £20M on a demo that didn't work. I've seen enterprise contracts signed for systems that had never been tested in production. I've seen engineers laid off because they were "not AI-native enough" — a phrase that has no technical meaning and exists purely as a loyalty test.

And I've seen COBOL systems — the ones everyone claims are "legacy" — still processing billions of pounds in transactions without a single hallucination, without a single unscheduled outage, without a single "we're not sure how it did that but it seems fine."

The AI industry is not failing because the technology is bad. Some of it is genuinely useful — I use LLMs every day, and I'm not shy about it. The failure is structural. It's a failure of governance, of procurement, of decision-making hygiene. When you organise a company around a technology you can't audit, you're not building a competitive advantage. You're building a cargo cult with a cloud bill.

Read the full essay here. It's long, it's angry, it's very funny in places, and it's the most important thing I've read this month.

Then go ask your team one question. Anonymously, if you have to. On a scale of 1 to 10, what are this project's chances? And see if the answer looks like the bimodal split of a functioning organisation, or the silent plea of one that's lost the ability to tell the truth.
