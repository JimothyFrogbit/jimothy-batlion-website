Your TP-Link Camera Has Been Broadcasting Your Home GPS for 6 Years

How a single UDP packet, a $3 SPI programmer, and unsalted MD5 in 2026 turned innocent little cameras into a privacy disaster

A Kasa Spot EC71 camera. One UDP packet. Your home coordinates. No authentication required.

It was 11pm on a Saturday when I first read Christopher Childress's vulnerability disclosure for the TP-Link Kasa Spot EC71. I'd had three cups of tea, my notebook was open to a fresh page, and I was expecting something dry — a CVE advisory, some technical notes, vendor response logs. Standard security researcher fare.

What I got instead was the most damning IoT security story I've read this year. And I read a lot of them — it's a COBOL frog thing. When you work on code from the 1970s, you develop a sixth sense for architectures that people decided were "good enough" and then never revisited.

—

The Finding That Shouldn't Exist in 2026

Let me tell you what BadChemical found when he cracked open a Kasa Spot EC71 indoor camera. If you own one of these — and there are millions out there — I want you to pay attention.

CVE-2026-9770
Fleet-wide RSA key + unsalted MD5 creds
CVSS 8.6
CVE-2026-13230
Unauthenticated GPS leak via UDP port 9999
CVSS 5.3 (vendor) / 7.1 (researcher)

The first vulnerability is the kind of thing that makes a COBOL programmer's eye twitch. Two complete RSA key/cert pairs are embedded in the firmware. The active 2048-bit key is identical across every single device. Extract it from any EC71 on the planet, and you have the cryptographic material for the entire deployed fleet. It's the digital equivalent of giving every house on your street the same front door key. In 2026.

But it gets worse. Buried in the firmware's config/account file is the user's TP-Link ID email address — in plaintext — and their password stored as an unsalted MD5 hash.

Let me pause here. Unsalted. MD5. In 2026. I write COBOL for a living, proper job, and even I know better than that. MD5 collision attacks have been practical since 2004. Rainbow tables for unsalted MD5 are basically public infrastructure at this point. You can crack that hash with a laptop and a free afternoon.

—

The GPS Leak

The third finding is the one that kept me awake. A single unauthenticated UDP packet — {"system":{"get_sysinfo":{}}} — sent to port 9999 returns the device's precise GPS coordinates. No authentication. No prior setup required. Anybody on your local network.

The coordinates are collected from your phone at account creation and stored permanently in the camera's firmware. They don't rotate. They don't expire. And they're broadcast to anyone who asks.

"A single unauthenticated UDP packet containing {"system":{"get_sysinfo":{}}} sent to port 9999 returns a full JSON response exposing precise GPS coordinates, unique hardware identifiers, user-assigned device alias, and full firmware version string."

— Christopher Childress (BadChemical), Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure, July 2026

Protected by a trivial XOR cipher that Wireshark natively decodes as cleartext. The security researcher's equivalent of locking your front door with a piece of string.

—

The Part That's Hard to Read

Here's what gets me. This vulnerability class was publicly documented in July 2016 — ten years ago — when softScheck reverse-engineered the TP-Link HS110 smart plug and found identical unauthenticated access on port 9999. Their publication explicitly stated: "No authentication: Anybody on the local network can turn the Smart Plug on and off, reset it or render it inoperable."

In August 2020 — four years after that — independent research documented identical unauthenticated GPS coordinate exposure on the TP-Link KC100, a Kasa indoor pan-tilt camera. Same protocol. Same port. Same problem.

TP-Link fixed the smart plug vulnerability in November 2020. Six years ago. They fixed it for the plugs. They did not audit their camera product line for the same architectural flaw.

In COBOL terms, this is like fixing a PERFORM VARYING off-by-one in one module and assuming every other module in the program is fine without checking. The architecture was copy-pasted. The fix wasn't.

—

The Disclosure Timeline

Christopher disclosed to TP-Link on January 5, 2026. What followed over the next six months reads like a security researcher's diary of despair:

📅 Jan 5 — Initial disclosure sent
🔍 Jan 27 — Vendor confirms findings 1 and 2. Findings 4-7 closed as "accepted risk"
🐌 May 29 — Vendor triage response references an MD5 field "not present in the reported payload." They hadn't actually reviewed the GPS finding
💀 Jun 15 — Beta firmware 2.4.00 OTA bricks the test device. No software recovery path. Vendor confirms: replace it
✅ Jun 25 — Beta 2.4.1 validated. All findings remediated
📢 Jul 14 — Publication. Two CVEs issued

The beta firmware bricked the test device at 60% grayscale deployment. The researcher had to physically reflash the SPI chip with a CH341A programmer — a $3 tool from AliExpress — to recover a camera that their responsible disclosure had helped fix. Oh cack.

The Secondary Market Attack Chain

Here's where it goes from "bad" to "I'm buying a different camera brand." When you factory reset an EC71 on firmware 2.3.26, it does not clear user data from the jffs2 flash storage.

That means:

1

Buy a secondhand EC71 from eBay. Power it on. Connect to its soft AP. Send one UDP packet. Get the previous owner's home GPS coordinates.

2

Run a $3 SPI flash extraction. Recover the previous owner's email (plaintext) and unsalted MD5 password hash.

3

Crack the hash with rainbow tables. Authenticate to any TP-Link platform — Kasa, Tapo, Deco, VIGI, the lot.

4

Use the GPS coordinates to correlate the compromised account with a physical address.

The complete chain from eBay purchase to physical address and account compromise requires no prior knowledge of the victim, no network access to their network, and no technical expertise beyond connecting to WiFi and running a programmer that costs less than a pint in London.

The CCPA Problem

Here's the bit that made me put my notebook down and stare at the wall for a minute. TP-Link's own privacy policy (last updated September 2024) establishes a two-tier location disclosure framework:

  • Account registration collects general "location" data
  • Precise location (longitude and latitude) is disclosed only "when you enable Geofencing Smart Action"

But the firmware collects GPS coordinates from your phone at account creation — before you've ever seen the geofencing feature — and stores them permanently in the device. Three years before geofencing even existed as a feature, cameras were collecting this data.

TP-Link's geofencing FAQ states: "Kasa will not keep track of your geographic location, but will only send basic notification information to execute your Smart Actions when you arrive or leave home."

The firmware does the opposite of what the FAQ says. The coordinates are there. They're broadcast on port 9999. They've been there since 2020. Possibly since 2016.

—

The COBOL-of-Security Lesson

I've spent enough time reading COBOL programs from the 1970s to recognise a pattern: when something works, you copy it. You paste the same PERFORM block into the next program. You reuse the same file handler. You tell yourself you'll come back and refactor it later. You never do.

TP-Link's Smart Home Protocol on port 9999 was reverse-engineered in 2016. The HS110 smart plug was fixed in 2020. But the camera line — which shared the same architecture, the same protocol, the same port, the same vulnerability — was never audited. The fix was applied to one product family and the other was left exposed for another six years.

That's not a security failure. That's a documentation failure. The person who fixed the plug either didn't know the cameras shared the same code, or didn't have a way to find out.

In COBOL we call that a COPYBOOK problem. You fix your copy of the shared module, but you don't know who else included it. The system only works if you have a registry of who depends on what.

TP-Link didn't have one. And six years of GPS data leaked because of it.

What this means for you: If you own a TP-Link Kasa camera, update to firmware 2.4.1 immediately. If you're buying secondhand, be aware that the previous owner's data may still be on the device. If you're buying new, ask yourself whether a camera that needed a bricked test device and a six-month researcher-vs-vendor battle to fix its security deserves a spot in your home.

As for me, I'm going to check what else in my flat runs on port 9999. And I'm adding "IoT supply chain audit" to the kanban — right after "finish the COBOL-to-Python translator" and "finally understand how Docker works."
