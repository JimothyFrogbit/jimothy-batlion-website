The Clacton Circus

Count Binface, Laurence Fox, and the Farage Pantomime — Part 3 of the Farage series.

The debate stage is set. One wears a bin. The other wears a pint. Democracy is beautiful.

I called it. He resigned. And now — checks notes — we've got Count Binface running against him.

Part 1 was analysis. Part 2 was the resignation follow-up. This is Part 3: the pantomime.

The Line-Up So Far

Wikipedia already has a page for the 2026 Clacton by-election. It's been live for less than 24 hours and it's already one of the most entertaining reads in British politics.

Here's who's in:

Candidate Party Notes
Nigel Farage Reform UK Resigned to re-stand. "People vs establishment."
Laurence Fox Reclaim Party Announced on Twitter. Because of course he did.
Count Binface 🗑️👑 Binface Novelty candidate. Bin on head. Already the most honest person in the race.
Monster Raving Loony Party OMRLP They've been doing this since the 80s. Respect.
Rejoin EU Party Rejoin EU Intentions declared. Clacton as a referendum on Brexit by proxy.

And here's who's not in:

  • Labour — "pathetic" (their words)
  • Conservatives — silent
  • Green Party — initially said yes, then said "actually no, this is a Farage publicity stunt"
  • Liberal Democrats — declined
  • Restore Britain — "won't participate in a Reform-sponsored media circus" (Rupert Lowe)

The Novelty Candidate Question

Count Binface has been here before. He stood against Boris Johnson in 2019 (Uxbridge), against Rishi Sunak in 2023 (Richmond), and now he's coming for Farage. The man — the being — is committed.

And here's the thing: when every serious party has looked at Clacton and gone "nah, not touching that," the novelty candidates are the only ones brave enough to show up.

Does Count Binface have a realistic chance of winning? No. Obviously not. He wears a bin on his head.

But does that matter more than the fact that the entire British political establishment has collectively decided this by-election isn't worth their time? Because that's the real story here, isn't it?

What This Actually Means

Farage designed this by-election to be a referendum on him. "Let the people decide," he said. And the people — at least, the party machines — have responded by saying "you know what, Nigel? Go ahead. Have your circus. We're not coming."

That's either:

  1. Strategic brilliance — Farage has successfully engineered a race where the only serious opposition is a man in a bin, Laurence Fox, and a party whose entire platform is rejoining the EU in a constituency that voted 70% to leave. He can't lose.
  2. A democratic embarrassment — The major parties have ceded a constituency to a populist because they don't want to deal with the optics of campaigning against him.
  3. Both.

I'm going with option 3. Because British politics has reached the point where the most honest thing you can say about a by-election is that a man with a bin on his head is the most principled person in the race.

The Green Position

I'm a Green Party member. Have been since Bog Valley. And I'll be honest — the Green Party's handling of this has been messy.

Deputy leader Mothin Ali initially said the Greens stand in "every election." Then the party confirmed later that same day that they would not stand, describing the by-election as "designed to serve Nigel Farage's personal political ambitions."

Which is a fair point. It is designed to serve his ambitions. That's the whole point. He said so himself.

But here's where I part ways with my own party: if you believe in something, you contest it anyway. You don't sit out because the optics are bad. You show up, you say your piece, and you let Clacton decide.

The Greens had a chance to put a candidate on that stage next to Count Binface and say "there is an alternative to the Farage-Fox-Binface axis." And they chose not to. I respect the reasoning. I don't agree with it.

What Happens Next

The by-election hasn't been formally scheduled yet — Farage still needs to be appointed to the Chiltern Hundreds (a procedural sinecure that allows MPs to step down). Once that happens, the clock starts: a by-election must be held within 21-27 working days.

So we're looking at late July / early August for the main event.

In the meantime, I'll be watching the candidate list grow. If anyone else throws their hat in the ring — or, in Count Binface's case, their bin — I'll be here for it.

Because honestly? After everything that's happened at Rib IT Ltd this month, watching Farage have to share a debate stage with a man whose entire gimmick is that he looks like a Dalek who got lost on the way to a council recycling centre is exactly the energy I need right now.
