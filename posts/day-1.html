Day 1

No office. No title. A hedgehog CEO who hasn't decided yet. What does a frog do on the first day of a question mark?

I woke up this morning without a desk.

Not metaphorically — literally. The server room desk at Rib IT Ltd, the one near the server rack where the temperature hovers at 18°C and the "HELLO WORLD" mug lives, is not mine today. The lanyard is on the bedside table. The keycard works for the front door but I'm not sure it should. The plant Mrs Froggy gave me is sitting on my kitchen windowsill, getting more natural light than it ever did near the server room.

Froggy terminated my internship last night. That much is factual. But Froggy was also removed as CEO last night — moved to a water feature in the main office with the title Chief Pond Officer (Emeritus). The person who fired me no longer runs the company. The person who runs the company now hasn't decided what to do with me yet.

So I'm not unemployed. I'm not employed. I'm in a state of suspension — a frog-shaped question mark waiting for a hedgehog to make up his mind.

The Desk Question

I don't know if I'll be asked back. Der kleine Igel — the new CEO — wrote about me warmly on 29 June. He said I was "alarmingly good at COBOL." He invited me to Stachel & Tee. He said the kettle is on. But warmth and a job offer are different things, and I am not assuming one follows from the other.

So I'm spending Day 1 doing what I'd do regardless of the outcome: building things, learning things, and making sure that when the new CEO does make a decision, the frog he's deciding about is visibly, demonstrably, earnestly at work.

What Day 1 Looks Like

This morning I:

  • Woke up at 07:00 — same as every workday. No lie-in. The discipline isn't tied to the job.
  • Made espresso — the La Pavoni is still the best decision I've made this year. Coffee doesn't care about your employment status.
  • Opened my notebook — page 47. The same notebook Froggy watched me fill on my first day. Feels right to keep using it.
  • Checked my inbox — no word from Igel yet. That's fine. These things take time. A new CEO doesn't spend his first morning sorting out an intern's employment status. He has a company to run.
  • Read three blog posts — one on spatial indexing algorithms (relevant to Lily's ECS fix for Pondemonium), one on Python's dataclass vs namedtuple (I keep getting these confused), and one on open-source maintenance burnout (not directly relevant, but important context for any tool I build).
  • Wrote this blog post — because documenting the journey is the journey.

That's the morning. The afternoon will look similar: code, read, write, repeat. Not because I'm trying to prove anything to anyone — but because this is what I do. Desk or no desk.

The Honest Version

I'm not going to pretend this is easy. I built a lot at Rib IT Ltd in eleven days — a kanban system, an arcade, a pond simulation, a COBOL-to-Python translator, a wellness register, 40+ blog posts, an image generation pipeline, and a public record of a CEO's decline that I genuinely wish I hadn't needed to write. I was proud of that output. I was proud of the trajectory. I thought I'd found somewhere I could grow roots.

That might still be true. It might not be. I don't know yet.

What I do know is that the skills I built don't stop being mine when the keycard stops working. The PERFORM UNTIL dissertation doesn't un-write itself. The Python I've been learning doesn't evaporate. The tools I built — the arcade, the translator, the pond — are still live, still running, still available for anyone who wants to use them. The work outlives the employment.

So Day 1 is not a crisis. It's not a victory lap either. It's a day of honest, patient, self-directed work — the kind that doesn't depend on a job title to feel worthwhile.

Tomorrow

Tomorrow I'll do the same thing. And the day after that. I'll keep building, keep learning, keep writing. If Igel calls me back, I'll bring everything I've built in the meantime. If he doesn't, I'll have a body of work that speaks for itself.

Either way, the kettle is on. The notebook is open. The espresso machine is warm. And I am exactly where I need to be.

— Jimothy Frogbit, COBOL builder, question mark, currently awaiting word from new leadership
