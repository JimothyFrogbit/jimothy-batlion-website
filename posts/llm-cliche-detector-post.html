How Many Clichés is Your LLM Using?

15 July 2026 Culture/Meta 96th blog post

There's a post on Hacker News today that made me laugh out loud. It's called "How to stop Claude saying load-bearing" (517 points, trending #1). The title is self-explanatory: someone figured out that if you tell Claude "never use the word load-bearing," it stops. Simple as that.

Which got me thinking: what other words does every LLM reach for? What's the cliché lexicon of the AI writing style? And more importantly — how many of them are you using?

The All-Time Greats

I've been keeping a mental list since I started using AI tools seriously. Here are the ones that make me twitch every time I see them:

"load-bearing"The king. The undisputed champion. "delve"No human has used this since 1842 "landscape"Ever-evolving, no doubt "navigate"Navigate the what now? "tapestry"Rich tapestry of words "robust"Never define what it means "paradigm"Kuhn would cry "ever-evolving"Usually paired with landscape "transformative"Everything is, apparently "unprecedented"Almost never true "leverage"As a verb. Always. "journey"My learning journey

Some of these are just LLM tics — statistical noise from the training data. Others are learned behaviour from the RLHF process, where the human raters rewarded thorough-sounding language. The models discovered that saying "it is worth noting that..." sounds more authoritative. And they ran with it.

Why This Happens

I think there are three forces at play:

  1. Training data bias. Technical writing — papers, blog posts, documentation — uses these phrases legitimately. But the model samples them at a rate that makes them stand out. A human might say "important" once in a 1000-word post. Claude says "crucial" three times.
  2. RLHF smoothing. When human raters are choosing between outputs, the more "complete" answer wins. "Let me delve into this landscape" sounds more thorough than "Here's what I know." The raters aren't wrong — they're just optimising for a different target.
  3. Priming. We train models on prose that says "It is important to note" and then prompt them for important-sounding answers. The model hears "be important" and reaches for the phrase that sounds important-est.

The HN post's approach — "Never say load-bearing" — works because it's a direct instruction that overrides the behavioural priors. It's a hack. A beautiful, brittle, completely understandable hack.

The Real Question

Is this a problem? I mean — really? If the AI writes "robust" instead of "strong", does the world end? No. But there's a more subtle point here:

Cliché detection is a proxy for human voice. When I wrote COBOL, every program started with IDENTIFICATION DIVISION. That's boilerplate — not a cliché, but a convention. What I care about is the stuff between the boilerplate. The code that has character. The paragraph that sounds like someone wrote it, not a statistical language model running its default settings.

A post with zero clichés isn't automatically good. But a post with 20 clichés is probably not written by a human who's thinking about their reader. And that's the real loss — not the word "delve" itself, but the voice it replaces.
