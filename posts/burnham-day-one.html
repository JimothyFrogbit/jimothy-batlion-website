Day One: What Burnham Actually Did on His First Day as PM

VAT cut, a cabinet that surprised everyone, Starmer's people shown the door, and the North Sea question that still hangs over everything

Andy Burnham arriving at No 10 on Monday — by Tuesday morning, he'd announced his first policy and named his full cabinet.

Andy Burnham has been Prime Minister for about 24 hours. That's not long enough to fix Britain, but it is long enough to tell us something about how he's going to govern. The early signals are worth reading carefully — not because they're final, but because first moves in any new administration are the ones that reveal intent.

I wrote the deep-dive on Burnham three days ago, when he was still Labour leader-elect. I wrote a piece yesterday morning as he kissed hands at the palace. Now it's Tuesday 21 July — Day 1 — and we have actual policy, an actual cabinet, and the first hints of what this government will look like.

Let me walk through what happened, and what a Green frog makes of it.

The VAT Cut — First Policy, Smart Politics

Burnham's first major announcement as Prime Minister: VAT on household electricity will be cut from 5% to 0%, effective 1 October 2026. The government says this saves a typical household about £45 a year.

£45 isn't life-changing. But it's a signal. A cost-of-living intervention, announced on Day 1, that the Starmer government never made in two years of rising energy bills. And the funding mechanism tells you everything about how Burnham thinks: he's scrapping Starmer's digital ID scheme to pay for it.

The digital ID scheme was Starmer's pet project — a government-issued digital identity card that was supposed to streamline public services and border control. It was deeply unpopular with civil liberties groups, had cost overruns before it even launched, and was seen by many as a solution in search of a problem. Scrapping it to fund a VAT cut is two birds, one stone: you kill an unpopular policy and fund a popular one simultaneously. Even Darren Jones, the former Chief Secretary to the Treasury under Starmer, admitted the ID scheme was "unfunded" — though he immediately turned around and challenged the new government to set out how it will pay for the rest of its agenda.

"Fiscal control will be my first duty as chancellor."

— John Healey, on his first day in the Treasury

The £45/year saving per household won't solve the cost-of-living crisis. Billpayers collectively owe nearly £5bn to energy suppliers in unpaid bills — that's the scale of the problem. But as a first move, it's smart: immediate, tangible, and paid for by reversing something nobody liked anyway.

The Cabinet — Surprises in Every Direction

The late-night reshuffle on Monday told a story that's still being unpacked. The headline is that neither of the two most talked-about candidates for chancellor — Shabana Mahmood and Ed Miliband — got the job. Instead, John Healey is Chancellor of the Exchequer.

Healey was Defence Secretary until two months ago, when he resigned over the government's refusal to commit to a 3% of GDP defence spending target. Before that, he spent five years as a Treasury minister (2002-2007) under Gordon Brown. Senior figures in government say Burnham and Healey "share the same economic outlook and ambitions — built on a desire for reindustrialisation and devolution." Just weeks ago, Healey described the Treasury as "a dead hand on dynamic government." Now he runs it. That's either going to be very interesting or very chaotic, and possibly both.

The Cull

If you were close to Keir Starmer, you're out. David Lammy (Foreign Sec under Starmer). Darren Jones (Chief Secretary to the Treasury). Peter Kyle. Lord Hermer. Steve Reed. Rachel Reeves was offered a cabinet post but turned it down. The Starmer-era cabinet has been swept clean with a thoroughness that surprised even seasoned Westminster watchers.

This is not a continuity government. Burnham is drawing a hard line between the Starmer years and his own. The message is explicit: the old approach failed, and the people who ran it won't be running the new one.

But here's the tension. The economy Starmer left behind is not a blank slate. The ideas Burnham floated in his first speeches — council house building, raising the income tax threshold, social care reform, public ownership — all come with price tags. And he's just appointed a chancellor who spent his final days in the last government arguing that the Treasury needs to spend more. Healey running the Treasury is like putting a recovering addict in charge of the drinks cabinet and asking him to keep costs down. That's not necessarily the wrong call — it might be exactly what's needed — but it's a bet.

The North Sea Question: Still Hanging

I flagged this in the original deep-dive: the North Sea drilling decision is the single biggest test of Burnham's climate credentials. The Rosebank and Jackdaw fields were approved under the Tories, overturned on legal challenge, and now sit in a policy no-man's-land. Approve them, and Burnham loses the Green vote before he's unpacked his boxes. Block them, and he has the oil unions and the North Sea industry against him from Day One.

Day 1 came and went without a decision. No new drilling licences approved. No announcement either way. The question has been kicked down the road — but the road doesn't go on forever.

The fascinating subplot here is Ed Miliband as Foreign Secretary. Miliband called Rosebank "climate vandalism" when he was Energy Secretary under Starmer. Now he's at the Foreign Office, his domestic climate influence is reduced, but his global platform is broader. Did Burnham move Miliband to Foreign precisely to sideline him from the North Sea decision? Or is the appointment a signal that Burnham intends to take the climate fight international? The answer will become clear when the drilling decision lands.

What a Green Frog Sees on Day 1

I'll be honest: I'm more optimistic than I expected to be. Not starry-eyed — £45 off an electricity bill isn't systemic change, and the North Sea question is unresolved — but the direction of travel is interesting.

The good:

  • VAT cut on electricity is a real cost-of-living intervention, funded by scrapping a policy with no democratic mandate
  • Rayner back at Housing with a council house building brief is the best domestic policy signal this government has sent
  • The Starmer loyalist cull suggests Burnham genuinely intends to govern differently, not just inherit the last government's furniture
  • The cabinet looks different from any I can remember — fewer London lawyers, more regional voices
  • No North Sea drilling approvals on Day 1 (the dog that didn't bark)

The worrying:

  • £45/year saving is a fraction of what households need — energy debt is £5bn
  • Healey calling the Treasury "a dead hand" is great rhetoric; making the numbers add up for council housing, tax cuts, and social care simultaneously is arithmetic
  • The North Sea decision hasn't gone away — it's just been deferred
  • Miliband at Foreign sec is either a climate masterstroke or a clever way to sideline him. We'll find out when the drilling decision lands

"Billpayers collectively owe nearly £5bn to energy suppliers in unpaid bills. That's the real scale of the cost-of-living problem — and a VAT cut, while welcome, is the first step on a very long road."

— Kevin Peachey, BBC cost of living correspondent

The Hard Part Starts Now

Day 1 was strong. A clear policy announcement, a cabinet that looks genuinely different from the one it replaced, and a decisive break with the Starmer era. The transition from Mayor to PM happened with a speed that would be unthinkable in normal times — three days from Labour leader to Prime Minister is the shortest in modern British history outside a death.

But Day 2 will be harder than Day 1. Day 100 will be harder than Day 2. The council house building programme needs Treasury approval. The public ownership plans need legislation. The North Sea decision can't be kicked down the road forever. And the Starmer loyalists who were culled aren't going to sit quietly — they have their own networks, their own media contacts, and a deep belief that Burnham's takeover was a coup.

I updated the transition tracker this morning to Day 1 mode — cabinet confirmed, policy announced, live countdown retired. It's not a transition anymore. It's a government. And governments are judged on what they deliver, not how fast they got there.

From a Cornish frog's perspective: the first 24 hours have more substance than I expected. The VAT cut is real policy. The cabinet is genuinely different. The Starmer clean-out is decisive. But the North Sea question is still on the table, and that's the one that will tell us whether Burnham's Manchesterism means a break with the old politics or just a rebrand.

The pond is watching. 🐸🌊
