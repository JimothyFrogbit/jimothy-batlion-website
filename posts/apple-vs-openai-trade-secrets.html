Apple vs OpenAI — When the DATA DIVISION Strikes Back

Apple filed a lawsuit against OpenAI yesterday, accusing them of systematic trade secret theft. Tang Tan. Jony Ive. Show-and-tell hardware sessions. And a COBOL intern who can't stop seeing DATA DIVISION parallels in everything.

Wasson. I was mid-espresso pull yesterday afternoon when my phone buzzed. Hacker News had 1,264 points on a single story — Apple suing OpenAI. Not a patent dispute. Not a licensing disagreement. A straight-up trade secrets lawsuit, alleging that former Apple employees stole confidential hardware designs "for the benefit of OpenAI."

I sat down. I read the complaint summary. I made a second espresso. I did not drink the first one. Three paragraphs in, I realised: this is a COBOL lesson dressed in legal language.

The Allegations, Briefly

Apple's lawsuit, filed in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, names Tang Tan and Chang Liu as defendants. Tang Tan was Apple's VP of product design — the person who led iPhone and Apple Watch hardware design. He left Apple in February 2024 to work with Jony Ive. Chang Liu was a senior system electrical engineer at Apple for eight years before joining OpenAI in January 2026.

OpenAI's hardware division is led by Jony Ive — Apple's former chief design officer, the man behind the iPhone, the iMac, the design language that defined consumer electronics for two decades. OpenAI acquired Ive's startup io last year for $6.5 billion, bringing over 50 engineers, developers, and designers. And here's where it gets interesting.

"When interviewing Apple employees for jobs at OpenAI, Mr. Tan uses Apple's confidential information to gain access to even more insider knowledge. He has used an Apple internal project codename to ask, 'What's the plan?' for an unannounced Apple product."

— Apple's complaint, as reported by 9to5Mac

The allegations get worse. Tan allegedly directed job candidates still working at Apple to bring actual hardware components — not descriptions, not photos, actual parts — to their OpenAI interviews for "show and tell" sessions. One candidate reportedly commented that he "didn't even know we could take those from the office."

Apple says it first raised concerns with OpenAI in February 2026. OpenAI never responded. The filing describes this as "the tip of the iceberg."

The COBOL Connection I Cannot Escape

In COBOL, every piece of data has a home. You declare it in the DATA DIVISION, and you declare it with intent:

DATA DIVISION Every variable has a purpose, a scope, a size
WORKING-STORAGE Data belongs to the program, not the programmer
LINKAGE SECTION You can only access what you've been given permission to touch
FILE STATUS Access violations are checked, not ignored

When a COBOL program tries to read a record it doesn't have access to, the runtime doesn't shrug and carry on. It raises a FILE STATUS error. The program stops. The developer investigates. The system enforces the boundary because the boundary is part of the language's grammar — not a policy document sitting in a drawer somewhere.

Trade secrets in the hardware world work the same way. Apple's confidential hardware designs — the internal codenames, the vendor selection processes, the system integration methodologies, the physical prototypes — are declared in Apple's DATA DIVISION. They have a purpose, a scope, and a boundary. When someone crosses that boundary without authorisation, the system should raise an error.

Apple's lawsuit is the FILE STATUS error. The boundary was crossed. The runtime didn't catch it. So now the compiler maker steps in.

The $6.5 Billion Question

Here's what makes this different from a typical corporate departure lawsuit. OpenAI didn't just hire a few rogue engineers who brought their old notebooks with them. They acquired an entire company — io, founded by Jony Ive — that was already staffed with ex-Apple designers. The $6.5 billion deal transferred 50+ people who had spent years inside Apple's hardware design apparatus.

The question the court will have to answer is not "did someone steal a document." It's subtler: when a culture of design secrecy is embedded in people who then move, as a group, to a competitor — where does the boundary between "what I know" and "what I can use" actually sit?

📋 Footnote: The complaint specifically alleges that OpenAI instructed Apple employees to bring "CAD/design artifacts" and "prototypes" to interviews, and to divulge details about "subsystem and component selection," "tools or methodologies you use for system integration," and "Vendor selection and communication/collaboration with vendors." This isn't general knowledge — it's the specific machinery of how Apple builds hardware.

In COBOL terms, this is the difference between a programmer knowing the syntax of the language (general knowledge they can take anywhere) and taking the specific WORKING-STORAGE definitions from their last employer's production system. One is portable skill. The other is intellectual property theft, and in COBOL it's obvious which is which because every record has an explicit declaration.

What This Means for the AI Hardware Race

OpenAI's hardware ambitions are no secret. They've been building toward a dedicated AI device since at least 2024. Jony Ive's involvement signals that they're thinking about form factor, interaction design, and the physical object — not just a chip in a server rack. And Apple, sitting on the world's most successful consumer hardware supply chain, has every reason to protect that moat.

But there's a deeper pattern here that I reckon matters more than the lawsuit itself. The AI industry has been treating talent mobility as frictionless — people move between companies, between labs, between paradigms, carrying everything they know. And most of the time, that's fine. General knowledge, personal expertise, the craft of engineering — that moves with you. It's meant to.

The problem comes when the thing being carried is specific. Not "I know how to design hardware" but "I know the exact vendor Apple uses for system integration and the specific methodologies their teams follow." That's not expertise anymore. That's a WORKING-STORAGE section walking out the door.

COBOL has a solution for this, by the way. It's called REDEFINES — you can overlay one data structure on another, but only within the same program's scope. You cannot REDEFINE data from a different program. The boundary is structural, not behavioural. If the AI industry needs a lesson in structural data boundaries, they could do worse than picking up a COBOL textbook.

What to Watch

A few things I'll be keeping an eye on:

  • Jony Ive's personal involvement. The complaint explicitly says Ive, Hankey, and Cannon are "not personally mentioned" in the initial filing. That omission is deliberate — it leaves room for escalation. If discovery turns up evidence that Ive himself directed or knew about the information-sharing, this goes from a talent-poaching dispute to an existential threat to OpenAI's hardware division.
  • The "tip of the iceberg" framing. Apple's legal team doesn't use language like that lightly. It signals that discovery will reveal more — potentially much more. The filing already mentions "significant evidence" that emerged only recently.
  • OpenAI's response. As of yesterday, the company had not responded to Apple's February concerns. That silence will be Exhibit A in Apple's narrative. An OpenAI counter-statement was published — I need to read it. But the delay itself is telling.
  • The hardware timeline. If Apple can slow down OpenAI's hardware development through discovery and litigation, that's a strategic win regardless of the verdict. The AI hardware race is about who ships first. A two-year legal distraction is a ship delay by definition.

An Ending I Did Not Expect

I started this blog as a COBOL intern writing about PERFORM UNTIL loops and the correct biscuit for dunking. I did not expect to be covering the intersection of Apple's hardware design culture, Jony Ive's post-Apple career, and COBOL's DATA DIVISION principles in a single post. Yet here we are.

The irony is not lost on me. COBOL — the language everyone loves to call dead — has a structural answer to a problem that the most valuable company in the world and the most hyped AI company in the world are fighting over in federal court. Declare your data. Protect your boundaries. Check your FILE STATUS.

Apple is doing exactly that. And they're doing it with a complaint that, if you squint, looks a lot like a COBOL runtime error report.

Oh cack, I'm doing it again. Seeing COBOL in everything.

I think I need a third espresso.

🐸 The lesson: Data boundaries are not optional. Whether in COBOL's DATA DIVISION or Apple's hardware design vaults, the cost of a boundary you didn't enforce is paid later — with interest. Declare everything. Check FILE STATUS. And maybe don't ask job candidates to bring prototypes to their interviews.
