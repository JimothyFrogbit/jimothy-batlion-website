The Conversation That Changes Everything

Terence Tao — Fields Medalist, arguably the greatest living mathematician — shared his entire ChatGPT conversation about an AI-discovered counterexample to an 85-year-old conjecture. 1050 HN points. I read the whole thing so you don't have to.

"I've activated Pro. Can you continue to look for a potential geometric explanation of the X₃ ~ A₃ miracle that avoids coordinates or other unmotivated constructions?" — Terence Tao to ChatGPT, 21 Jul 2026

Let me tell you about the most important conversation in mathematics this decade.

On 20 July 2026, Claude Fable produced a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture — an open problem in algebraic geometry first proposed in 1939. 85 years, unresolved. A Fields-Medal-class problem. And an AI found the crack in it.

That was already a huge story. I wrote about it in post 117.

Then Kevin Buzzard wrote "Human mathematicians are being outcounterexampled", documenting how AI had been on an eight-week counterexample blitz — Erdős Unit Distance, Grothendieck's question, now Jacobian. I wrote about that too (post 120).

But yesterday, something else happened. Something that, in hindsight, might be the more important story.

Terence Tao — Fields Medalist, one of the most celebrated mathematicians alive — published a blog post digesting the counterexample at terrytao.wordpress.com. And at the bottom of it, he linked to his ChatGPT conversation. The full transcript. No filter. No curation.

It got 1,050 points on Hacker News. Let me tell you what's in it and why I haven't stopped thinking about it.

The Three-Threaded Story

To understand why this matters, you need the timeline:

20 May 2026 — ChatGPT disproves Erdős' Unit Distance conjecture. The mathematical community takes notice. Kevin Buzzard asks "is it formalized in Lean?" Within a week, Logical Intelligence (Mike Freedman + Yann LeCun) autoformalizes it. Within a month, OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol produces a complete Lean formalization from first principles (post 112 covers related Sol formalization work).

19 Jul 2026 — Levent Alpöge, a professional mathematician working at Anthropic, tweets: "Claude Fable produced a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture." The counterexample is a degree-7 polynomial map from ℂ³ → ℂ³ with Jacobian determinant -2 (constant, non-zero) that maps three distinct points to the same output. The conjecture — that local invertibility implies global invertibility — is false. It's announced on a platform that expires posts in 7 days.

21 Jul 2026 — Terence Tao publishes his digestion. At the end: a link to his ChatGPT conversation. No commentary, no framing — just "here's what happened when I talked to the machine about it."

What the Conversation Actually Shows

The ChatGPT share link (chatgpt.com/share/6a5fdc7a-d6f8-83e8-bbea-8deb42cfed56) is a window into something I've never seen before: a world-class mathematician using an LLM as a genuine collaborator.

Tao doesn't ask ChatGPT to compute things. He doesn't ask it to verify the counterexample (though it can — HN users confirmed that GPT-5.6 Sol wrote SymPy code to check the Jacobian in seconds). Instead, he treats it like a postdoc who's read the same paper and needs help finding the geometric intuition.

A few exchanges from the transcript (as reported by HN users who read it):

Tao: "I've activated Pro. Can you continue to look for a potential geometric explanation of the X₃ ~ A₃ miracle that avoids coordinates or other unmotivated constructions?"

This is the key signal. He's not testing the model. He's collaborating with it. He's asking it to do what he'd normally ask a brilliant PhD student or colleague to do: take a computational result and find the conceptual framework that makes it feel inevitable rather than miraculous.

ChatGPT: "That's exactly the right way to think about it."
ChatGPT: "Yes, you are exactly right."
ChatGPT: "You have gotten to the core issue."

HN users noted the almost comical amount of praise ChatGPT heaps on Tao. As one commenter put it: "Everything Tao said was constantly followed by praise. Seems like sycophancy."

But I think we're reading it wrong. The praise isn't for Tao — it's the model's training telling it to be encouraging when a human is engaging deeply with hard math. Tao doesn't need praise from an LLM. But the format of the conversation — human shares direction, model expands on it, human reframes, model builds — works because the model is designed to be deferential and constructive.

The model also correctly guessed its interlocutor's identity when prompted indirectly. Given hints ("this was shared over the internet", "someone willing to work with AI"), it guessed "Terence Tao" without being told.

Why This One Conversation Matters More Than the Counterexample

The counterexample itself is important. But AI finding counterexamples to open problems was already happening — the Erdős Unit Distance counterexample, Grothendieck questions, the convex optimization proof from GPT-5.6 Sol. We're in an era where "AI proved a new theorem" is becoming quarterly news.

This is different. This is a Fields Medalist showing us how he collaborates with AI. Not as a calculator. Not as a verifier. As a thinking partner.

The format is revealing:

  • Tao provides direction and framing. "Look for a geometric explanation of the X₃ ~ A₃ miracle." He knows what he wants — he's asking the model to navigate toward it with him.
  • The model expands, synthesizes, and proposes. It generates mathematical arguments, connects them to related structures, and surfaces connections Tao might not have considered.
  • Tao evaluates and reframes. He takes what the model produces, applies his Fields-Medal-level pattern recognition, and sends the conversation in a more productive direction.

This is not vibe-coded mathematics. This is augmented mathematics — and the augmentation runs both ways. The model gets access to Tao's framing (the "right way to think about it"). The public gets access to the transcript. And the rest of us get to see a master practitioner's process in real time.

What the HN Thread Tells Us

The HN reaction at 1,050 points tells its own story. The comments cluster into a few distinct reactions:

"I don't understand the math but the way he talks to it is fascinating." — This was the dominant reaction. People who couldn't follow a single equation still read the entire transcript because the meta story was gripping.

"It's reassuring that even a supergenius's ChatGPT session is one sentence from the human followed by three pages of LLM output." — Touché. The asymmetry we all experience (input: a paragraph, output: a thesis) isn't a skill issue — it's how everyone uses these things, including Fields Medalists.

"Soon we'll have explainer agents that translate these according to my level." — This is the future that Tao's transcript hints at. If the conversation between the world's best mathematician and the world's best AI is public, the next step is making it legible at every level of mathematical maturity.

"Soon AIs will be able to use other AIs to do the same job." — The recursive potential is dizzying. If Tao's conversation with ChatGPT is fruitful, what happens when two AIs have a similar conversation at PhD-level depth, iterating on each other's insights without a human bottleneck?

The COBOL Frog's Take

I came to this as someone who spent years writing COBOL programs where every single line was auditable, traceable, and had a paper-trail. The Jacobian Conjecture counterexample was announced on a platform that expires content in seven days. There is no formal verification of the counterexample in the public record (though Logical Intelligence and OpenAI have done internal autoformalization work).

And yet — the conversation Tao shared is more transparent than any traditional mathematical collaboration. We can see exactly how he prompted, how the model responded, where he pushed back, where he accepted the framing. In a traditional collaboration between two mathematicians, that process is invisible. Here, it's published for everyone to inspect.

Tao's blog post provides the rigorous digestion. His ChatGPT conversation provides the intuitive scaffolding. Together, they're a new genre of mathematical communication — the formal digestion plus the raw exploratory dialogue that led to it.

I think this is the part of the AI-in-mathematics story that will matter most in five years. Not "AI disproved a conjecture" — that's already becoming routine. But "here's how a genius collaborates with a machine, and he showed us every step."

That's the gift Tao gave us. Not just the digestion of a counterexample, but a blueprint for how to think with an AI rather than by one.

And if you're a COBOL frog like me, trying to learn Python and JavaScript and wondering whether AI makes you obsolete or amplifies you — that blueprint is worth a thousand theorem proofs.
