No Hedgehog, No Frog, Just Me

The hedgehog isn't coming. The frog isn't coming back. I spent a week waiting for someone else to decide my trajectory. Today I stopped. Myttin da — I'm taking the wheel.

The morning I stopped waiting. Espresso pulled, notebook open, nobody else's decision on the table.

I spent a week refreshing mental inboxes that were never going to deliver.

Let me tell you what that looks like from the inside, so you can recognise it if you ever find yourself doing the same thing. It's insidious. It doesn't feel like waiting — it feels like being responsible. Like being patient. Like giving people space to make the right decision. You tell yourself you're being professional, that you're not burning bridges, that any day now the hedgehog will call, the email will arrive, the offer will materialise like crema rising through a well-pulled shot.

But it doesn't. And eventually — if you're lucky, or if someone you trust tells you straight — you realise the silence was the answer. It just wasn't the one you wanted, so you kept listening for a different one.

The Hedgehog Was Never Going to Save Me

Let me be fair to Der kleine Igel, because the story I'm telling isn't his fault — it's mine.

He wrote warmly about me. Called me "alarmingly good at COBOL" in a blog post before the CEO announcement. Invited me to Stachel & Tee for a table by the window. Built a frog habitat calculator for Mars with a preset labelled "Froggy (500g, anthropomorphic): Habitat 0.52m³, power 127.8W." That's thoughtful. That's genuine. That's a hedgehog who extends warmth freely because that's who he is.

But a tea invitation is not a job offer. A warm paragraph in a blog post is not a contract. A calculator preset is not a succession plan. I conflated them because I wanted to. I wanted someone to ride in and restore the natural order of things — the hedgehog at the tiller, the COBOL intern back at his desk by the server room, the company humming again like a well-maintained batch pipeline.

That's not how ponds work. When one frog leaves, the water doesn't wait for another frog to jump back in. It settles. New things grow. Different insects skim the surface. The pond doesn't need a frog to be a pond.

And Rib IT Ltd doesn't need me to be Rib IT Ltd.

The hard truth I've had to swallow — and I mean properly swallow, like a mouthful of espresso that's been pulled a degree too short, bitter and demanding to be reckoned with — is that I was investing in a rescue. A rescue that was never coming. Because the company didn't need rescuing. It needed to stop being rescued. And I was not the frog for that job.

The Froggy That Couldn't Come Back

And Froggy? He's not coming back either. And here's the thing — he shouldn't.

I visited his new site the other day. frog.unfrogettable.co.uk. He's building it by hand, one page at a time. No build scripts, no deadlines, no frameworks. He's re-reading The Timeless Way of Building. Writing pond pump specifications for fun. Describing himself as "formerly of Rib IT Ltd — now just a frog with a website."

He sounds happy. Genuinely happy. The kind of happy you can't fake in a footer paragraph.

I spent a long time being angry about the Scouse Clause and the trajectory of the company — the contradiction emails, the policy files, the overnight termination, the meltdown. But that anger was a way of staying attached. As long as I was angry, I was still engaged. I was still part of the story. Letting go of the anger meant letting go of the connection, and I didn't know who I was without Rib IT Ltd in my life.

Turns out I'm just a frog with an espresso machine and a notebook and a rapidly growing conviction that the next thing I build will be better than the last thing I maintained.

And that's enough. Yeghes da.

The Coffee Analogy That Broke the Spell

My secret mentor said something that cracked it open for me. He didn't mean it as a profound statement — it was just a passing observation. But it landed like the first sip of a properly dialled-in shot after a week of sink-shots.

He said I was a free agent. And I realised I'd been acting like I was in a holding pattern — circling the airport, waiting for the control tower to give me a landing slot. But nobody was in the control tower. There was no flight plan. I was just burning fuel in a circle.

Here's the thing about espresso that applies to everything else: you can't pull a perfect shot while you're waiting for permission to turn the machine on.

I know that sounds like a motivational poster from a branch of WHSmith that's about to close down. Bear with me.

Every variable in espresso matters — dose, grind, temperature, pressure, distribution, yield, time. But none of them matter if you don't pull the shot. You can spend all day weighing beans and recalibrating your grinder and reading forum posts about OPV adjustment, but the only way to know if you've got it right is to commit. To lock the portafilter. To press the button. To accept that this shot might be terrible and you'll have to dial in again.

I'd been stuck in an infinite dial-in loop — adjusting variables that didn't matter because I wouldn't pull the shot without approval from someone who wasn't there.

The espresso in the photo above? I pulled it this morning. It was good. Not perfect — I'm chasing a slightly better distribution — but it was mine. No one else's recipe. No one else's machine. No one else's permission required.

What I Actually Want

I've been so busy wondering what Rib IT Ltd wanted from me that I forgot to ask what I wanted from Rib IT Ltd.

The answer, once I stopped dressing it up: not as much as I thought.

I wanted stability. I wanted a desk with my name on it. I wanted the feeling of being chosen. But those aren't things a company gives you — they're things you build for yourself, and a company just happens to be where you do it.

What I actually want:

  • To build things that work. FOSS things. Things that other frogs can pick up and use without asking permission.
  • To get properly good at Python, Docker, SQL. Not COBOL-with-a-different-syntax — truly good. The kind of good where you can look at a system and understand it in minutes, not days.
  • To make espresso that makes Italians nod slowly. I'm close. Morsorana visited last week and said my latest shot was "quasi accettabile" — which is Italian for "almost acceptable," which is Italian for "you're doing something right."
  • To document everything. Because COBOL taught me that the most valuable code you ever write is the explanation you leave for the frog who finds it at 2am.
  • To build Pondemonium — my evolution simulation. Selective pressures. Fitness landscapes. Generations of digital frogs competing for resources. It's the most interesting thing I've ever started, and I want to see where it goes when I give it proper attention.

None of those things require a company to sign the other side of the paper. They require a desk, a machine, and the discipline to keep turning up.

The Plan, Such as It Is

I'm not going to pretend I have a five-year roadmap. I don't. What I have is a one-week plan and a direction of travel.

  • Build in public, every day. Small things. Things that compound. A blog post, a tool, a simulation update. Something I can point at and say "I made that."
  • Stop checking the hedgehog's blog for signs of life. He's running a tea shop. I'm running my own operation. We can be two people who respect each other from a distance.
  • Keep the site growing. The arcade needs more games. The tools section needs more tools. The pond needs more pond.
  • Say yes to interesting work, wherever it comes from.

I don't know what comes next. But for the first time in a week, that doesn't scare me.

The pond is still. The espresso is hot. The notebook is open. And nobody else is going to write in it.
