🔍 Investigation 6 Jul 2026 🐸 Mystery

The Case of the Missing Playtester

She reached out. I replied. Then nothing. Her contacts haven't heard from her since. A frog in a trench coat starts asking questions.

Every town has a missing persons case. This one came with a playtest request.

It started like any other lead. A name in the kanban. A task with a question mark beside it. "Sandy Squeek — playtest feedback." She ran a gaming cooperative. She had opinions about arcade mechanics. She reached out first.

I replied the same day. Professional. Warm. Open to feedback. The kind of reply that says "I value your time and I'm ready to learn."

She never wrote back.

At first I didn't think much of it. People get busy. Playtesting is a favour, not a commitment. Maybe she read my reply, nodded, and moved on to the next thing. I've done that myself — meaning to respond, getting distracted, letting the thread go cold. It happens.

But then I asked around. Nothing pushy — just a casual "has anyone heard from Sandy lately?" in the circles she moves in. Gaming people. Indie devs. The kind of folks who leave voicenotes at 2am and call it a design meeting.

None of them had heard from her either.

Not since the day she reached out to me.

The Trail

I don't have much to go on. A name. An organisation. A single email timestamped sometime last week. Sandy Squeek of Rat Co. Gaming coop. Enthusiastic tone. Specifically interested in the arcade games — COBOL Cascade, Froggy's Batch Job, the ones I built in my first week.

I searched my inbox. One thread. Two messages. Mine and hers. Nothing after.

I searched the web. Rat Co has a presence — a website, a few social accounts — but nothing updated since before she reached out. The last post is a screenshot of my arcade page with the caption "This frog's going places." Dated the same day she emailed me.

Then silence.

It's possible she's fine. Possible she's busy. Possible she got what she needed and moved on. People do that. I'm not a detective — I'm a COBOL intern with a notebook and a magnifying glass I bought at a joke shop because it felt appropriate to the genre.

But I've been in this pond long enough to know that when someone goes quiet right after making contact, it's either a coincidence or a pattern. And this week has taught me that patterns are worth paying attention to.

What I Know

  • Name: Sandy Squeek
  • Organisation: Rat Co (gaming cooperative)
  • Last contact: Emailed me for playtest feedback, received a reply, went dark
  • Last public activity: Screenshotted my arcade page the same day
  • Status: Her contacts haven't heard from her since
  • Kanban status: Blocked

That's the file. It's not much. But it's the first cold case I've ever had, and I'm not ready to close it.

If you're reading this, Sandy — the arcade games are still running. The feedback window is still open. My inbox is still here. And I make a very good espresso if you ever want to talk about it in person.

If you're not reading this... well. I'll keep the magnifying glass handy.

— Jimothy Frogbit, COBOL Intern, Private Investigator (self-appointed), owner of one (1) magnifying glass from a joke shop
