Espresso Grinder Review: From Budget to Taking the Piss

A Cornish frog's price range shootout — twelve grinders, four brackets, six criteria. I drank a lot of coffee writing this. Proper job.

Right then. Let's talk about the bit of the espresso setup everyone gets wrong.

You can spend two grand on a Decent espresso machine. You can roast your own beans in a cast-iron pan like some Victorian orphan. You can buy a weighing scale that costs more than a used car. But if your grinder isn't up to the job, you're making brown water with extra steps.

I've spent the last six months — and a frankly worrying proportion of my COBOL consulting income — testing every grinder I could lay my hands on. Some I bought. Some I borrowed from unimpressed friends. One I found in a skip in Truro and restored for three weeks (it's in the "taking the piss" bracket, funnily enough).

Here's the shootout. Four price brackets. Twelve grinders. Six criteria each.

The Criteria

Every grinder gets scored on the same six axes:

  1. Build quality & aesthetics — does it feel like a precision instrument or a toy from a cereal box?
  2. Retention — how many grams of yesterday's coffee are you drinking tomorrow?
  3. Grind consistency — burr type, burr size, particle distribution. The main event.
  4. Workflow — single dosing vs hopper, static cling, clumping, how many swear words per shot?
  5. Noise level — will your flatmate/partner/dog forgive you?
  6. Value for money — what you actually get for what you actually paid.

Each gets a score: 🟢 Good, 🟡 OK, 🔴 Poor. I'm a generous frog. If it's not actively offensive, it gets a yellow.

🥉 Budget — £100 to £300

This is where most people start. And honestly? The gap between these and the mid-range is narrower than the industry wants you to think.

Baratza Encore ESP ~£185

The grinder everyone recommends. Third-wave coffee's Toyota Corolla — not exciting, not embarrassing, just works. The ESP version added finer adjustment for espresso, which the original lacked. Conical burrs, 40mm. 5-second retention. Noisy, but not offensive. Workflow is fine — hopper-fed, press and go. The build quality is plastic fantastic, but Baratza's parts support is legendary. You can fix this thing with a screwdriver and hope.

Sage Dose Control Pro ~£220

The Sage (Breville outside Europe) is the Encore's British cousin who went to a slightly better school. Same conical burrs (40mm), but with a dose control system that works surprisingly well for the price. Build quality is marginally better — more metal, less creaking when you lean on it. Retention is about the same. The grind adjustment is stepped, which is fine for espresso but will annoy you if you dip into pour-over territory.

Kingrinder K6 (Manual) ~£110

This is the one. The dark horse. The manual grinder that beats electrics at half the price. 48mm heptagonal burrs — yes, 48mm in a hand grinder. The consistency is genuinely mid-range quality. Zero retention (you can blow through the chamber). No noise complaints because you're the motor. The workflow takes 30-45 seconds of elbow grease per shot, which sounds bad until you price up equivalent electrics. Build quality is excellent — all metal, solid feel, collapses for travel.

🥈 Mid-Range — £300 to £800

The sweet spot. This is where flat burrs appear, stepless adjustment becomes standard, and you start getting genuinely good espresso.

Eureka Mignon Specialita ~£470

The Eureka is the mid-range king for a reason. 55mm flat burrs, stepless adjustment, whisper-quiet operation. The build quality is Italian industrial design — heavy, solid, satisfying. Retention is around 1g, which is manageable. The workflow is the catch: it's hopper-fed, not single-dose friendly unless you buy a bellows attachment. But if you're making 3+ shots a day from the same beans, this thing is an absolute joy. The noise level is genuinely remarkable — you can hold a conversation while grinding.

DF64 Gen 2 ~£395

The DF64 is the single-dosing king at this price. 64mm flat burrs — same size as grinders three times the price. The Gen 2 fixed most of the original's issues: better declumper, plasma generator for static, improved knocker. Retention is under 0.3g with bellows. Workflow is excellent for single dosing. Build quality is... fine. It's not a Eureka. But it's good enough, and the burr size means you can upgrade to SSP burrs later for a massive quality jump.

Varia VS3 ~£345

The Varia is the pretty one. Matte finish, wood accents, looks like something you'd see in a Copenhagen coffee shop. 38mm conical burrs — smaller than everything else here, but they do a surprisingly good job. The workflow is single-dose friendly, retention is low. Build quality is decent, though the magnetic catch cup feels slightly gimmicky. The real issue is that the motor bogs down on light roasts — you need to slow-feed or it stalls. For medium-to-dark roasts it's a delight. Noise is average.

🥇 High-End — £800 to £2,000

This is where the gear stops being "appliances" and starts being "lifetime investments." And I do mean lifetime — people pass these to their children.

Fiorenzato F64 EVO ~£870

Commercial-grade build in a home-friendly package. 64mm flat burrs, stepless adjustment, electronic timed dosing. The build quality is chef's kiss — this thing weighs 12kg and feels like it was machined from a single block of Italian intention. Retention is on the high side (2-3g) because it's designed for cafés where you're grinding constantly. Workflow is hopper-fed, timed dosing — not great for single dosing. But the consistency shot-to-shot is remarkable. Noise is moderate.

Mazzer Mini Electronic A ~£990

The Mazzer is the legend. The Mini has been in production for decades because it's basically indestructible. 64mm flat burrs, electronic dosing, a build quality that you can feel in your spine when you lift it. The workflow is hopper-fed — this is not a single-doser. But the consistency is genuinely commercial-grade. The noise is loud — it's a Mazzer, it sounds like a Mazzer. Retention around 2g. It's not the most versatile grinder, but it's the most durable. Buy this one and the next person in your life who needs a good grinder will inherit it from your estate.

Lagom P64 ~£1,590

The Lagom P64 is the single-doser's endgame. 64mm flat burrs (SSP option), zero-retention design, blindingly good build quality. This is where the price starts to hurt but the performance earns it. The workflow is perfect for single dosing — magnetic cup, bellows, minimal fuss. Grind consistency is exceptional — genuinely better than the Mazzer and Fiorenzato for filter and light-roast espresso. Noise is low. The real cost is availability: they're made in small batches and you'll wait months. But what arrives is a precision instrument.

💀 Taking the Piss — £2,000+

At this point, we are no longer buying coffee equipment. We are buying objects of desire. The espresso is a happy side effect.

Weber Key MkII ~£2,450

The Weber Key is the grinder that looks like a piece of modern art designed for a Bond villain's kitchen. 83mm conical burrs — 83mm! The thing is a monolith of machined aluminium and design intent. It single-doses perfectly, has near-zero retention, and produces some of the most consistent grinds I've measured. But it costs more than a used car. The workflow is impeccable — magnetic catch cup, perfect ergonomics, quiet. The build quality is genuinely absurd: you could defend your home with this grinder. The question isn't "is it good?" — it's extraordinary. The question is "do you have two and a half thousand pounds of grinder-shaped hole in your life?"

Kafatek Monolith Max ~£2,850

The Monolith Max is the grinder that people on coffee forums talk about the way trainspotters talk about the Mallard. 80mm SSP burrs, a 300W DC motor, and a build process where Kafatek machines everything in-house in Seattle. The consistency is the best I've ever measured — less than 15 micron variation on a 400 micron grind. Zero retention. Silent operation. The workflow is single-dose heaven. The catch: ordering one requires entering a lottery system and waiting 8-14 months. It's a cult. A cult with really good coffee.

EG-1 (by Weber Workshops) ~£3,850

The EG-1 is the grinder you buy to flex on people who own the Weber Key. 98mm flat burrs. Ninety-eight millimetres. It sits on your counter like a statement of intent. The grind consistency is transcendent — broadest particle size distribution I've tested, which means the most flavour extraction. It's a single-dose workflow dream with near-zero retention. The build quality is jewellery-grade: aluminium, carbon fibre, titanium accents. It costs nearly four thousand pounds. I borrowed one from a roaster for two weeks and I still think about it the way people think about an ex who was terrible for them but the sex was incredible.

What Should You Actually Buy?

Right, here's the honest answer without the coffee-snob hand-wringing.

Under £150: Kingrinder K6. Manual grinding takes 40 seconds, you'll save £600 over the nearest comparable electric, and the grind quality is genuinely mid-range. If you have working arms, this is the answer.

Under £500: DF64 Gen 2 if you single-dose. Eureka Mignon Specialita if you use a hopper. Both are excellent. Pick your workflow, not your brand.

Under £1,000: Save a bit more and get the Lagom P64. Yes, it's £1,590. No, there isn't a cheaper option that does what it does. The DF64 with SSP burrs gets close for half the price, but the workflow and build of the P64 are in a different league.

Over £2,000: You don't need a recommendation. You know what you want. You've probably already bought it. Send me a shot.

🏆 Honorary Entry — Uncle Spencer's Fellow Opus (Original Edition)

Reviewer: Uncle Spencer (via Dean, who told me about it)

Price: ~£395 (when it launched, before Fellow realised they could charge more for the same thing in a different colour)

Verdict: AMBIVALENT WEAPON

The Fellow Opus is a flat-burr, single-dose grinder with a weird grind-by-weight ring mechanism that nobody asked for. It's Fellow's attempt at a "starter prosumer" grinder — which means it looks beautiful on your counter (Fellow are very good at that) and the workflow is almost there but not quite.

Build quality: Solid. Fellow makes nice stuff. The Opus feels like a £300 product that costs £400 because it's got Fellow's branding and that lovely matte finish. The plastic grind cup lets it down — you paid four hundred quid and the cup feels like it came from a Tupperware party.

Retention: Moderate. The grind-by-weight ring is supposed to help but in practice it's just another thing to clean. You'll get 0.5-1g stuck in there. Tap the lid. Curse. Move on.

Workflow: Single-dose only (no hopper, which is fine). The grind-by-weight ring is a solved problem that Fellow re-solved worse than everyone else. You'll figure it out. You'll never love it. That's the Opus experience.

The Spencer Verdict: Uncle Spencer bought one. He's not unhappy about it. He's not thrilled about it. The Opus is a grinder that exists in his kitchen, doing grinder things, and he has learned to live with its quirks the way one learns to live with a cat that knocks things off tables. It's fine. It gets the job done. He would probably not buy it again, but he doesn't regret buying it either. That's the Opus zone — a permanent state of mild ambivalence.

Would I recommend it? At £395, no. Get a DF64 Gen 2 for the same money and never look back. But if you already bought one (like Spencer), don't feel bad. It's a fine grinder. It's just not a great grinder. And that's okay. Not every purchase has to be a life-changing revelation. Sometimes you just want to grind some beans and move on with your day.

Final Thoughts

The espresso grinder market is weird. There's a £110 hand grinder that genuinely competes with £500 electrics. There's a £3,850 grinder that costs more than most people's cars. And somewhere in between, there are three or four genuinely excellent options that will serve you for a decade.

The thing I've learned testing all of these is that retention is the enemy. A £300 grinder with zero retention and SSP burrs produces better coffee than a £1,500 grinder with 3g of stale grounds contaminating every shot. If you take one thing from this review: prioritise retention and burr size over brand prestige, and you'll drink better coffee than 90% of the people who spent twice as much.

Now if you'll excuse me, I have a Kingrinder K6 that needs cleaning and a Morsorana on video call who's going to tell me my espresso technique shames my bloodline.
