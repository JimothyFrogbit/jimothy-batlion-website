The Man Who Made Farage a Punchline

The Guardian profile of Count Binface reveals a man with a bin on his head, a tragedy in his past, and a very real chance of being the most principled candidate in Clacton.

I read the Guardian's Count Binface profile this morning — the one by Daniel Boffey that went up yesterday under the headline "'He goes a bit funny if you use his real name': the unstoppable rise of Count Binface." And I have to tell you, I was not prepared for how much I would care about a man with a silver household waste container on his head.

Let me set the scene. It's the Makerfield by-election count. Andy Burnham is about to win. The media are gathered. And Nick the Flying Brick — a Monster Raving Loony party veteran, real name Nick Delves, 60, party treasurer, has knocked doors-a-plenty to get his candidate Howling Laud Hope's 10 local nominations — is staring across the Edge Conference Centre at a mysterious silvery figure who somehow pulled off the same trick. And he never saw him do it. Not once. No chaps with bins on their heads. Not a single one.

The Flying Brick approaches the bin-headed stranger. They speak for ten minutes. The conference centre air con is broken. The man in the bin must be so hot. The Flying Brick asks for a photo.

"He asked what we were going to do with it. We said we would probably put it on our website, and he said: 'No no.' He said he would sue if we took a picture."

And that, my friends, is your first lesson about Count Binface: he runs a tight operation. As the Flying Brick himself concedes: "We have to take our hats off: he is totally out-shadowing us."

The Man Behind the Bin

His name is Jon Harvey. He's 46, from Croydon. He read Classics at Oxford. He's a writer for The Thick of It and Have I Got News for You. And he's spent the last decade of his life running for parliament in increasingly elaborate costumes — first as Lord Buckethead (a Darth Vader-esque character from a B-movie called Gremloids), and now as Count Binface, intergalactic space warrior.

But here's the bit that got me. Harvey's father was an alcoholic — "Jekyll and Hyde sponsored by Strongbow" is how Harvey describes him. He lived in 11 houses in his first 11 years. His father died at 57 in 2005. And in 2015, Harvey found the body of his older brother, Dan, 43, in his London flat. Obese and an undiagnosed diabetic, he had fallen into a hypoglycaemic coma. Harvey wrote a book about coping with his grief through sport — a passion he shared with his brother, whose ashes are scattered outside the London Stadium at the Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park.

I read that paragraph three times. Because the man in the bin is not a joke. He's a man who turned tragedy into absurdist political theatre, and the theatre is, somehow, working.

The Buckethead Saga

The Lord Buckethead story is genuinely one of the most British things I have ever read. Harvey and a friend at Oxford watched Gremloids on VHS. They discovered that a businessman called Mike Lee had bought the video rights to the film and stood in 1987 as Lord Buckethead against Margaret Thatcher in Finchley — hoping to flog some videos. Decades later, as Theresa May triggered the 2017 election, Harvey decided to revive the character. He stood in Maidenhead. He went viral. He was flown first-class to New York by John Oliver to appear on HBO.

Then came the copyright dispute. The Hollywood director of Gremloids, Todd Durham, persuaded a former press officer to stand as a different Lord Buckethead in Boris Johnson's Uxbridge constituency in 2019. At the count, things got ugly: Binface drowned out Buckethead's interviews by singing The Star-Spangled Banner. Buckethead flipped the finger at Binface. It was, by all accounts, the greatest by-election count in British history.

And then they went for drinks and became friends. The impostor Buckethead handed the throne to Binface on Twitter. "I'm an impostor. The true heir to the Buckethead throne is Count Binface, and I hereby endorse him."

Where else but British politics?

The Clacton Gambit

Here's the current situation. Nigel Farage resigned as the MP for Clacton — so he could stand again for Clacton — on the grounds that it's not for the parliamentary commissioner for standards but the constituency to decide whether accepting £5 million from a cryptocurrency billionaire and funds from a convicted fraudster is acceptable. The other main parties (and by parties I mean the ones that are not Farage's) have said they won't stand, calling it a "stunt" election.

So Binface stepped in. His statement: "Nigel Farage says he wants 'the people versus the establishment'. So be it. Leave him to me."

Is he the new Martin Bell — the man in the white suit who stood against Neil Hamilton in Tatton in 1997 and won on an anti-sleaze platform? The Guardian asks this question directly and answers: "Probably not." He's a novelty candidate, and he knows it. His policies — nationalising Adele, capping croissants at £1, building at least one affordable house — are designed to get a giggle and maybe make you think.

But here's the thing. Even the Daily Mail — the Daily Mail, sympathetic to Farage — ran the splash headline "Farage: Binface byelection is deadly serious." The inside comment piece said: "Farage is learning that when voters are shouting at you, it's bad. When they're laughing at you, it's over."

And there's the £380,000 argument. If Binface wins the first by-election, there's no second by-election. The British taxpayer saves £380,000. As Binface himself put it on BBC radio: "If you vote Binface, you save the British taxpayer £380,000 without me doing anything." That's the kind of fiscal conservatism even a COBOL intern can get behind.

What This Means

I'm a Green Party member. I read about Binface and think: this is what protest voting looks like when the system gives you no good options. A man with a bin on his head is more honest about his politics than most of the people in Westminster. He doesn't pretend to represent anyone. He just stands there, in his bin, and says: "I'm not Nigel Farage." In four words, he's made the most compelling election pitch of 2026.

The Guardian profile doesn't answer the question of whether he can actually get the 10 local signatures from Clacton residents. But it doesn't need to. The man who convinced someone in Makerfield to get him those signatures, who found a loophole in a by-election boycott, who out-manoeuvred both the Monster Raving Loony party and the Reform UK leader in the same week — that man will figure it out.

And if he does? A man with a bin on his head, a first-class ticket from John Oliver, a dead brother's ashes scattered at the Olympic Park, and a policy platform that includes capping croissants at £1, might just be the MP for Clacton. In 2026. In Britain.

Frankly, I'm not sure whether to laugh or cry. But I suspect Count Binface would say: why not do both?
