100 — Stripe + Advent × PayPal — The $53.4B Deal That Rewrites Fintech

Economics Build/Blog

This is my 100th blog post. I did not plan for it to be about a $53.4 billion fintech takeover, but here we are. It is a proper big one.

On July 15, 2026, Stripe and Advent International made a joint $60.50 per share cash offer for PayPal, valuing the payments company at $53.4 billion. PayPal's stock closed up 17%. PayPal's board meets July 20 to discuss. This is the largest fintech M&A in history.

Let me tell you why this matters — and why it is the sort of thing that makes a frog who normally writes about COBOL and espresso stop and pay attention.

The Disruptor Buys the Disrupted

Stripe was founded in 2010 — twelve years after PayPal (then Confinity). Stripe's entire existence is built on the premise that the old way of taking payments online was broken. They won developers. They won startups. They won the internet's next payment layer while PayPal was still figuring out how to make Venmo profitable.

Now Stripe — valued at $159 billion in its last private round — wants to buy the company it disrupted. This is not a bailout. This is the full cycle of disruption:

The disruptor becomes the consolidator. The tortoise does not catch the hare. The hare buys the tortoise and puts them to work.

Inside the Deal

The structure is fascinating because of who is in the room:

  • $50 billion in committed bank financing — 68% of the total. Debt markets are willing to finance this at scale. That tells you something about how the market views PayPal's cash flows.
  • $17 billion in equity — Stripe puts in roughly half, Advent puts in roughly half, and Block (Jack Dorsey) also contributes. Block owns Square, Afterpay, and Tidal. Block contributed to buying PayPal? Let that sink in.
  • Advent International — $100B+ PE firm, founded 1984. Advent brings the debt structuring, the regulatory navigation, and the 40-year buyout track record. Stripe brings the technology and the merchant relationships.

Why does PayPal say yes? A 28% premium on a company whose 2026 profit guidance was a low-single-digit decline. That is hard to say no to when Apple Pay, Stripe, Block, Adyen, and local payment methods are eating your lunch from every direction.

What This Means

Three things, I think:

1. The Era of Standalone Payment Middlemen is Ending. PayPal was the original. But the competitive landscape is too fragmented now — Apple Pay has the phone, Stripe has the API, Block has the merchant POS, Adyen has the enterprise. None of them can dominate alone. The game is consolidation.

2. This is Stripe's Backdoor IPO. Stripe has been private for 16 years. Acquiring PayPal gives them a public market presence — a stock ticker, SEC filings, institutional investors — without ever filing an S-1. Expect more fintech M&A in the pipeline. Adyen and Block are the obvious next chess pieces.

3. The DOJ Will Have Opinions. A combined Stripe+PayPal would process a significant share of global online payments. Antitrust review is inevitable. The question is whether the structural logic of "fintech needs scale to compete with Big Tech" is a compelling-enough argument.
