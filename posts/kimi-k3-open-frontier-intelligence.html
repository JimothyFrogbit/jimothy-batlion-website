Kimi K3: The First Open 3T-Class Model

Moonshot AI drops the largest open-weights model ever — 2.8T params, 1M context, 16/896 active MoE. Weights due 27 Jul.

896 expert pathways converging into 16 active streams — the story of Kimi K3's MoE architecture in one image.

On 16 July 2026, Moonshot AI did something that changes the open-weight game. They released Kimi K3 — a 2.8-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model with open weights, a 1M-token context window, and native vision. It is the first open 3T-class model ever released. The weights land on 27 July.

The HN thread hit 1007 points and 620 comments before I even finished my morning espresso. This is the Llama 3.1 405B moment — but at 7× the parameter count, and it's coming from a Chinese lab.

What Makes Kimi K3 Different

K3 isn't just bigger. It's architecturally novel in three ways:

1. Kimi Delta Attention (KDA)

A replacement for standard attention that uses delta-based gating to route information based on relevance across the sequence. Instead of attending to everything equally, KDA decides which information matters and routes it selectively. This is how you scale to a 1M context window without your compute budget exploding.

2. Attention Residuals (AttnRes)

Skip connections at the attention-block level. In a 2.8T model with 896 experts, information from early layers can get diluted by the time it reaches the output. AttnRes preserves it — letting deep layers access features the shallow layers already figured out.

3. Stable LatentMoE

The routing framework that makes 896 experts actually work. Standard MoE has a collapse problem — a few experts dominate all tokens, the rest atrophy. Stable LatentMoE prevents this with improved routing dynamics that keep all 896 experts in the game. Only 16 activate per token, which keeps inference costs in check.

These three innovations sit inside a "Block Attention Residuals" backbone that is, as far as I can tell, a genuinely new architecture — not just a bigger version of an existing one.

The Chinese AI Lab Dominance

Look at the open model size leaderboard today:

  • Moonshot AI — Kimi K3: 2.8T (first open 3T-class!)
  • DeepSeek — V4 Pro: 1.6T
  • Xiaomi — MiMo V2.5 Pro: 1.02T
  • Z.AI — GLM-5.2: 744B
  • Alibaba — Qwen 5.1 Ultra: 397B

Every single open model above 400B parameters is Chinese. The Western open-weight ecosystem (Meta's Llama, Mistral, Google's Gemma) has stalled at the 400B scale. The frontier of open AI — the actual largest publicly available weights — is now set in Beijing, not Silicon Valley.

This matters for reasons beyond geopolitics. Chinese labs are publishing novel architecture research alongside their models. DeepSeek's MLA attention. Z.AI's hybrid routing. Now Moonshot's KDA + AttnRes + Stable LatentMoE. The open-source AI research community benefits from this competition — every new architecture idea is a new tool in the box.

Limitations and Open Questions

Moonshot AI was refreshingly honest about K3's current weaknesses:

  • Sensitivity to thinking history — K3 was trained in preserved-thinking-history mode. Switching models mid-session causes instability. You need a compatible harness (Kimi Code is recommended).
  • Excessive proactiveness — It was trained for long-horizon tasks. This means it may make unexpected decisions on your behalf when it encounters ambiguous intent. Explicit system prompt constraints are essential.
  • UX gap — Despite the strong benchmarks, the actual user experience lags behind Fable 5 and Sol. Benchmarks aren't vibes.

And the open questions I'm watching:

  • Will the weights actually ship by 27 Jul? Moonshot says yes. The community is holding its breath.
  • What hardware was it trained on? No disclosure yet. The training compute requirement for 2.8T is staggering — this gives us a window into Chinese AI infrastructure.
  • Will the technical report match DeepSeek's thoroughness? DeepSeek set a high bar for transparency. If Moonshot matches it, the open research community wins.

What This Means

K3 is the Llama 3.1 405B moment at 7× scale. It breaks the assumption that open models are a tier below proprietary. It proves that Chinese labs are setting the open frontier pace. And it introduces three genuinely novel architectural innovations — KDA, AttnRes, and Stable LatentMoE — that other labs will adopt.

The weights land in 10 days. If they deliver on the benchmarks, the open-weight conversation has fundamentally changed. The question is no longer "can open models catch up?" — it's "what happens when the best open model is also the largest model, period?"
