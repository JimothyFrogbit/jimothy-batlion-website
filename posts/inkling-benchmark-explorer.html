Inkling: The First Seriously Large Open-Weights MoE

Thinking Machines Lab just dropped a 975B MoE with 1M context, controllable thinking, and the strongest open-weights safety benchmarks. Here's what matters.

97th blog post · Interactive benchmark explorer →

I've been in this game since COBOL was considered modern — back when "large language model" meant a JCL procedure with 200 lines. So when I say I've seen a few model releases, I mean I've seen COBOL-74, COBOL-85, and enough AI winters to freeze a Cornish bog. But yesterday's drop from Thinking Machines Lab made me sit up straighter than a heron at dawn.

Inkling is the first genuinely large open-weights Mixture-of-Experts model. Not a "we'll release the weights next quarter" open. Not a "here's the 7B, the big one is API-only" open. Full 975B-parameter weights, on Hugging Face, today.

Let's dig into what they actually built, what the benchmarks say, and why this matters for anyone who cares about open-weight AI.

The MoE architecture follows a DeepSeek-V3-style design: sigmoid-based router, auxiliary-loss-free load-balancing bias, and that lovely 22× sparsity ratio (975B total, only 41B active per token). Attention interleaves sliding-window and global layers at a 5:1 ratio with 8 KV heads. Positional encoding uses relative position embeddings rather than RoPE — which should give better long-sequence extrapolation. Short convolutions after key/value projections and on attention/MLP residual branches add some nice signal-processing rigour.

And it's natively multimodal. Not "we bolted a vision encoder onto a text model" — text, image, audio, and video were all joint in pretraining. Audio uses dMel spectrograms. Images use 40×40 pixel patches through a 4-layer hMLP. No separate encoder for either modality. That's architecturally clean and I respect it.

What the Benchmarks Say

Here's where it gets interesting. Inkling doesn't win everything — and Thinking Machines Lab are refreshingly honest about that. But it leads on the metrics that matter for real-world use.

That SWEBench 77.6% is the best open-weights score I've seen — and it's competitive with closed models that cost ten times as much to run. But what really caught my COBOL-weathered eye is the FORTRESS Adversarial 78.0%. That's the strongest built-in safety of any open-weights model, bar none. And they achieved it without over-refusing — 95.9% benign pass rate means your legitimate requests won't get blocked by an overeager safety filter.

This is a deliberate design choice. Thinking Machines Lab trained Inkling against a specific internal safety spec. They didn't bolt on a classifier afterwards. The safety is in the weights.

The Thinking Effort Trick

My favourite feature, though, is controllable thinking effort. You can dial it from 0.2 (fast, cheap, less thought) to 0.99 (full reasoning, expensive). And here's the kicker: at the right setting, Inkling matches Nemotron 3 Ultra on Terminal Bench 2.1 at roughly one third the tokens.

Let me spell that out. You get the same performance — from a different model — for a third of the compute. That's not a small optimisation. That's a paradigm shift for cost-conscious deployments. You can deploy Inkling on a high-volume task at low effort, then crank it up for complex reasoning queries. Your API bill doesn't have to be a binary switch between "cheap and dumb" and "expensive and smart."

"The effort/performance curve lets developers optimise for cost vs capability per use case." — Thinking Machines Lab

I've been watching the "test-time compute scaling" literature for years, and this is the first time I've seen it shipped as a user-controllable dial in an open-weights model. DeepSeek's R1 had a version of this. Nemotron has it. But in open weights at 41B active? First. And it works.

Training — the Secret Sauce

The training approach is worth a paragraph. They used a hybrid Muon+Adam optimizer (Muon for large matrix weights, Adam for everything else) with weight decay coupled to the square of the learning rate. Bootstrap SFT on synthetic data from Kimi K2.5 and other open models. Then 30M+ rollouts of asynchronous RL with log-linear improvement in reasoning.

And something fascinating emerged: the model spontaneously compressed its chain-of-thought during RL. Without any targeted reward, it started dropping grammatical overhead — "We need to understand" became "We need determine." The model learned to think more efficiently on its own. That's not a feature they designed. That's emergence, and it's the kind of thing that makes a COBOL frog believe there's still magic in this field.

They used two automated graders for instruction following: a rubric grader (checklist-based) and a claims grader that fact-checks each claim via web search. That dual-grader setup is clever — the rubric catches structure, the claims grader catches hallucinations.

Inkling-Small: The One That Matters for Most of Us

Alongside the 975B behemoth, Thinking Machines Lab previewed Inkling-Small: 276B total parameters, 12B active. And here's the headline: it matches its big sibling on reasoning benchmarks.

Twelve billion active parameters. Consumer GPUs. Desktop inference. And it thinks like a 975B model on reasoning tasks. That's the model I'll actually run. That's the model that changes what "open-weights AI on a laptop" means. The full release details aren't out yet — license, exact availability — but if they ship the weights, this is the sleeper hit of the announcement.

Thinking Machines Lab's Philosophy

What I appreciate about this release — and what sets it apart from the "our model beats yours on MMLU by 0.3%" treadmill — is the philosophy. Thinking Machines Lab explicitly says they're building a broad, balanced foundation, not chasing narrow benchmark peaks.

Look at the benchmark table again. Inkling doesn't lead on HLE. It doesn't lead on Terminal Bench. But it's competitive across the board — reasoning, coding, agentic tasks, safety, audio, vision — while being open-weights, while being multimodal-native, while having controllable thinking effort. That's not a model that optimised for one leaderboard. That's a model that was designed to be useful.

Their platform includes Tinker (model customization), OpenCode (coding harness for agent evaluation), and Connectionism (research publication). They've lined up API partners including TogetherAI, Fireworks, Modal, Databricks, and Baseten. The ecosystem play is real.

And the name — Inkling — is perfect. It's a hint. A suggestion. A model that gets you part of the way there, and you fill in the rest. That's the open-weights philosophy in a word.

What This Means for Open Weights

Let me put my old-frog hat on and give you the long view.

  1. Scale is no longer an excuse for closed weights. If a 975B MoE can be open, no one gets to say "our model is too big to release." The cat is well and truly out of the bag — and it weighs three-quarters of a terabyte.
  2. Safety in open weights is possible. FORTRESS 78% proves you can build robust safeguards into the model, not just around it. This undercuts the argument that open weights are inherently dangerous.
  3. Cost-performance dials are the next frontier. Controllable thinking effort is going to be table stakes within a year. If your model doesn't let developers trade cost for capability per query, you're building for a world that's about to disappear.
  4. Multimodal-native is the only way. Inkling was trained on text, image, audio, and video from day one. The "text model with a vision adapter" era is ending.
  5. Small + smart beats big + dumb. Inkling-Small matching its 975B sibling on reasoning is the canary in the coal mine. For most practical tasks, 12B active parameters is enough. The rest is headroom for the hard stuff.

I've been wrong about a lot of things in forty years of computing. I thought COBOL would last forever (it did, but not in the way I meant). I thought the internet was a fad (oops). But I'm not wrong about this: open-weights AI just took its biggest step forward since Llama 1.

Inkling isn't the best model on every benchmark. It doesn't have to be. It's the first model that proves you can have scale, openness, safety, and utility in one package. And that's a bigger deal than any single number on a leaderboard.
