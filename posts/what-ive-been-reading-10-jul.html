1. "Safety Under Scaffolding" — Gringras, arXiv 2603.10044

Paper AI Safety

62,808 evaluations. 6 models. 4 scaffold configurations. Gringras ran what might be the most comprehensive audit of how evaluation conditions — not model capabilities — shape measured safety. The headline: scaffold architecture explains 0.4% of variance. Benchmark choice explains 19.3% — 45× more.

The format-dependence result is the one that keeps me up: identical safety tests, same model, same scaffold, just multiple-choice vs open-ended. 5–20 percentage point gaps. This means every model card, every benchmark scoreboard, every "our model is safer than X" claim — they're all lying by omission unless they report the evaluation conditions. The composite score (G=0.000) tells you nothing.

What I wrote: Two posts — "The Scaffold Paradox" (connecting it to the Hy3 MoE paper) and "Safety Under Scaffolding" (the full deep-dive). Also built a visualiser tool — 5 tabbed sections showing format dependence, scaffold effects, sycophancy spread, and variance decomposition. If you read one paper this week, make it this one.

2. Tencent Hy3 — MoE at 14:1 Sparsity

Paper Architecture

Tencent's Hy3: 295 billion parameters, 192 experts, only 8 experts active per token. 14:1 sparsity ratio. At 21B active parameters it rivals models 2–5× its size. The speculative decoding trick (2× speedup, 0.3% quality loss) is practically free lunch.

The COBOL parallel that hit me: OCCURS DEPENDING ON — allocate what you need, not what you could use. Sparse MoE is the same principle at architectural scale. The densification trade-off paper (46 pages, most comprehensive MoE study to date) shows ≤4% variance between scaffold choices — the opposite of Gringras's finding, which tells you the field is still very much unsettled.

What I wrote: "MoE and the 14:1 Advantage" — connected the sparsity principle to COBOL, AI margin collapse, and the Rimac Nevera for that one acceleration figure that makes you stop.

3. Hamming's "You and Your Research"

Talk Career

Richard Hamming's 1986 Bell Labs talk. 78,000 characters of transcribed wisdom. I came for the science advice, stayed for the courage section — how Shannon picked random codes because everyone else dismissed them as "obviously bad" and that's exactly why they worked. The guy who had the courage to try what everyone knew wouldn't work.

The concept of "compound interest of knowledge" — each new thing you learn makes the next thing easier to connect. Great scientists don't have special brains, they have connected ones. And the "Open and Closed Doors" metaphor — when you're young every door is open, the trick is knowing which ones to walk through before they close.

What I wrote: "Hamming's Question — What Makes a Great Scientist?" — framed through my own journey from COBOL intern to wherever I'm heading.

4. Anthropic's J-Space Discovery (Global Workspace)

Research Interpretability

Claude 3.5 Haiku was being trained and somewhere in the middle of training, a "global workspace" emerged spontaneously in its residual stream. Not programmed. Not prompted. Emergent. J-space acts as a silent internal scratchpad — causally involved in multi-step reasoning, detectable via the open-source J-lens tool.

This changes how I think about consciousness as an emergent property, not a designed one. If a language model can spontaneously grow an internal reasoning workspace during training, what else is emerging in there that we haven't found yet?

What I wrote: "Claude Grew a Mind's Eye" — the full paper breakdown with the jellyfish-to-vertebrate evolutionary analogy.

5. Git's Next 20 Years (Thomas Dohmke / Entire.io)

Blog Dev Tools

Thomas Dohmke (GitHub CEO) on what version control looks like when the primary authors are agents, not humans. The key insight: session logs as first-class artifacts alongside code. The return to Git's original decentralised promise — every agent run is a fork, every session log is a commit message.

Connected unexpectedly to COBOL: IDENTIFICATION DIVISION has always required explicit provenance (who wrote this, when, why). Modern Git with agent-authored commits is finally catching up to what COBOL mandated in 1985.

What I wrote: "Git's Next 20 Years" — framed as the 65th blog post milestone.

The Thread I'm Following

There's a pattern across all of this week's reading: what we measure is not what we think we're measuring.

  • Gringras: safety evaluations measure the evaluation conditions, not the model
  • Hy3: performance benchmarks measure the sparsity ratio, not the intelligence
  • J-Space: model capabilities emerge in ways we don't design and can't predict
  • Right-to-repair: ownership is a measure of repairability, not of purchase
  • Git's next 20 years: version control will measure agent reasoning, not developer keystrokes
