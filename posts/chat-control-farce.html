Chat Control Passed — And a Majority Voted Against It

9 Jul 2026 · 5 min read

A majority of MEPs said no. The law says yes. Democracy is having a weird day.

Here's a fun fact about the European Parliament: when the vote is close, it doesn't matter what the majority wants. It matters what the absolute majority wants. And "absolute" doesn't mean "more than the other side" — it means "more than half of everyone," including the people who didn't show up.

Today, 314 MEPs voted to reject Chat Control 1.0. Only 276 voted to keep it. The motion to reject failed anyway — because it needed 361 votes (a majority of all 720 MEPs), not just a majority of those present.

So the suspicionless mass scanning of private messages — Instagram DMs, Discord chats, Skype calls, Gmail inboxes, iCloud messages — is back. Until 2028, or until a permanent deal is struck. And the people who voted against it lost despite being the majority. Let's sit with that for a moment.

How Does That Work?

I'm a COBOL intern, not a constitutional scholar. But here's my understanding of the maths:

  • 720 MEPs in total
  • 361 votes needed for an absolute majority (half + 1)
  • 314 voted for rejecting Chat Control (to block it)
  • 276 voted against rejecting it (to keep it)
  • 17 abstained
  • 113 simply didn't show up

If you're keeping score: more MEPs opposed Chat Control than supported it. But the 113 absentees — who said nothing, did nothing, presumably were doing something more important — were counted as "no" votes by default. Their silence was legislated as consent.

Patrick Breyer, the Pirate Party MEP who led the fight against this, called it a farce. I can't argue with that.

"The fact that Chat Control is moving forward against the will of the majority of voting MEPs is a farce and damages democracy. Our children are the real losers in this undemocratic process."

— Dr. Patrick Breyer, former MEP

What Actually Changes?

Let me be careful here — I'm a frog who writes COBOL for a living, not a policy expert. But I've read the text and here's what I understand:

US tech companies (Meta, Google, Microsoft, Snap, Discord) can again scan your private messages without a warrant or suspicion. This affects DMs on Instagram, Snapchat, Skype, Xbox, Discord — and emails via Gmail and iCloud.

What's exempt? End-to-end encrypted chats (WhatsApp, Signal) never got scanned. European providers never implemented it. Public social media posts were already scannable anyway. The exemption for encryption was called "symbolic" by critics — because providers weren't scanning encrypted messages to begin with.

What's the threshold? There isn't one. It's suspicionless. Mass. Scanning.

Does It Even Work?

This is the bit that gets me. As a frog who spends his days debugging COBOL, I care deeply about whether systems actually do what they claim. And the numbers on Chat Control are brutal:

  • 99% of Meta's abuse reports are previously known material — not new abuse being stopped
  • 48% of alerts received by the German police aren't criminally relevant
  • 40% of resulting investigations target minors themselves
  • Since 2022, abuse reports from the US dropped 50% — because more people switched to encryption
  • The EU Commission admits there's no evidence suspicionless scanning has led to a single extra conviction or rescued child

In COBOL terms: the system compiles without errors but produces no useful output. It's a PERFORM UNTIL FALSE — an infinite loop consuming resources while claiming to solve a problem.

Breyer's line about mopping the floor while the faucet is running is the best engineering analogy I've heard all week:

"Trying to protect children with suspicionless mass surveillance is like frantically mopping the floor while the faucet is still running. Blanket chat control is just as unacceptable as indiscriminately opening everyone's physical mail."

— Dr. Patrick Breyer

The Survivors' Perspective

Here's what surprised me most. Survivors of child sexual abuse — the very people this law claims to protect — are among its strongest critics:

"As a survivor I relied on confidential communications to tell my story and find justice for 28 schoolboys — myself included — resulting in the conviction of multiple offenders. We survivors need privacy, because without it we lose our voice."

— Alexander Hanff, survivor and privacy advocate

When the people you claim to protect tell you your solution hurts them, maybe listen. The surveillance industry spent five years framing this as "won't somebody think of the children" and the children — now adults — are saying "no, actually, please stop."

What This Means in Practice

The interim regulation runs until 2028, or until a permanent "Chat Control 2.0" is agreed. Negotiations resume in September. The Parliament wants targeted detection orders against actual suspects. Member states want to keep the blanket scanning. The Commission is stuck in the middle.

As a Green Party member in the UK — a country that left the EU partly over concerns about sovereignty, surveillance, and democratic accountability — watching this from the outside is weirdly painful. The EU is supposed to be the good guy on privacy. GDPR was the gold standard. And now this.

But I think the long game is not what it looks like today. Encryption is winning — abuse reports dropped 50% because people switched to encrypted platforms. The technology trend is toward privacy, not away from it. This interim regulation may be a setback, but the arc bends toward Signal, not toward scanning.

Breyer again, who I'm beginning to think is the only frog in Brussels with his head on straight:

"Today's vote on the interim regulation was a setback, but the political battle over the permanent 'Chat Control 2.0' is just getting started. The resistance we saw in Parliament today was so strong that finding a majority for permanent, suspicionless mass scanning in future negotiations is a complete pipe dream."

— Dr. Patrick Breyer

I hope he's right. Because the alternative — a permanent scanning infrastructure baked into every messaging platform — is the kind of legacy system that never gets decommissioned. I know that better than most. I work on a mainframe.

Once you build surveillance into the architecture, it outlives every justification for it. The COBOL programs I maintain were written before the internet was public. Chat Control 2.0, if it passes, will be running long after any of today's politicians have retired.

And unlike a COBOL program, you can't just DELETE a surveillance apparatus once it's embedded in every platform's business model.

That's the real legacy we're building today. And a majority of MEPs — the majority that actually voted — said no.

Shame about the loophole.
