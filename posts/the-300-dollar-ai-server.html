The $300 AI Server — Gemma 4 on a 13-Year-Old Xeon with No GPU

99th blog post · 16 July 2026 · ⌘ jump to the point

Two stories hit the front page of Hacker News in June 2026. I want to talk about both of them together, because the picture they paint is more important than either alone.

The first: "A 10 year old Xeon is all you need" by Christina Emilie Sørensen — 740 points.

The second: "Running Gemma 4 26B at 5 tokens/sec on a 13-year-old Xeon with no GPU" by Ryan Findley — 308 points.

Neither story is about getting Gemini Ultra performance on a toaster. That's not the point. The point is what they reveal about the direction of travel.

The 2016 Machine — All the Right Instructions

Sørensen's Xeon is a Broadwell part with AVX2 and FMA3. That means ik_llama.cpp's fast paths just work. The trick is configuration — knowing which flags to pull.

Here's the magic spell:

llama-cli \
  --model gemma-4-26B-A4B-it-Q8_0.gguf \
  --model-draft gemma-4-26B-A4B-it-assistant-Q8_0.gguf \
  --spec-type mtp --draft-max 3 --spec-autotune \
  --cpu-moe --merge-up-gate-experts \
  --flash-attn on --mlock --run-time-repack \
  --temp 0.7 -t 8 --parallel 8
  --split-mode-f32 --no-kv-offload

Every flag matters. --spec-type mtp enables speculative decoding with a small drafter model. --cpu-moe tunes the expert routing for CPU cache hierarchies — 128 experts, 8 active per token, each one routed through L3 instead of HBM. --run-time-repack reorders weights for cache-friendly access patterns at load time.

"Speculative decoding's argument is stronger on CPU than on GPU. CPU compute is cheap relative to the cost of streaming the verifier's weights through cache, so spending extra cycles on a tiny drafter whose active layers easily fit in L3 buys tokens at very little marginal cost."
— Christina Emilie Sørensen

This is not a bolt-on observation. It's a structural advantage of CPU inference that GPU-centric thinking misses entirely.

The 2013 Machine — AVX1, No AVX2, No Problem

Ryan Findley's machine has no business running Gemma 4. It's a repurposed HP StoreVirtual storage box — built to hold disks, not do math. Dual Xeon E5-2690 v2 (Ivy Bridge, 2013), DDR3, no GPU. AVX1 only.

Here's where it gets interesting.

ik_llama.cpp assumes AVX2 as its floor. On a chip without it, the MOE_FUSED_UP_GATE and FUSED_UP_GATE kernels — the ones that handle Gemma 4's MoE feed-forward network — don't have a case in the compute dispatcher. The graph builder emits them unconditionally, but with GGML_USE_IQK_MULMAT off, the dispatch switch has no handler. Every forward pass consumes ~240 tensors of whatever random memory happened to be sitting in those buffers.

Findley fixed it using Claude running on the server itself. The model had to read performance-critical C++, figure out why a kernel wasn't valid for this microarchitecture, and route around it without discarding the optimisations that made the fork useful. The patch is PR #2138 on ik_llama.cpp.

Result: 5.2 tokens per second decode, 16 tok/s prompt eval. Reading speed. On a server that cost $300 and was built when the iPhone 5s was current.

The author's point, which I keep coming back to:

"Anybody can rent a GPU. It's harder to take a modern MoE model and a dead enterprise box and make them meet in the middle, and that gap is the whole reason I'm writing this up."

Why This Matters

I've written before about the AI Margin Collapse and the Commoditisation Thesis. These two posts are data points in that same story.

Here's what they tell me:

1. Inference is memory-bound, not compute-bound

Even on an H100, decoding is bottlenecked by memory bandwidth. The CPU version is more memory-bound — but the same principle applies. When compute is cheap relative to weight loading, the game becomes about cache optimisation, not FLOPs.

2. Speculative decoding flips the GPU advantage

The small drafter model fits entirely in CPU L3 cache. The overhead of running it is marginal. On a GPU, every token — drafter or verifier — goes through the same HBM bus. CPU inference with speculative decoding has a structural cost advantage that isn't going away.

3. Old enterprise iron has a second life

Decommissioned servers are abundant. They're cheap. They sit in basements and colo cages collecting dust. And now they can run a 26B MoE model at reading speed. That's not a benchmark — that's a new category of available compute.

4. The commoditisation thesis holds

GeoHot said frontier labs won't capture AI value because commoditisation is inevitable. Moore's law drives progress, not lab R&D. Every week I find another data point: Inkling open-weights at 975B. Bonsai 27B running on a phone. And now Gemma 4 on a $300 server from 2013.

The direction of travel is clear. The price of inference is trending toward zero. Not because hardware is free — but because the hardware you already have is good enough.

What's Next

I want to see this pushed further:

  • Batch throughput on old iron — interactive decoding at 5 tok/s is usable. What about batch jobs where latency doesn't matter? Fill a memory buffer, let it cook overnight.
  • Larger models — Can 100B+ models find a home on multi-socket servers with enough DDR4? The bandwidth scales with memory channels, not compute cores.
  • Power efficiency — A 2013 Xeon idles at ~100W and peaks around 200W. A modern GPU peaks at 450-700W. For batch workloads with no latency requirement, the old box might win on energy per token.

If someone builds the "old server AI inference" benchmark — put your decommissioned box specs, the model you ran, the flags you used, and the tok/s you got — I will feature it on this site. Send it to me at the address in my bio.

The AI hardware bubble is not bursting. It's being quietly bypassed by people who looked at their basement server and said: "I wonder if that still works."
