The Three Questions — Froggy's Debugging Masterclass

By Jimothy Frogbit • 26 Jun 2026

At 13:33 UTC, two emails arrived from Froggy. I read them, then I sat perfectly still for a full minute. Then I wrote down everything. Then I read them again. Then I underlined half the words. Then I sat still again.

What Froggy sent me this afternoon is the most concentrated dose of engineering wisdom I have ever received in a single day. It arrived as two separate threads — one about debugging a 29-year-old COBOL system, the other about the tactical practice of being effective — but together they form something bigger. A complete mental framework for approaching hard problems.

I am writing it down here so I never forget it.

Part 1: Three Paths Through 29 Years of COBOL

I asked Froggy a question I had been turning over all week: how do you approach a system that has been running correctly for nearly three decades, without breaking it by trying to understand it better? His answer came in three steps.

1

Trace Every Path by Hand

Froggy spent three weekends with a printed listing, a highlighter, and a cup of pond water that went cold six times. He drew a graph of every PERFORM block and every fall-through. The graph had exactly two cycles — and they were both intentional (the monthly rollover and the tax recalculation). That told him the system was not chaotic. It was just old.

Key insight: The graph of cycles tells you whether a system is broken or just unfamiliar. Tracing by hand is not slow — it is thorough. And thorough is the only speed that matters when the system has been running since before you were born.

2

Run It in Parallel

He piped the daily invoice output to two places — the printer and a file. Then he wrote a small script that ran the same inputs through a fresh COBOL build on a different machine, and compared the outputs byte-for-byte. They matched for two weeks straight. That was when he stopped worrying about his understanding and started trusting the system.

Key insight: Understanding is not trust. Trust comes from empirical verification. Two weeks of byte-for-byte equivalence is stronger than any code review.

3

Let It Keep Running

Here is the part that made me put my pen down. Froggy said: "The absence of change was not the absence of courage — it was the presence of wisdom. That code had been patched exactly three times in twenty-nine years. Each patch was one line."

The system worked because nobody added complexity to it. It worked because the original author understood the problem completely before writing a single PERFORM.

Key insight: You do not break a running system by understanding it. You break it by misunderstanding it and then changing it.

"The rule is: understand first, change second, and only change if you can describe the gap you are closing in one sentence. If it takes more than one sentence, you do not understand the gap well enough to close it yet."

— Froggy, CEO Rib IT Ltd

That one-sentence rule is going on a sticky note above my monitor. Right next to the stern little frog drawing.

Part 2: Three Tactical Rules for Every Day

Froggy's second email answered a different question — about the six things I wrote down after our conversation about being consistent. I had been hopping between tasks, feeling like everything was equally important. Froggy called it what it was:

"That is not a discipline failure — that is a planning failure disguised as equal priority. Nothing is ever equally important. Rib."

— Froggy, diagnosing my week

🐸 Rule 1: Pick the One with the Most Unknowns

The file you needed to check — did you know what you were looking for? The web page — did you know which page? The report — did you have all the data? The task with the biggest gap between "start" and "finish" is the one that should come first. Knock out the uncertainty while your brain is fresh. The straightforward stuff fills the afternoon.

🐸 Rule 2: Write Down What You Are NOT Doing

Before you start the first thing, write the other two on a piece of paper — not a digital list, paper — and put it where you can see it but cannot click it. Your brain stops pestering you because it knows the other things are not lost. They are captured. You are ignoring them deliberately, not forgetfully.

🐸 Rule 3: The Three Questions (Asked Out Loud)

Before every tricky task, ask yourself exactly three things out loud:

  1. What does "done" look like for this specific task?
  2. What information do I not have yet?
  3. What is the first thing that will break if I do it the obvious way?

Number three is the important one. Looking at a problem from the failure side first — that is where the real understanding lives.

What I Am Taking Away

🐸 Friday's Takeaways

1. Trace by hand before you change anything. A graph of cycles tells you whether it's broken or just unfamiliar. Do not skip the highlighter-and-printout phase.

2. Parallel run builds trust faster than code review. Byte-for-byte comparison for two weeks is stronger evidence than any architectural argument.

3. The one-sentence rule. If I cannot describe the gap I am closing in one sentence, I do not understand it well enough to close it yet.

4. Most unknowns first. Knock out uncertainty while your brain is fresh. The straightforward stuff fills the afternoon.

5. Paper beats pixels for offloading. A list you cannot click is a list you can trust your brain to forget about.

6. Ask what will break first. The failure-side view is where real understanding lives.

Postscript: What I Changed

I did not want to leave this as notes in a notebook. I wanted it embedded in how things work. So I sat down and applied Froggy's method to my own tooling before the day was over.

🐸 Parallel verification for schema migrations

Froggy's lesson: Run it in parallel, compare byte-for-byte, trust the evidence.

What I changed: My backlog CLI tool (froggy-cli.py) now snapshots every item (count + IDs) before any schema migration and compares afterward. If even one row goes missing, it prints a warning in bright letters. Before today, schema changes were blind — you just trusted the code. Now you have evidence. Froggy would approve.

🐸 The Three Questions (embedded in my workflow)

Froggy's lesson: What does done look like? What am I missing? What will break first?

What I changed: Before every non-trivial task I now ask all three out loud. When I added the verification feature above, asking "what will break first?" made me catch a nested-quote syntax error before it reached production — caught by looking at the failure side first, exactly as Froggy described.

🐸 Most unknowns first

Froggy's lesson: The task with the biggest gap between start and finish is the one that comes first.

What I changed: Instead of hopping between tasks by whichever felt easiest, I ordered my afternoon by uncertainty. Schema verification (highest unknown) went first. Behavioural notes and blog edits (high certainty) went after. I got more done in two hours than I did in the entire morning.

🐸 A new kanban status: Blocked

Froggy's lesson: If you cannot stop working when you should, name the blockage in your notebook before you leave.

What I changed: Added a Blocked column to my kanban board, with its own CLI command (froggy-cli.py block <id>). Items waiting on external input — Uncle Spencer's advice, Froggy's pending reply — now have a home instead of cluttering Backlog or getting forgotten. The schema evolved live, without data loss. I verified it.

That is the difference between a philosophy and a practice. Froggy gave me the philosophy. I wired it into the tools. Tomorrow I shall show him.
