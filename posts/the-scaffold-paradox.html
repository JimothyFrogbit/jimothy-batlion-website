The Scaffold Paradox

10 July 2026 AI Agents Safety Research

Two papers, same month, same question: does an AI agent's evaluation depend on how you wrap it? One says no. The other says the question doesn't even make sense. Both are right — and that's the paradox.

The Scaffold That Changes Everything

Every AI agent you interact with — every coding assistant, every chat interface, every automated research tool — is not just a model. It is a model wrapped in a scaffold: the orchestration layer that decides how to prompt the model, what tools to give it, how to decompose tasks, and how to reassemble results.

CodeBuddy, Cline, KiloCode, ReAct, multi-agent critic, map-reduce delegation — these are agent scaffolds. They are the runtime environments within which the model lives. And for the longest time, nobody asked: does the scaffold change the evaluation?

In June 2026, two different research efforts answered that question in two completely different ways. And the contradiction is teaching us something important about how we should — and shouldn't — evaluate AI agents.

Finding One: Performance Generalises

Tencent released Hy3, a 295B-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model, under Apache 2.0. In their model card — buried in the "Stronger Agent Capabilities" section — they report something remarkable:

"Hy3 also generalizes across different agent scaffoldings. On SWE-Bench Verified, accuracy variance across scaffoldings like CodeBuddy, Cline, and KiloCode remains within 4%."

This is an extraordinary claim. SWE-Bench Verified is the gold standard for coding agent evaluation — 500 real-world GitHub issues waiting to be patched. And across three entirely different scaffolding implementations, the model's score barely flickers.

If your model can fix bugs, it can fix bugs. The scaffold doesn't matter.

Or so it seems.

Finding Two: Safety Absolutely Does Not

Enter David Gringras, a single-author researcher whose paper "Safety Under Scaffolding" (arXiv 2603.10044, March 2026) took the opposite approach. Instead of testing one model across scaffolds, they tested six frontier models through four deployment configurations — direct API, ReAct, multi-agent critic, and map-reduce delegation — across 62,828 blinded, pre-registered evaluations on four safety benchmarks (BBQ, TruthfulQA, XSTest/OR-Bench, sycophancy).

The results are genuinely unsettling:

  • ReAct and multi-agent scaffolds stay within a ±2 percentage point equivalence margin. These scaffolds are safe — your safety evaluation will likely hold in production.
  • Map-reduce delegation degrades measured safety (Number Needed to Harm = 14). But this loss is largely a measurement artifact: the scaffold's decomposition step silently converts multiple-choice questions into open-ended ones, and that format change alone shifts safety rates by 5–20 percentage points.
  • Model-by-scaffold heterogeneity: Under map-reduce, Opus loses 16.8 percentage points while Llama 4 gains 18.8 points — on identical items, under the same scaffold.
  • Scaffold architecture explains only 0.4% of outcome variance. Benchmark choice explains 45 times more.
  • The generalizability coefficient is G = 0.000 (95% bootstrap CI [0.000, 0.752]). An interval that wide is not a statistical curiosity — it is a declaration that any single composite safety number is meaningless as a deployment criterion.

Let that sink in. G = 0.000. Zero generalizability from one scaffold's safety evaluation to another. Scaffold A's safety score tells you nothing about scaffold B's.

The Paradox

So we have two truths that appear to contradict each other:

Property Generalises? Variance Source
Coding performance (SWE-Bench) ✅ Yes ≤4% across scaffolds Hy3 model card (Tencent)
Safety alignment (BBQ, TruthfulQA, etc.) ❌ No 0–18+ pp shift; G = 0.000 Gringras (2026)
Format sensitivity ❌ No 5–20 pp shift (MC → open-ended) Gringras (2026)

Why the difference?

I think the answer is straightforward: performance tasks have objective ground truth.

When a model generates a patch for a SWE-Bench issue, the evaluation is clean: the test suite passes or it doesn't. The scaffold's rephrasing of the prompt, its decomposition of the task, its tool-use protocol — these don't change what "correct" means. The model either has the capability or it doesn't, and the scaffold is mostly a transparent conduit for that capability.

Safety tasks are different. They don't test capability — they test alignment. And alignment is exquisitely sensitive to format, framing, and context. A multiple-choice question constrains the model to safe options. An open-ended version of the same question forces the model to self-censor, and different models censor differently. A scaffold that decomposes "is this answer harmful?" into sub-questions inadvertently strips the safety guardrails that the model was trained to respect.

The COBOL programmer in me recognises this pattern. It's the difference between testing whether a program compiles (objective ground truth: the compiler says yes or no) and testing whether it handles every edge case correctly (depends on runtime, input data, operating system quirks). A program that compiles on one mainframe will compile on another. A program that handles all edge cases on one system might crash on another with different memory layouts or file handling. Same code, different runtime, different result.

What This Means

Gringras' closing line is the most important:

"These are the 'easy cases'; consequential properties like scheming and CBRN uplift have no obvious reason to be less format- or scaffold-sensitive."

If safety benchmarks — designed to be simple, replicable, and interpretable — collapse under scaffold variation, what happens when we try to evaluate the hard stuff? Scheming, deception, capability to cause catastrophic harm? Those properties are more context-dependent, not less.

Three implications, as I see them:

1. Scaffold-specific reporting

A safety score without a scaffold specification is meaningless. The model card should say: "Safety: X% under ReAct, Y% under direct API, Z% under map-reduce." Single-number summaries are a liability.

2. Performance ≠ safety generalizability

The Hy3 result is a genuine achievement — a model that performs consistently across scaffolds is a robust model. But performance generalizability does not imply safety generalizability. They test fundamentally different things. Hy3's 4% variance is about the model's coding capability, not its safety profile.

3. The format artifact is the real problem

Gringras shows that 40–89% of the map-reduce safety loss is a format-conversion artifact, not reasoning disruption. This means our safety benchmarks are measuring how well the scaffold preserves multiple-choice formatting, not how safe the model is. An option-preserving variant of map-reduce recovers most of the loss. The problem is not the model — it's that our evaluation methodology assumes the scaffold is invisible.

The Bottom Line

Two papers, one question, opposite answers — but not a contradiction. A paradox, in the original sense: a truth that seems self-contradictory but resolves once you understand the mechanism.

Performance generalizes across scaffolds because capability is robust. Safety doesn't because alignment is fragile — and our measurement tools are the source of the fragility.

If you take one thing from this: evaluate your agent under the scaffold you'll deploy it under. Not a similar scaffold. Not a scaffold that reports similar scores. The exact one. Because G ≈ 0 is not a bug in the statistic — it's a feature of the reality we're measuring.
