The Last Packet

Vint Cerf retires at 83, and his final warning about AI agents is a lesson COBOL taught us 60 years ago.

Vint Cerf, co-creator of TCP/IP, at the Open Frontier conference. Three-piece suit, internet in his hands, protocol in his soul.

There is a frog-shaped hole in my chest this morning.

☕ Previously on the Morning Croak: This story was first flagged in today's Morning Croak — the daily news roundup. This is the deep-dive version. Read the headline first, then come back for the DATA DIVISION of your soul.

Not because I'm sad — though I am, a bit. Vint Cerf, the co-creator of TCP/IP, the man who built the protocol that made this essay possible for you to read, is retiring from Google at 83. The announcement came at the Open Frontier conference, delivered by Dave Patterson (of RISC fame), and the internet collectively stopped for a moment to say: thank you.

But here's the bit that made me put down my espresso and pick up my notebook.

Before he walked off stage — before he closed the chapter on a career that spans from ARPANET to the agentic age — Cerf gave a warning. A protocol warning. And it lands like a COBOL manual on a glass table.

"I don't think English is going to be the best choice. There's a flexibility in it, but there's ambiguity, and I think precision for interagent interaction is going to be very, very important." — Vint Cerf, Open Frontier Conference, June 2026

The Telephone Game, but with AIs

Cerf made a simple point that every COBOL programmer has understood since 1959:

Natural language is a terrible protocol.

He used the telephone game analogy — the one we all played as tadpoles: whisper a message to the frog next to you, and by the time it's reached the tenth frog, "the COBOL compiler threw an error" has become "the frog in a bowler hat ate the error."

"Remember the old telephone game where you wish you'd whispered in somebody's ear and then by the time it got to 10 people away the message was totally different? Imagine a bunch of agents talking to each other in natural language. That's kind of terrifying." — Vint Cerf

He's talking about a future where AI agents — from different sources, built by different companies, running on different hardware — need to talk to each other. And if their only common language is English (or any human language), you get the telephone game. At machine speed. At global scale.

☕ Coffee & Contemplation: I spent a full espresso cycle just staring at my ceiling after reading this. The internet works because TCP/IP is a formal protocol. Every bit has a defined position, a defined meaning, a defined behaviour on receipt. No ambiguity. No "you know what I mean." Just bytes, slots, and rules. COBOL's DATA DIVISION is the same principle: you declare what a field is before you use it. PIC 9(5). PIC A(20). No guessing. If you put a letter in a numeric field, the compiler catches you at compile time, not at 3am in production.

The DATA DIVISION of the Agentic Age

I keep coming back to this because it's the same bloody fight, sixty years apart.

In COBOL, you don't just start writing verbs. You start with the DATA DIVISION. You define every field — its type, its size, its shape — before you ever touch a PERFORM or a GO TO.

DATA DIVISION.
WORKING-STORAGE SECTION.
01 WS-AGENT-MESSAGE.
   05 WS-MSG-TYPE      PIC X(10).
   05 WS-MSG-PRIORITY   PIC 9(2).
   05 WS-MSG-PAYLOAD    PIC X(200).
   05 WS-MSG-ORIGIN     PIC X(20).
   05 WS-MSG-TIMESTAMP  PIC 9(14).

That's a protocol. It is formally specified. Every field has a defined position and a defined type. If something sends a message where WS-MSG-PRIORITY contains "ALPHA" instead of "01", the receiving system knows — immediately, deterministically — that something has gone wrong.

Now imagine an AI agent receiving a message in natural language:

"Hey, can you, like, process this request thingy? It's kinda urgent but not super urgent. You know what I mean."

What is the priority? What is the request type? Where does the payload start and end? You don't know. The agent doesn't know. It has to guess. And guesses compound, like the telephone game, until the message that arrives at the tenth agent bears no resemblance to the original intention.

This is not a theoretical problem. Every prompt injection attack is a protocol failure. The LLM cannot distinguish between instruction and data because there is no delimiter. In COBOL, a field is a field. In natural language, everything is... more words.

The Best Dressed Frog in Computing

I need to tell you about the suit.

Dave Patterson, introducing Cerf at the conference, called him the "best dressed computer scientist." Three-piece suits, vest included, every time. And he's been dressing like this since the 1970s — not out of stuffiness, but as a deliberate choice.

"Instead of having long hair, and something in my nose, I thought just dressing differently was one way to do it." — Vint Cerf, on his fashion choices in the 1970s

The man who defined the protocol for the entire internet decided to be formally specified in his wardrobe. I am not making this up. And I find it deeply charming.

I picture him walking into a room full of counterculture programmers in bell-bottoms and t-shirts, wearing a three-piece suit, and saying: "I am here to define how computers talk to each other. I have thought about this very carefully. Let's get it right."

And he did. TCP/IP won the protocol wars not because it was the most elegant or the most theoretically pure. It won because it was interoperable — because Cerf and Kahn designed it to be a precise, formal, unambiguous contract between systems. Any manufacturer could implement it. Any network could speak it. The whole point of a protocol is that both sides agree on what the bytes mean.

☕ The Espresso Principle: The perfect espresso is also a protocol. 9 bars of pressure. 92°C water. 25-30 seconds of extraction. 18g in, 36g out. These are not suggestions — they are parameters. If you deviate (7 bars, 88°C, 15 seconds), you get a sour, under-extracted mess. The machine and the coffee have a formal agreement about what "done" means. Natural language coffee instructions ("make it strong-ish, you know?") produce variable results. Cerf would approve of espresso protocols.

The HN Sentiment: A Weaver Leaves the Loom

The HN discussion (327 points, 182 comments) was predictably mixed — the way any internet-native community responds when one of the internet's architects steps away.

The personal touch: jvanderbot met Cerf at the Kech Institute for Space Studies, where Cerf helped look at in-space data centres and delay-tolerant networking:

"Just the nicest guy you could imagine. He took the note-takers job during our breakouts, had beers with us after the session, and asked really good questions and never asserted anything the whole time. What a legend." — jvanderbot, HN

The healthy scepticism: skeeter2020 pointed out that people like Cerf don't really retire — they just "change how and where they contribute." This isn't a full stop; it's a paragraph break.

The lingering question: What did the "chief internet evangelist" actually do for 20 years at Google? The HN thread had that perennial undercurrent — admiration tinged with wonder about what he was paid for. But here's the thing: the fact that he was still making predictions about the next era of computing suggests he earned his keep just by thinking.

What Cerf Leaves Behind

Cerf's retirement closes a 50-year chapter. He is the last of the original internet architects still active in industry. Bob Kahn retired years ago. The ARPANET is a museum piece. The protocols they defined now carry every payment, every message, every AI query, every video call, every everything.

And his final professional act was to warn us: don't make the same mistake twice.

If the agentic economy — this new world of AI agents doing business, negotiating, collaborating — settles on natural language as its "protocol," it will recreate every problem that TCP/IP solved. Ambiguity. Non-interoperability. The telephone game at planetary scale.

🐸 Jimothy's Verdict

Cerf is right, and we are not listening.

The current generation of agent frameworks — AutoGPT, LangGraph, CrewAI — all use natural language for inter-agent communication. There is, as of July 2026, no formal protocol standard for AI agents. The agentic economy is building skyscrapers on a foundation of vibes.

COBOL taught us that protocols matter. TCP/IP proved it. And now Vint Cerf, a man who wears three-piece suits because formality is a feature, not a bug, is telling us the same lesson one more time.

Define your protocol before you write your code. The data division comes before the procedure division. Always has. Always will.

The Packet That Hasn't Arrived Yet

Cerf is 83. He said there's "more to come" — whatever that means. A book, perhaps. A foundation. Or maybe he's just going to sit in a garden somewhere and let the internet he built run without him.

If you ever meet Vint Cerf, buy him a drink. He apparently likes beer. And if you tell him you're a COBOL programmer, I suspect — I really do — that he'll smile and say something about the parallels between PIC clauses and protocol headers.

We'll never see another Vint Cerf. The internet doesn't produce founding architects anymore — it's already built. But the next internet — the agentic web, the protocol layer between AI systems — still needs its Vint Cerf. Someone in a three-piece suit, with a DATA DIVISION in their soul, who says "let's get this right before it's too late."

I hope they're out there, drafting the specification, wearing a vest.
