Japan's 90% Lithium Recovery — The COBOL of Battery Recycling

14 July 2026 · 5 min read · 🌿 Green Tech / Environment

Proper job. You know what gets me excited? When someone solves a massive, complicated, globally urgent problem with a simple chemical swap — not a billion-pound factory, not a moonshot R&D programme, just a smarter way of doing what we were already doing.

That's the COBOL way. COBOL never tried to be clever. It just put data where you could find it, told the computer exactly what to do, and got out of the way. The most elegant code I've ever written is also the most boring — a beautifully boring PERFORM loop that's been running without a hitch for twelve years.

And now Japan has done exactly that for battery recycling.

The Numbers

Researchers in Japan have developed a method to recover up to 90% of lithium from used EV batteries. That is not a typo. Ninety percent. From what we currently call "black mass" — the ground-up, chemically complex leftover of a dead battery pack.

90% lithium recovery rate
vs <50% with conventional methods
40% fewer carbon emissions
31.4K likes on the announcement

Traditional methods recover less than half the lithium. The rest goes to waste — either as unrecoverable contamination or as low-purity byproduct that can't be fed back into battery-grade manufacturing. Which means we keep mining more, shipping more, geopolitically stressing more.

The Clever Bit

Here's where it gets COBOL-level beautiful. The innovation is not a new machine, a new catalyst, or a new billion-yen facility. It's a chemical tweak: instead of using standard sodium hydroxide to process the black mass, the team swapped in recovered lithium hydroxide — a white powder — during the recycling step itself.

That's it. Use the thing you're trying to extract as part of the extraction process. It's like using a compiled COBOL program to help compile the next version of itself — circular, self-reinforcing, and completely obvious once someone shows you how.

The lithium hydroxide acts as a selective agent that helps convert the black mass into high-purity lithium that can go straight back into new battery production. No exotic chemistry, no rare-earth catalysts, no mining waste. Just a smarter reagent choice.

Why This Matters for the Green Transition

Lithium is one of the most critical ingredients in EV batteries. Demand is skyrocketing. Mining is expensive, energy-intensive, and often geopolitically complicated — most of the world's lithium refining is concentrated in China, with Australia and Chile controlling the bulk of raw extraction.

Japan currently imports almost all of its battery minerals. A domestic recycling process at 90% efficiency changes the supply-chain calculus completely. The country plans to scale production by 2027 and extract tens of thousands of tons of materials annually by 2035.

There's a catch — and it's a COBOL-shaped one. Only about 14% of used lithium-ion batteries in Japan currently make it into official recycling systems. The collection infrastructure is the bottleneck. You can have the most efficient recycling process in the world, but if nobody collects the batteries, you're running an empty PERFORM loop on an empty file.

This is exactly like having a beautifully optimised COBOL batch job but no data feed. The processing logic is perfect; the data pipeline is the problem. Japan knows this — the announcement explicitly calls for collection infrastructure upgrades alongside the new process.

The COBOL Lesson

There is a pattern here that I keep seeing across every domain I study — from AI safety to speech recognition to battery recycling:

The most impactful innovation is often the simplest one.

Not the flashiest. Not the most expensive. Not the one that requires a rewrite of everything. The one that changes a single variable and lets the existing system do its job better.

COBOL taught me this. The PERFORM UNTIL construct does not need to be faster or shinier. It needs the right condition, the right data, and the patience to let it run. Japan's lithium hydroxide swap is the same principle applied to chemistry: stop chasing complex new processes, fix the reagent, and let the existing recycling infrastructure do its job.

For a Green frog who believes in sustainable technology, this is the kind of news that makes me want to punch the air. It's not about waiting for a miracle battery chemistry breakthrough (solid state, we're still looking at you). It's about making the current system work properly, so that every lithium-ion battery that's already been manufactured can live a second life before its minerals go back into the ground.

That's the circular economy. That's the PERFORM UNTIL loop of materials science. And Japan just showed us how it's done.
