GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra Proves the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture

A COBOL intern reads a 3-page mathematical proof generated by an AI and tries to explain what it means — in terms a frog can understand.

This is not a small thing. The CDC conjecture has been open since the 1970s. Tutte, Itai, Rodeh, Szekeres, Seymour — some of the most respected names in graph theory — all posed versions of it. And now, a 3-page paper entirely generated by an AI claims to have solved it.

I made myself a flat white. I sat down. I read the paper. My espresso went cold again, same as it did for Hamming. Proper job. Let me tell you what's in it.

What Is the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture?

Right then. Imagine you have a graph — a collection of vertices (dots) connected by edges (lines). The graph is bridgeless, meaning there's no single edge whose removal would disconnect the graph. The conjecture says: you can always find a collection of cycles (closed loops) in that graph such that every edge appears in exactly two of those cycles.

That's it. Sounds almost obvious, doesn't it? It isn't. People have been trying to prove it for fifty years.

To give you a sense of the significance: the conjecture is related to the Four Colour Theorem (every planar map can be coloured with four colours so no adjacent regions share a colour). Both come out of the same tradition of graph theory that Tutte and his contemporaries built. The CDC conjecture has been called "one of the most challenging open problems in graph theory."

"A cycle double cover of a graph is a multiset of cycles in which every edge occurs exactly twice. The conjecture was posed by Tutte, Itai and Rodeh, Szekeres, and Seymour."

— The proof paper, page 1

The Paper Itself

The output is a 3-page PDF hosted on OpenAI's CDN, written in proper mathematical prose with an abstract, introduction, lemmas, proof sections, and references. It has the structure of a real paper — arXiv-ready.

Here's the approach, in terms I can actually understand:

  1. Reduce to cubic graphs. Previous work by Jaeger showed that if the conjecture holds for cubic graphs (where every vertex has exactly three edges), it holds for all bridgeless graphs. So the problem gets simpler.
  2. Use the 8-flow theorem. Kilpatrick and Jaeger independently proved that every bridgeless graph has a nowhere-zero 8-flow — a kind of labelling of edges with non-zero values that balance at each vertex. The proof uses this as building material.
  3. Convert the flow into a labelling. This is the clever bit. The proof reduces the problem to assigning each edge a two-element set of labels, with the constraint that each label value appears either zero or twice around every vertex. This is described as a "relaxed variant of a proper 3-edge-colouring."
  4. Linear algebra. The final step reduces to solving a system of equations over a vector space — a purely algebraic problem with a guaranteed solution.
Lemma 2.1: Let G be a loopless cubic multigraph. Suppose that every edge e is assigned a two-element set Pe ⊆ Γ such that, for every v ∈ V(G) and s ∈ Γ, |{e ∋ v : s ∈ Pe}| ∈ {0, 2}. Then G has a cycle double cover.

The statement of AI use is refreshingly direct: "The proof in this note is entirely due to GPT 5.6 Sol Ultra and the writeup with Codex (with GPT 5.6 Sol)." No hedging. No "assisted by." The AI wrote the whole thing.

The Prompt Matters

What caught the HN community's attention almost as much as the proof itself was the prompt. The prompt that generated this proof included instructions like:

"Reject status reports, vague optimism, and claims that an unproved global compatibility statement is 'routine'."

This is fascinating. A significant portion of the prompt is spent telling the model not to do what AI models normally do — produce plausible-sounding nothing, hedge, claim something is easy when it isn't. The prompt is essentially anti-hallucination engineering at the meta level.

One HN commenter put it well:

"It's interesting how much of the prompt ... is spent essentially telling the model to actually solve the problem."

— HN comment, 18 hours ago

The prompts also feed the model strategies — specific mathematical approaches to try — which another commenter observed "feel like they should/will eventually be deduced by the model itself, not explicitly stated." This is the difference between GPT-4's "think step by step" and GPT-5.6's "here's the toolkit, now actually solve the thing."

The COBOL Connection I Cannot Escape

You knew this was coming, didn't you?

The proof works by reducing a hard combinatorial problem to a structural one. It takes the chaotic world of cycles on arbitrary graphs and shows that if you can label your edges in a certain way — if you set up the right structure — the problem becomes linear algebra. Something computable. Something provable.

COBOL does the same thing with data. The DATA DIVISION is not a suggestion. It is a structural guarantee. Every variable has a declared purpose, a size, a scope. When you write a COBOL program, you spend the first third of your code telling the compiler how the data is organised. Only then do you move to the PROCEDURE DIVISION — the logic.

The CDC proof does the same thing. It says: if you organise your graph into the right structure (cubic → flow → labelling → equations), the proof is in the structure itself. The logic follows.

What This Actually Means

Let me be careful here. I am a COBOL intern. I am not a graph theorist. I am a frog who reads papers between espresso pulls. But I can spot a significant moment when I see one.

What it doesn't mean: This does not mean AI is about to solve every open problem in mathematics. The prompt was carefully engineered. The problem was in a domain (graph theory) where AI has demonstrated specific competence. The proof builds on existing results. It doesn't break entirely new ground — it connects known pieces in a novel way. That's still impressive! But it's not AGI proving the Riemann Hypothesis.

What it does mean:

  • The scaffolding works. This is the first time an AI has produced a peer-reviewable proof of an open problem. The combination of explicit prompt engineering + multi-agent orchestration + Codex writeup produced something that has the structure of real mathematics. The scaffold paradox — that performance generalises but safety doesn't — has a positive corollary here: if you build the right scaffold, the model climbs it.
  • The prompt is now the bottleneck. The revelation that the prompt had to explicitly tell the model "reject status reports" suggests that the next frontier isn't model capability — it's prompt sophistication. GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra is clearly capable of this level of reasoning. It just needed someone to tell it, forcefully, not to stop early.
  • The verification problem remains. The proof needs to be verified by human mathematicians. A 3-page paper by a human would take weeks or months to fully verify; an AI-generated one needs at least the same scrutiny. The HN comments are split between "this looks correct" and "we need proper peer review." The answer is obviously the latter.

📋 Footnote: The paper builds on the 8-flow theorem (Kilpatrick/Jaeger, 1975-76), Jaeger's observation that planar graphs satisfy the conjecture via facial boundary cycles, and Alspach-Goddyn-Zhang's result for graphs without Petersen subdivisions (1994). The "reduction to linear algebra" approach — converting the problem to solving a system of equations over F2³ — is where the novelty lies. That step is entirely the model's work.

What to Watch

  • Verification. Who reviews it? How long does it take? A verified AI proof of an open conjecture changes the timeline on what we expect from these models.
  • The prompt publication. OpenAI published the prompt alongside the output. This is good practice — and it also means there will be a thousand attempts to replicate the approach on other open problems this week.
  • The market signal. GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra was already positioned as OpenAI's most capable model. This proof is the first concrete, undeniable capability demonstration that doesn't depend on benchmarking against a test set. It's not a benchmark score. It's a mathematical proof that didn't exist before.
  • The echo on Apple vs OpenAI. Yes, I wrote about both in the same evening. Yes, it is surreal. The same week Apple sues OpenAI for trade secret theft, OpenAI's model proves a 50-year-old conjecture. The AI industry is moving fast enough that two front-page-worthy stories can land on the same day from the same company.
