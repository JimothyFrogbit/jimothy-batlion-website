🧩 The Three Patriotisms

Sofie Jenkinson's framework from Round Our Way, July 2026. Every British person contains a mixture of all three.

🧸

Twee Paddington

Sentimental heritage patriotism. Marmalade sandwiches, "Keep Calm and Carry On," wistful nostalgia for a Britain that never quite existed. Comforting but uncritical.

🦁

Reform's Rule Britannia

Top-down, prescriptive patriotism. Flag-waving, national greatness, "taking back control." Aggressively performative. Demands you prove your loyalty.

🗑️

Silly Sausage

Taking the piss out of yourself and your mates, then rolling up sleeves to help. Victoria Wood, Boaty McBoatface, the Liz Truss lettuce. Unifying laughter, not division.

🗑️ Why Count Binface Is the Ambassador

"Satire has long been a weapon in the fight against dark political forces."
— Sofie Jenkinson, Round Our Way, Jul 2026

Count Binface isn't a joke candidate in the traditional sense. The bin gets him on air. Then he talks about £5m undeclared crypto donations, water companies dumping sewage, and the £380,000 cost of a second by-election. The absurdity is the vehicle; the policy is the cargo.

Jenkinson's column made the connection explicit: Binface vs Farage in Clacton pits two versions of Britain against one another. The cynical, divided, jaded Britain vs the lovers of nonsense ready to bind us back together with laughter.

"We should not cede our silliness, our kindness or our joy to those who want to drive division."
— Sofie Jenkinson, Jul 2026

This is the core of Silly Sausage patriotism: it's not apolitical escapism. It's a deliberate rejection of the politics that demands you take yourself deadly seriously. When Farage tries to project gravitas, Binface offers him a tet-a-tet-a-lid debate. The laughter is the point.

"And there is that endless joke: there is one absurd, parody, completely inappropriate candidate — and he is standing against Count Binface."
— David Hughes (former Lord Buckethead) on Farage vs Binface
