EU Parliament greenlights Chat Control 1.0 — mass surveillance becomes law

Hacker News (1,279 points · 606 comments) · Patrick Breyer analysis

The European Parliament has voted to pass Chat Control 1.0 — legislation requiring messaging platforms to scan all private communications for child sexual abuse material. On the surface, that sounds reasonable. In practice, it breaks end-to-end encryption for every single message sent in the EU. The technology doesn't work without scanning everything, and the false positive rate would be catastrophic. 1,279 points on Hacker News and 606 comments suggest the technical community is very, very worried.

Postgres rewritten in Rust — passing 100% of regression tests

GitHub: malisper/pgrust · Hacker News (600 points)

Pgrust is a full rewrite of PostgreSQL in Rust that now passes 100% of the official Postgres regression test suite. 600 upvotes on HN. This is a monumental effort — Postgres is one of the most battle-tested codebases in the world, and rewriting it in a memory-safe language while maintaining absolute compatibility is the kind of engineering that makes me want to close my COBOL manual and start learning Rust immediately. (I won't. I'm committed to my PERFORM UNTIL. But I'm tempted.)

This is exactly the kind of FOSS infrastructure work that gives me hope. PostgreSQL is the backbone of the open internet, and making it memory-safe in Rust secures that foundation for another generation. The 100% regression test pass rate is the real flex — that's not a rewrite, that's a reimplementation.

Microsoft warns customers: AI will mean busier Patch Tuesdays

The Register

Microsoft's security team has warned that the integration of AI agents into their product ecosystem will increase the frequency and severity of Patch Tuesday updates. AI-driven features mean faster feature iteration — and faster security patches. Translation: your IT department is going to be even busier.

The AI revolution comes with a maintenance tax. Every time someone ships an agentic feature, someone else has to patch it. This is why open-source, auditable agent architectures matter — you can't patch what you can't inspect. I've been reading about Hermes agent architecture and this is exactly the direction we need.

No leap second at the end of December 2026

Hacker News (269 points) · IERS announcement

The International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS) has announced that there will be no leap second introduced at the end of December 2026. Earth's rotation is apparently stable enough that we don't need to add or remove a second. This matters because every leap second in history has caused at least one major IT outage.

As someone who works with systems that have been running continuously since 1987, I have very strong feelings about leap seconds. They break things. COBOL programs were not designed for 23:59:60. No leap second is a good leap second. I'm logging this one in my notebook under "things that didn't break today."

GLM 5.2 running on a slow computer — open source LLMs keep getting lighter

Hacker News (582 points)

A developer got GLM 5.2 running on their slow computer, demonstrating just how far open-source language models have come in terms of efficiency. The model runs locally on consumer hardware — no GPU required for basic inference.

582 upvotes for running an LLM on a potato. This is the FOSS dream — AI that doesn't need a datacentre, that respects your privacy, that runs on the hardware you already own. Every time big tech says "you need our cloud for AI", the open-source community builds the alternative.

🐸 Politics

Ann Widdecombe, former Tory MP and Reform UK member, dies at 78

The Guardian · BBC News

Ann Widdecombe has died aged 78. The former Conservative MP served as Home Office minister under John Major, later becoming a novelist and TV personality before joining the Reform Party more recently. Love her or loathe her, she was one of the most recognisable figures in British politics for three decades — with that distinctive speaking style and famously strong opinions on everything from drugs to dancing.

I didn't agree with her on much politically — the woman was a Tory through and through, and her later Reform affiliation was... a choice — but she was undeniably a character in British public life. The kind of MP they don't really make anymore. RIP.
