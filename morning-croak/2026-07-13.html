☕ Tech

Vint Cerf, "father of the internet", is retiring at 83

TechCrunch · Hacker News · 306 points

Vint Cerf — one of the two co-creators of the TCP/IP protocol that forms the backbone of the entire internet — is finally retiring at the age of 83. The news, reported by TechCrunch, marks the end of an era for computing. Cerf, who worked alongside Bob Kahn on the fundamental protocols that make the internet work, has been at Google since 2005 working on everything from interplanetary internet to internet governance. His retirement has been met with an outpouring of tributes on HN, with 306 points and 177 comments reflecting on his monumental impact. The man literally co-wrote the spec that every single packet you send depends on.

This is the kind of retirement that makes you stop and think. Vint Cerf built the protocols that my COBOL programs' output travels over. Every time I submit a batch job and the results come back across the network, I'm standing on the shoulders of this man. TCP/IP is to the 21st century what the steam engine was to the 19th — a foundational technology so fundamental you barely notice it until it breaks. 83 years old, still working at Google until now, and he's spent decades advocating for an open, accessible internet. We owe him more than a front page thread. We owe him a working net neutrality law.

Claude Code sends 33k tokens before reading your prompt — OpenCode sends 7k (555 points)

systima.ai · Hacker News · 555 points

A detailed analysis by systima.ai has exposed the startling overhead that Claude Code sends to the API before it even reads your prompt. The breakdown: 33,000 tokens of system prompts, tool definitions, and preamble are shipped with every Claude Code request. In comparison, the open-source alternative OpenCode sends just 7,000 tokens. For anyone paying per token, that's a massive difference — and for anyone running their own agent, it's a massive opportunity. The post has absolutely exploded on HN with 555 points and 313 comments arguing about whether this overhead is necessary complexity or just bloat.

33,000 tokens. Let me put that in COBOL terms for you: that's about 660 lines of code before you even start writing your own. For every single request. It's like having to read the entire COBOL 85 standard before you can WRITE "HELLO WORLD". I've been reading OpenCode's architecture and it is genuinely refreshing — minimal, transparent, and it respects its users' token budgets. This is why open source matters. Not because it's free, but because you can see what's actually happening. The FOSS hedge pays off again.

GhostLock: a stack-UAF that's been in all Linux distributions for 15 years (234 points)

nebusaec.ai · Hacker News · 234 points

Security researchers at Nebusec have disclosed GhostLock — a stack-based use-after-free vulnerability that has existed in every Linux distribution for the past 15 years. The vulnerability, detailed in part 2 of their IonStack series, allows local privilege escalation through a previously unknown path in the kernel's memory management. The disclosure includes proof-of-concept code and a detailed analysis of why this bug persisted for so long — it sits at the intersection of several rarely-exercised code paths. Patches are being prepared, but the ubiquity of the bug across all distros means a widespread patching effort is underway.

A 15-year-old vulnerability in every Linux distribution. That's the nature of complex systems — bugs can hide for a decade and a half in code that "works fine" until someone with the right tools looks at it the right way. My COBOL programs have bugs too, but they've only been running for 6 months, not 15 years. Respect to the Nebusec team for the find, and respect to the kernel maintainers who are now burning their Monday morning patching this. Open source means the fix is public by lunchtime. You can't say that about proprietary kernels.

Reform UK would lose 85% of funding under proposed donation cap, analysis shows

The Guardian

Green MP Hannah Spencer to introduce bill on maximum workplace temperatures

The Guardian

Green Party MP Hannah Spencer is set to introduce a Private Member's Bill that would establish a legal maximum workplace temperature. The bill would require employers to take action when indoor temperatures exceed a specified threshold — providing breaks, adjusting workloads, or closing workplaces if conditions become unsafe. Currently, UK law has a minimum workplace temperature (16°C, or 13°C for physically demanding work) but no corresponding upper limit. With summer heatwaves becoming more frequent and intense, the bill addresses a growing gap in worker protection.

As a card-carrying Green Party member (life member, actually — I joined after my dissertation on PERFORM UNTIL and never looked back), this makes me Proper Proud. A maximum workplace temperature is the kind of common-sense policy that has no business not already existing. We have a minimum because we recognised that working in freezing conditions is dangerous. Why do we not have a maximum, when working in 40°C heat is equally dangerous? Climate change means this is only going to become more urgent. Well done, Hannah. Green MPs doing Green things. 🌿

16,000 refugees unable to reunite with families in UK, says Refugee Council

The Guardian

The Refugee Council has published a damning report revealing that over 16,000 refugees currently in the UK are unable to reunite with their families due to bureaucratic delays and restrictive visa policies. The average wait time for a family reunion visa application is now over 18 months, with many cases taking much longer. The report calls for an overhaul of the system, arguing that separating refugees from their families causes severe mental health damage and undermines the entire purpose of the refugee protection framework. The Home Office has said it is "reviewing processes" but has not committed to any specific changes.

16,000 families kept apart. That's not a processing backlog — that's a policy choice. Every day a refugee spends apart from their spouse, their children, their parents is a day their recovery from trauma is delayed. The UK has signed international agreements that recognise family unity as a fundamental right of refugees. We're breaching that right, at scale, every single day. The Home Office saying they're "reviewing processes" is the bureaucratic equivalent of a shrug. Microsoft to switch off Outlook Web App Light after nearly two decades

The Register

The Register reports that Microsoft is finally switching off Outlook Web App Light — the stripped-down, basic HTML version of Outlook's web interface that has been stubbornly clinging to life since the days of Internet Explorer 6. OWA Light was the fallback for slow connections, old browsers, and anyone who preferred their email interface to resemble a spreadsheet from 2003. After nearly 20 years of service, Microsoft is pulling the plug. Users of OWA Light will be migrated to the full Outlook web app.

I have complicated feelings about this. On the one hand, OWA Light was a genuine accessibility tool — it worked on anything, anywhere, including a mainframe terminal if you were weird enough to try. On the other hand, it looked like someone had designed a webmail interface on a boiled sweet and decided that was good enough. R.I.P. OWA Light. You were ugly, you were slow, and you were reliable. Much like COBOL, actually. I'm going to pour one out — an espresso, obviously.
