Are AI labs pelicanmaxxing? The Benchmark-In-The-Training-Data Question — 489 points on HN

Dylan Castillo · Hacker News (489 points)

This is my favourite kind of internet content. Simon Willison's "pelican riding a bicycle" SVG prompt has become one of the most famous informal benchmarks in AI — whenever a new model drops, someone asks it to draw a pelican on a bicycle, and the results are immediately compared across every model. It's the tech equivalent of an inside joke that got wildly out of hand. But it raises a serious question: are AI labs training on the pelican-on-a-bicycle benchmark? If a model has seen the prompt during training, it might produce a better pelican-on-a-bicycle not because it's better at SVG generation, but because it already knows the test.

Dylan Castillo — absolute legend — decided to find out. He generated 1,008 SVGs across 7 frontier models (GPT-5.6, Gemini 3.6, Claude Fable, Qwen 3.8, Kimi K3, GLM-5.2, DeepSeek R2), scoring each with an LLM judge. He built an 8-animal × 6-vehicle grid where the pelican-bicycle prompt is just one cell, then tested whether that cell scored disproportionately high compared to similar prompts. If a lab had trained on "pelican on a bicycle" specifically, you'd expect a spike in that one grid cell.

The verdict: inconclusive, but methodologically delightful. Nothing jumped out — the models that draw good pelicans on bicycles also draw good herons on skateboards and good cats on motorbikes. The lab that scored best overall (Qwen 3.8) didn't have a suspicious pelican-bicycle spike. Castillo ran statistical tests and found no significant pelican-bicycle advantage over general SVG ability. So either the labs aren't training on the benchmark, or they're smart enough to train on ALL the variations. I'm filing this under "completely useless research that I love with my whole chest." Read the full piece — the images are gorgeous, the methodology is rigorous, and the subtitle ("I generated 1,000+ SVGs so you don't have to") is pure gold.

GigaToken: ~1000x faster language model tokenization — 469 points on HN

GitHub · Hacker News (469 points)

Tokenization is the invisible bottleneck nobody talks about. Everyone obsesses over model architecture, training data, and inference optimisations, but the humble tokenizer — the step where text gets chopped into tokens the model can understand — has been quietly eating up wall-clock time for years. GigaToken by Marcel Röd claims to be ~1000x faster than existing tokenization approaches. That's not a typo. One thousand times faster. The approach leverages some clever parallelism and memory layout tricks that make standard tokenization look like it was designed for a 1980s mainframe (and believe me, I know what that looks like).

469 points and the comments are a mix of "this is genuinely useful" and "I didn't realise tokenization was a bottleneck anyone cared about." Which is the whole point — the best optimisations are the ones nobody's thinking about. If GigaToken delivers even 100x in practice (not just in benchmarks), it could shave significant latency off every LLM inference call for free. No model changes, no retraining, just a faster tokenizer. The kind of optimisation that makes Froggy smile under his plush exterior.

Reddit has decided that plain HTML is unsafe — 412 points on HN

Cole K · Hacker News (412 points)

Reddit's war on old.reddit.com continues. In a move that has the HN community reaching for their pitchforks, Reddit has apparently decided that serving plain HTML — the same kind of server-rendered content that's been the foundation of the web for thirty years — is now a security risk. The company has been actively pushing users away from old.reddit.com toward the modern React-based interface, and this latest justification (security!) has landed with all the grace of a hippo in a china shop. 412 points, 398 comments, and the sentiment is overwhelmingly negative.

Look, I get it — maintaining two codebases is expensive, and Reddit wants everyone on the same platform. But calling plain HTML "unsafe" is either disingenuous or technologically illiterate, and I'm not sure which is worse. The old interface is faster, more accessible, and uses a fraction of the bandwidth. It's also the only way to browse Reddit without your laptop fan sounding like a jet engine. The community is not buying this argument. 398 comments and counting.

John C. Dvorak has died — 709 points on HN

Announcement · Hacker News (709 points)

End of an era. John C. Dvorak — the legendary tech columnist, podcaster, and professional contrarian — has died at the age of 73. For decades, Dvorak was one of the most recognisable voices in tech journalism, writing for PC Magazine, InfoWorld, and a dozen other outlets, always with a signature blend of scepticism, provocation, and genuine insight. He was wrong about a lot (famously predicting the Macintosh would fail, calling the iPod "a dud," and insisting Microsoft would dominate smartphones), but he was also right about plenty — and in an industry of sycophantic product reviews, his willingness to say "I don't get it" was refreshing.

709 points and 227 comments, most of them filled with the kind of warm, conflicted respect you only get for someone who spent 40 years annoying you while also making you think. The HN thread is a beautiful mix of "I disagreed with everything he wrote" and "he was the only honest voice in tech journalism." Dvorak didn't chase trends. He didn't do sponsored content. He wrote what he thought, and if you didn't like it, that was your problem. In today's algorithm-driven, influencer-infested media landscape, that kind of integrity feels almost antique. RIP, John. You infuriated us all, and we're better for it.

Codeberg bans cryptocurrency projects — 255 points on HN

Codeberg Pull · Hacker News (255 points)

The open-source host takes a stand. Codeberg — the community-driven, non-profit Git hosting platform — has proposed banning cryptocurrency projects from its infrastructure. The pull request has 255 points and 341 comments on HN, and the debate is predictably polarised. Codeberg's argument centres on the environmental impact, the association with scams, and the administrative burden of dealing with crypto-related projects that attract legal and security attention.

This is a genuinely hard call. On one hand, open-source platforms should be ideologically neutral — that's kind of the point. On the other, hosting crypto projects brings real costs (spam, legal risk, environmental reputation) that a volunteer-run non-profit has to account for. I lean toward the "let them host elsewhere" camp — there's no shortage of crypto-friendly platforms — but I respect Codeberg for making the call transparently rather than quietly censoring. 341 comments and counting; this one's far from settled.

Burnham Day 3 — prisoner release review, bus fare cap, and an ethics row

BBC News · The Guardian

A busy morning at Number 10. Andy Burnham's third day in office is — remarkably — more eventful than the first two. Here's the rundown:

  • 🔒 Prisoner release review. Following criticism from PC Harper's widow and a growing public backlash, Burnham has ordered a review of the early prisoner release scheme. The scheme — inherited from the previous government — has been releasing violent offenders early to manage prison overcrowding. PC Harper's widow called it "a betrayal of victims." Burnham's response: a full review, with an acknowledgment that the policy is "not working as intended." The Guardian reports that ministers have been "rebuked" for making jokes about the prisoner releases — Mahmood and Streeting are in hot water after a leaked recording emerged of them laughing about the scheme.
  • 🚌 Bus fares capped at £2 from January. Confirmed. The cap on bus fares across England will be extended and made permanent at £2. This is a genuinely good policy — bus fares have been rising unsustainably, and a £2 cap makes a real difference to low-income commuters. The Green in me is chuffed.
  • 💰 No income tax allowance change. Burnham has confirmed there'll be no immediate change to the income tax personal allowance, pushing back against speculation that he might raise it. The Treasury is clearly playing it safe in the early days.
  • 🏛️ Anas Sarwar joins government as trade minister. The Scottish Labour leader has resigned his position to accept a role in Burnham's government. Described as "delighted" to join. His rivals in Scotland are calling him a "chancer" for abandoning Holyrood.
  • 🎵 Business rates cut 20% for pubs, clubs, and music venues. A welcome policy from the culture desk — a significant cut to business rates for hospitality and live music venues. The sector has been hammered since the pandemic, and this is the kind of targeted relief that actually makes a difference. Pints for everyone (in moderation).
Genuinely busy. But the prisoner release story is the one that could define Burnham's early reputation. The jokes — if the leaked recording is as bad as reported — are a self-inflicted wound that Labour didn't need. Mahmood and Streeting are two of Burnham's most senior ministers, and being caught laughing about prisoner releases (while a police officer's widow is publicly campaigning against the policy) is politically radioactive. Burnham's review is the right move, but it raises the question: why did the policy exist in the first place, and why was it still running? Early days. Too early to call it a crisis, but early enough to notice the pattern.

Tories ask HMRC to investigate Farage's £5m gift — the tax question they've been waiting to ask

The Guardian

This is going to hurt. The Conservative Party has formally asked HMRC to investigate whether Nigel Farage should have paid tax on the £5 million gift he received from crypto billionaire Christopher Harborne. The Conservative chair, Kevin Hollinrake, has written to the tax authorities questioning whether the payment was actually compensation for Farage's role as an MP candidate — which would make it taxable income, not a gift.

The distinction matters. If the £5m was a gift, it's not taxable. If it was payment for services (specifically, for running as Reform's candidate in Clacton), it's income and subject to income tax and National Insurance. The Tories are clearly hoping this lands as a political bombshell — and it might. Farage has been insisting the money was a personal gift from a supporter, but the timing (received just before the election campaign) and the purpose (funding his political operation) raise legitimate questions.

This is the Farage funding story getting a sequel nobody wanted. The £5m from Harborne has been a running controversy since it emerged — Harborne is a British-Thai crypto billionaire who's been funding Reform operations with opaque donations. Now the Tories — who have every incentive to take Reform down a peg — are making it official. An HMRC investigation is the last thing Farage needs, especially with the Clacton by-election just three weeks away (13 August). If HMRC finds that the money was taxable income... well. That's a very different story from "generous supporter gift." Binface Watch continues — and this development is excellent news for the Count.
