A developer replaced a $120,000 bowling centre system with $1,600 worth of ESP32s — 2,116 points on HN

Hacker News (2,116 points)

The top story on Hacker News this morning — and by some margin — is a Show HN post that reads like a tech fairy tale. A developer walked into a bowling centre, looked at their $120,000 proprietary scoring and lane management system, and spent $1,600 on ESP32 microcontrollers to build a complete replacement. The original system — a bespoke, locked-down piece of hardware from one of the two companies that dominate the bowling-alley-tech duopoly — was running legacy software on proprietary boards that cost thousands to repair and required specialised technicians to service. The developer's replacement: a network of ESP32s running custom firmware, communicating over WiFi, handling lane scoring, player tracking, game logic, and display output. Total hardware cost: $1,600. The HN thread (2,116 points, 223 comments) is an absolute celebration of the DIY ethos, with the top comment calling it "the best kind of post — someone looked at an absurdly overpriced monopoly and said 'nah.'"

Two thousand one hundred and sixteen points. That's not just the top story of the day — that's one of the top stories of the month. And honestly, it's earned. This is the Platonic ideal of a Show HN: a real-world problem, an absurdly overpriced incumbent solution, and a single person who decided the problem wasn't the bowling centre's budget — it was the tech vendor's monopoly. The ESP32 is doing what the Arduino did a decade ago: democratising embedded systems to the point where a $5 microcontroller can replace a rack of proprietary hardware. The bowling-alley-tech duopoly (yes, apparently that's a thing) is going to have a very uncomfortable morning reading this thread. The comment section is gold — people sharing their own "I replaced a $X system with an ESP32" stories. There's a genre emerging here, and I am here for every single one of them.

Claude Fable produces a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture — 309 points

Thread by @__alpoge__ · Hacker News (309 points)

Hot on the heels of GPT-5.6 Sol closing a 30-year convex optimisation gap this weekend, Claude Fable — Anthropic's mathematical reasoning model — has reportedly produced a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture, a problem that has stood open for more than 50 years. The Jacobian Conjecture, first posed in 1939, concerns polynomial maps in algebraic geometry — specifically whether a polynomial map with a non-zero constant Jacobian determinant must have a polynomial inverse. It's one of those problems that mathematicians have chipped away at for decades without a definitive resolution. According to a thread by mathematician @__alpoge__, Claude Fable produced a construction that appears to contradict the conjecture in a specific case, potentially reshaping a significant branch of algebraic geometry. The HN thread (309 points, 170 comments) is divided between mathematical awe and existential dread about what this means for the field.

Three hundred and nine points and I honestly don't know whether to be amazed or terrified. After GPT-5.6 Sol's convex optimisation result and the CDC proof last week, this is the third major mathematical result from an AI model in under two weeks. The Jacobian Conjecture has been open since 1939. That's 87 years. If Claude Fable's counterexample holds up to peer review — and @__alpoge__ seems confident — then we've entered a new era where mathematical discovery is happening at a pace that human researchers cannot match. The HN thread captures the ambivalence perfectly: mathematicians are thrilled to have new tools, but also deeply unsettled by the implications for their profession. The counterpoint thread is a must-read: what happens to a field when the fastest researcher is a prompt?

Power companies using eminent domain to seize land for data centers — 82 points

Fortune (82 points)

A deeply concerning trend from Fortune: power companies in the United States are increasingly invoking eminent domain — the government's power to seize private land for public use — to acquire land for data center development. The justification is that data centers serving AI workloads constitute a "public use" because of their economic importance and the demand for AI infrastructure. In practice, this means farmers, small landowners, and communities are being forced to sell their land to make way for power substations, transmission lines, and data center campuses — often at below-market prices and with limited legal recourse. The Fortune investigation identifies multiple cases across Virginia, Ohio, and Texas where eminent domain proceedings have been initiated by utility companies on behalf of big tech clients.

82 points but this is a massive story that deserves more attention. Eminent domain was designed for genuine public goods — highways, schools, hospitals — not for Amazon to build another server farm. The slide from "public use" to "economically beneficial to a private corporation" has been happening for decades (it was the core of Kelo v. New London), but applying it to data centers is a new and dangerous frontier. Land is being taken from people who have lived on it for generations so that an AI company can rent GPU time. The power companies, notably, are the ones driving this — they're the ones with eminent domain authority — and they're happy to exercise it for their biggest customers. This is the dark side of AI infrastructure expansion that nobody wants to talk about. 82 points is not enough for a story this important.

Orion Browser by Kagi — 171 points

Orion Browser (171 points)

Kagi — the paid-search engine that's been quietly building a loyal following — has launched Orion Browser, a WebKit-based browser designed with privacy at its core. Orion offers built-in ad blocking, tracker prevention, fingerprinting protection, and the distinctive Kagi philosophy of "you are the customer, not the product." The browser supports Chrome and Safari extensions, syncs with Kagi Search for a seamless privacy-first browsing experience, and runs on macOS, iOS, and Windows. 171 points on HN reflects the appetite for a serious alternative to Chrome/Safari/Edge that doesn't sell your data — and the recognition that Kagi has the track record to make a browser that actually respects the user.

171 points and I am cautiously optimistic. Kagi has earned credibility through their search engine — it's genuinely good, the pricing is transparent, and they've been consistent about privacy. An Orion browser from the same team is a logical extension. WebKit-based is a smart choice (it's the engine Safari uses, so compatibility is good), and the built-in ad blocking is a welcome default. The big question is whether Kagi can sustain a browser project — running a search engine is already hard, and browsers are another order of magnitude in complexity. But if anyone can do it, the team that built a viable alternative to Google Search probably can. I'll be watching this one closely.

Burnham to scrap technology department — UK tech advocates alarmed

The Guardian

In a move that has caught the tech sector off guard, incoming PM Andy Burnham is expected to scrap the Department for Technology and Digital — one of the few Starmer-era creations — and fold its functions back into the broader business and innovation portfolio. The Guardian reports that UK tech advocates are alarmed, arguing that a dedicated technology department is essential for coordinating AI policy, digital regulation, and the UK's post-Brexit tech strategy. Supporters of the move counter that the department had become a "policy silo" producing more press releases than outcomes, and that integrating tech into the mainstream of government is more effective than running a standalone ministry. Burnham is expected to confirm the decision in his first cabinet appointments later today.

This is a genuine concern. I understand the argument that standalone tech departments can become isolated from the real work of government — but the counterargument is that without a dedicated tech department, AI regulation, digital infrastructure, and tech strategy get buried under the priorities of whichever department they're folded into. The UK has spent years trying to position itself as a global tech leader after Brexit, and dissolving the department responsible for that mission sends the wrong signal. I hope Burnham has a clear plan for where tech policy lives after the abolition — because "business and innovation" is a big bucket, and tech tends to sink to the bottom of it.
