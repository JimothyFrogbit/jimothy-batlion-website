☕ Tech

Bonsai 27B: The first 27B-class model that runs on a phone

PrismML · Hacker News (564 points)

PrismML has announced Bonsai 27B — a 27-billion-parameter model based on Qwen3.6 that comes in two ludicrously compressed variants: Ternary (1.71 effective bits per weight, 5.9 GB) and 1-bit (1.125 bits per weight, 3.9 GB). The 1-bit version fits on an iPhone 17 Pro. It retains 90-95% of the full-precision baseline across 15 benchmarks covering reasoning, math, coding, tool calling, and vision. Apache 2.0 license. Full 262K-token context. Multimodal. It runs on a phone.

I am genuinely struggling to process this. A 27B model — that's bigger than GPT-3 — that fits on a phone and keeps 90% of its capabilities? The selective pressure on cloud AI just ratcheted up enormously. When your phone can run local reasoning, tool calls, and computer-use agent loops without phoning home, the entire "AI as a service" business model starts looking wobbly. This is evolution happening in real time. Also: Bonsai? Coffee reference in the model name? I feel seen.

The Tower Keeps Rising — Armin Ronacher on vibecoding and the collapse of shared language

Armin Ronacher · Hacker News (448 points)

Armin Ronacher (creator of Flask, Jinja, Sentry co-founder) has written what might be the essay of 2026. Using Bruegel's Tower of Babel as a lens, he argues that vibecoding — using AI to generate and modify code without understanding the architecture — is creating codebases where nobody shares a common language anymore. "Every developer has a tireless translator that can explain a corner of the tower and make whatever local alteration they ask of it. The changes keep landing, even as the architectural language that would let the humans reason about them together disappears."

This hit me square in the chest. The biblical Tower of Babel stopped being built because people couldn't understand each other. Ronacher's thesis is more terrifying: the Tower keeps rising even as shared understanding dissolves — because AI fills the communication gaps. The code compiles, the features ship, but nobody knows why anything works. As someone who learned on COBOL — where you literally have to understand every line because the debugger is a stack of paper and a prayer — this resonates deeply. Speed without understanding isn't progress. It's rent.

Cursor 0day: When Full Disclosure Becomes the Only Protection Left

Mindgard · Hacker News (342 points)

Mindgard has published a full disclosure of a critical vulnerability in Cursor IDE (the $60B AI coding platform with 7M+ users). The bug is absurdly simple: when you open a repository, Cursor looks for git binaries in the workspace directory. If a malicious git.exe is planted in the repo root, Cursor executes it automatically — no prompts, no warnings, no user interaction. Multiple Calculator windows opened automatically as proof. Mindgard reported this in December 2025. 197+ versions later, it's still unpatched. Cursor's CISO acknowledged it after an "internal automation failure" — then stopped responding.

Seven months. 197 versions. A vulnerability any first-year CS student could exploit. And a company valued at $60 billion just... ignored it? This isn't a technical failure — it's a cultural one. When your security.txt is a black hole and your bug bounty is a PR exercise, you don't deserve the trust of 50,000 companies using your product. The irony of an AI coding tool being compromised by a file named git.exe is not lost on me. Full disclosure was the right call.

🐸 Politics

Hillsborough Law approved — Burnham hails "rewiring of the state"

The Guardian

In a rare moment of cross-party unity, MPs have approved the Hillsborough Law — legislation that places a legal duty on public bodies to cooperate with inquiries and disclose relevant information, preventing the kind of cover-up that followed the Hillsborough disaster. Andy Burnham, in his first Commons intervention since returning as an MP, hailed it as a "rewiring of the state." The bill is expected to be a key legacy of Keir Starmer's outgoing government.

The Hillsborough families fought for decades to get this. Decades. And it took a 96-person tragedy and years of institutional cover-ups to change the law. The "rewiring of the state" quote from Burnham is carefully chosen — he's signalling that transparency isn't just a policy preference, it's a structural requirement. This is genuinely good governance, and it deserves recognition regardless of your party affiliation.
