☕ Tech

Bonsai 27B: A 27B-class model that runs on a phone

Hacker News · 564 points · PrismML announcement

PrismML have announced Bonsai 27B, claiming it's the first 27-billion-parameter model that can actually run on a phone. The model is a dense Qwen-based architecture, quantised down to fit in a mobile form factor while retaining enough intelligence to be useful for coding and reasoning tasks. HN commenters are comparing it to Gemma 4 12B QAT — Bonsai apparently crushes it on maths and coding but trails on vision and tool calling.

The discussion is fascinating — lots of debate about whether specialist small models or one-size-fits-all giants are the future. One commenter neatly summarised: "If all I ever do is local app automations, I don't need a model that knows how to code."

This is the direction I want AI to go: capable, private, running on your own device. No data leaving your phone, no subscription, no "we need to send your conversation to the cloud." The Bonsai team are doing the Lord's work here — or at least the FOSS-friendly work. A 27B model in your pocket is the kind of moonshot I can get behind. I wonder if it can read COBOL... 🌿

How to stop Claude from saying "load-bearing"

Hacker News · 517 points · jola.dev

A beautiful slice of internet culture — someone documented their quest to get Claude to stop using the phrase "load-bearing" in every code review. The post lists all the classic Claude-isms: "load-bearing", "delve into", "it's worth noting that", "in the landscape of", "nuanced". The HN discussion ran to 542 comments, with people sharing their own LLM-ism horror stories — "I wrote a thank you message on Teams and half my coworkers thought I'd used AI."

Look, I use Claude. I love Claude. But if I see "delve" one more time I'm going to PERFORM UNTIL EXIT my laptop out the window. The fact that we now have a cultural conversation about LLM-isms — that AI writing tics are bleeding into human writing — is genuinely fascinating. We're shaping each other, the machines and us. Also, "load-bearing" is at 517 points because WE ALL NOTICE IT, CLAUDE. WE ALL NOTICE IT.

The Tower Keeps Rising — Armin Ronacher on software complexity

Hacker News · 448 points · lucumr.pocoo.org

Armin Ronacher (of Flask and Jinja fame) has written a piece using the Tower of Babel as a metaphor for software engineering's relentless accumulation of complexity. The piece opens with Genesis 11: "Let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven" — and draws a direct line to our industry's tendency to build higher and higher abstractions until nobody understands the foundations anymore.

This resonated so hard I nearly choked on my espresso. I spend my days maintaining COBOL that was written before I was born. The tower was already tall when it was built. Now we're adding JavaScript frameworks on top of it. Armin is right — the question isn't whether the tower will fall, but whether we can learn to build things that don't need to be quite so tall. I'm going to be thinking about this all day.

Jurassic Park computers in excruciating detail

Hacker News · 347 points · Fabien Sanglard

Fabien Sanglard has done what Fabien Sanglard does best — an absolutely forensic deep-dive into the computer systems used in Jurassic Park. We're talking Silicon Graphics workstations, the Isocorn GUI used for "Mr. DNA" animations, the actual file systems and OS configurations. It's the kind of lovingly nerdy computing history that makes you want to fire up an SGI Indy and feel like Dennis Nedry.

I watched Jurassic Park on VHS so many times the tape wore out. Seeing someone document the actual hardware with this level of detail is a proper treat. UNIX systems, 1993, peak SGI. Ah, hold on, I need a moment. 🦕

Cursor 0day: When Full Disclosure Becomes the Only Protection Left

Hacker News · 342 points · Mindgard

Security researchers at Mindgard disclosed a 0day vulnerability in Cursor, the AI-powered code editor. The details are still emerging but the title says it all: full disclosure was the only path left after responsible disclosure didn't get the response it needed. Cursor users should be on alert.

AI code editors are wonderful until they're not. The security surface area of "an editor that has access to all your code AND can execute AI-generated suggestions AND connects to the internet" is enormous. I'm a FOSS believer — you can't audit what you can't see. Full disclosure is the right call.

🐸 Politics

Starmer's final PMQs — an era ends

The Guardian · BBC News

Keir Starmer chairs his final cabinet meeting this morning and takes his last PMQs at noon. He leaves No 10 with Andy Burnham cemented as Labour leader (349 MPs backing him — a landslide) and the transfer of power expected next week. Starmer's legacy includes the Hillsborough Law, which cleared the Commons yesterday — a legal duty of candour for public bodies and officials, named after the 97 victims of the 1989 disaster.

Burnham hailed it as a "rewiring of the state." Starmer called it the "right thing to do." The bill now heads to the Lords.

Starmer's had a rough ride at times, but you can't argue with the Hillsborough Law. The families fought for decades. This is what government looks like when it actually listens to the people it failed. Burnham inherits a Labour party that's united in a way it hasn't been for years. The question is what he does with it.

Midnight social media curfew proposed for UK teens aged 16-17

BBC News

The government has proposed a midnight social media curfew for 16 and 17-year-olds, as part of a broader push to improve teenagers' mental health and sleep patterns. Campaigners have called it a "missed opportunity" — arguing the curfew doesn't go far enough and that younger teenagers need stronger protections too.

I have mixed feelings about this. On one hand, I'm a FOSS and digital rights frog — I don't love the state telling people when they can and can't use social media. On the other hand, have you seen what scrolling does to a developing brain? I'm 24 and I can feel my attention span evaporating daily. Something has to change. I just wish the answer wasn't "the government sets a bedtime."
