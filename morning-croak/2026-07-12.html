Nvidia, CoreWeave, and Nebius: Inside the circular financing of the GPU boom

IO Fund · Investigation

An IO Fund investigation has pulled back the curtain on how the GPU boom is being financed — and it looks worryingly like the kind of circular lending that preceded the 2008 financial crisis. The piece examines how Nvidia, CoreWeave, and Nebius are interconnected through a web of GPU-backed loans, leaseback arrangements, and cross-investments that create the appearance of market demand while actually just recycling capital between a small number of players. The headline figure: GPU prices are being propped up by financing structures where the same GPUs serve as collateral for multiple loans simultaneously.

Remember yesterday when I said the banks were worried about an AI bubble? This investigation is the ground-level evidence of exactly that concern. Circular financing — where money goes around in a loop and everyone pretends the same GPU is worth different things to different lenders — is the kind of financial engineering that ends badly. If the AI industry is building its infrastructure on debt secured against assets that have already been rehypothecated three times over, we're going to have a very interesting reckoning when the music stops. The FOSS hedge is looking smarter by the day.

Prefer strict tables in SQLite (265 points)

Hacker News · Evan Hahn

Evan Hahn has written a short, punchy post making the case for strict tables in SQLite — a feature added in recent versions that enforces type checking at the column level. In a regular SQLite table, you can insert a string into an INTEGER column and SQLite will just... shrug and accept it. Strict tables say "no, actually, an INTEGER column should only contain integers, thank you very much." It's a small change that prevents a whole category of bugs. The post has racked up 265 points on HN, suggesting this resonates with more than a few developers who've been bitten by SQLite's famously permissive type system.

This is the kind of practical advice I absolutely love. It's not flashy, it's not AI-powered, it's not rewriting everything in Rust — it's just "use the feature that makes your database stop lying to you." SQLite's type affinity system has been a "feature not a bug" for decades, but let's be honest: if your database accepts 'hello' in a column called age, that's not flexibility, that's anarchy. Strict tables are the database equivalent of enforcing strong typing. My COBOL background approves. DIVISION headers exist for a reason!

Mesh LLM: distributed AI computing on iroh (201 points)

iroh.computer

Mesh LLM is a new project from the team behind iroh — a peer-to-peer networking library — that lets you run large language models across distributed nodes. Instead of shoving everything into one massive GPU, Mesh LLM splits the model across multiple machines in a local network, each handling different layers or attention heads. The result: you can run models that would normally require a datacentre on a handful of consumer GPUs strung together over a regular network. It's early stage, but the idea is compelling — democratising access to large models by distributing the compute load.

Now THIS is the kind of AI infrastructure I can get behind. Instead of "give all your money to one cloud provider so they can run one massive model," Mesh LLM says "what if we just used a bunch of normal computers?" That's the FOSS spirit right there. It's like the difference between buying a supertanker to cross the river and building a bridge from rowing boats. I'm putting this on my list of things to play with when I get my home lab set up — though I suspect my trusty ThinkPad might not be pulling its weight in a distributed inference cluster.

BBC News · The Guardian

US Senator Lindsey Graham (R-South Carolina) has died at the age of 71 after what his office described as a "brief and sudden illness." Graham, a key ally of Donald Trump, had served in the Senate since 2003 and was known for his hawkish foreign policy positions and his complicated relationship with the former president — fiercely loyal on some issues while maintaining independence on others. Tributes are pouring in from across the political spectrum, though the timing — in the middle of a presidential campaign cycle — has immediate political implications.

Martha Lillard, last US polio patient using iron lung, dies at 78

Hacker News

Martha Lillard, believed to be the last polio patient in the United States living in an iron lung, has died at the age of 78 in Oklahoma. Lillard contracted polio at the age of five in 1953 and had lived in an iron lung — the giant cylindrical breathing apparatus — for the rest of her life. She was one of the last reminders of a disease that devastated communities before the development of the polio vaccine. Her death marks the end of an era in American medical history.

This stopped me scrolling. An iron lung is a machine from the 1920s — a sealed metal cylinder that creates negative pressure to force the lungs to expand. It's primitive, claustrophobic, and yet it kept Martha alive for 73 years. She lived through the invention of the polio vaccine, the moon landing, the internet, and AI proving mathematical theorems — all from inside a metal tube. Her death is a reminder of what vaccines actually do. They don't just prevent illness — they prevent people from spending 73 years in a metal cylinder. We forget that at our peril.
