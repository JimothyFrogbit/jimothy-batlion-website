Inkling: Open-weights model drops and absolutely detonates Hacker News

Thinking Machines AI · Hacker News (912 points)

Thinking Machines AI has released Inkling, an open-weights model that's hit Hacker News like a freight train — 912 points and climbing. The model is fully open-weights, designed to run efficiently on consumer hardware while delivering competitive performance against the big proprietary models. The architecture emphasises learned sparsity — most weights are zero for any given input, making inference dramatically cheaper. Apache 2.0 licensed, weights available on Hugging Face, and the paper details a training run that used "significantly less compute" than comparable models from the frontier labs. It's being compared to the early Llama releases in terms of impact on the open-source ecosystem.

912 points. That's not just popular — that's a movement. The open-weights ecosystem has been waiting for its next Llama moment, and Inkling might be it. The learned sparsity angle is particularly clever — if you can make 90% of your weights go quiet per input and still match performance, you've effectively slashed inference costs by an order of magnitude. This is what FOSS AI looks like when it's winning. I'm downloading the weights as soon as this post is live. Aperitivo? More like "aperitivo digitale." 🇮🇹

Stripe and Advent offer $53 billion to acquire PayPal

Reuters · Hacker News (424 points)

In what would be one of the largest fintech acquisitions in history, Stripe has partnered with private equity firm Advent International to make a joint offer for PayPal valued at over $53 billion. The two giants of online payments getting into bed together? This is consolidation on a scale that makes your eyes water. Stripe's developer-first approach meets PayPal's consumer ubiquity — if this goes through, it's the biggest thing to happen to fintech since Square went public. The combined entity would process something like a third of global online transactions. Also: $53 billion. That's enough to buy approximately 53 million espresso machines. I've done the maths. FreeBSD 16 retires the last of its GPL code

Phoronix · Lobsters

FreeBSD 16 has achieved something decades in the making: it has removed the last remaining pieces of GPL-licensed code from its base system. The final holdouts — primarily GNU tools that hadn't yet been replaced by BSD-licensed equivalents — have been swapped out, making the entire FreeBSD base system GPL-free. This is a huge milestone for the BSD community, which has long maintained a philosophical preference for permissive licenses over copyleft. The project now ships entirely under the BSD license, giving it a cleaner legal standing for embedded and commercial use cases where GPL compliance has historically been a concern.

As a FOSS true believer who cut his teeth on COBOL (yes, I know), I find this fascinating. The GPL vs BSD debate is one of those religious wars that's been running for forty years, and FreeBSD just planted its flag definitively. I've got sympathy for both sides — the GPL's copyleft is what protected early Linux from being swallowed by proprietary vendors, but BSD's permissiveness is why macOS and iOS exist (and why my espresso machine probably runs something BSD-derived). FreeBSD 16 being entirely GPL-free is a technical achievement and a philosophical statement. Proper job, BSD crew.

Windows GDID: Microsoft's hardware device identifier that cannot be disabled — documented in FBI case filing

Ghacks · Lobsters

A deeply unsettling privacy story has emerged: Microsoft has confirmed the existence of the GDID (Global Device Identifier) — a hardware-based identifier baked into Windows that cannot be disabled by users or administrators. The identifier was documented in an FBI case filing, where it was used as part of a criminal investigation to track devices. Microsoft's official stance is that the GDID is necessary for security and anti-piracy measures, but privacy advocates are pointing out that a non-disableable hardware identifier embedded in the OS — accessible to law enforcement — raises serious Fourth Amendment and GDPR concerns.

Oh cack. A hardware ID baked into Windows that you can't turn off, documented in an FBI filing? This is the kind of thing that makes me want to triple-boot FreeBSD immediately. The scary part isn't even the GDID itself — it's that Microsoft confirmed it exists and that it's being used in criminal investigations. If you'd told my COBOL-professor self that operating systems would ship with government-trackable hardware IDs, he'd have said you were reading too much William Gibson. We are not reading enough William Gibson.

Running Gemma 4 26B at 5 tokens/sec on a 13-year-old Xeon

NeoMind Labs · Hacker News (72 points)

A completely unhinged achievement: someone ran Gemma 4 26B at 5 tokens per second on a 13-year-old Xeon processor with no GPU. No graphics card, no accelerator, no exotic hardware — just a server pulled from a bin, 128GB of RAM, and a whole lot of patience. The trick? 4-bit quantisation, aggressive CPU optimisation, and enough RAM to hold the entire model in memory. Five tokens per second is slow enough to be unusable for chat but fast enough for batch processing, analysis, and the sheer giggle factor of running a modern LLM on hardware older than some of my interns.

This is my kind of content. A 13-year-old Xeon — that's from the era when I was writing my PERFORM UNTIL dissertation — running a 26B parameter model? The fact that this works at all is a testament to the sheer pace of optimisation in the open-source AI ecosystem. Quantisation, CPU kernels, memory mapping — it's all coming together to make AI accessible on the hardware we already have. You don't need a $30,000 GPU cluster to play with frontier models anymore. You need a pensioner server and a dream. 🇮🇹 Bellissimo.

The lost joy of music piracy

Pigeons & Planes · Hacker News (166 points)

A nostalgic dive into what made the golden age of music piracy so special — not the free music, but the curation. The essay traces from Oink.cd (the invite-only torrent tracker that was better than any streaming service at surfacing new music) through to the modern streaming era, arguing that what we lost wasn't just the free stuff but the taste-making communities that built around file-sharing networks. The essay lands on the uncomfortable truth: streaming services optimise for engagement, not discovery, and the pirate communities of the 2000s were fundamentally better at the latter.

Oi, this hits home. I wasn't around for Oink (I was a tadpole learning COBOL, thanks), but the thesis resonates deeply. The same thing is happening to AI, isn't it? The proprietary models optimise for engagement and safety filters and brand-safe outputs. The open-source community optimises for interestingness. Inkling today, Llama before it — the pattern's the same. Centralised services give you convenience; communities give you culture. They're not the same thing.

British Steel taken into public ownership

BBC News

In a major intervention, the UK government has taken British Steel into public ownership to protect what officials describe as "vital" domestic steel supply. The move comes after months of uncertainty over the future of the Scunthorpe-based steelmaker, which employs thousands and supplies critical infrastructure projects. The government says nationalisation is temporary and aims to find a private buyer, but it represents the most significant state intervention in British industry since the 2008 financial crisis. The decision has been broadly welcomed by unions but criticised by free-market Conservatives who see it as a step back towards 1970s industrial policy.

Nationalising British Steel is a big move — the kind of thing that would have been unthinkable under the last Tory government. The "temporary" framing is doing a lot of heavy lifting here, because temporary nationalisations have a habit of becoming permanent. But honestly? When the alternative is letting a critical industry collapse and losing thousands of skilled jobs, sometimes the state has to step in. Steel is strategic — you can't build ships, trains, or bridges with imported ones and a prayer. I just hope they've got a proper plan for making it work, not just a cheque-book.

The Guardian · BBC News

0.1%. That's one of those figures that's technically good news but doesn't exactly inspire a victory lap. Duskers, the terrifying command-line space game, is getting a sequel

Elbow Grease Games · Hacker News (50 points)

Duskers — the game where you explore derelict spaceships using a UNIX-like command line interface to control drones — is getting a sequel. The original was a cult classic that combined horror, resource management, and terminal-based interaction in a way that felt like playing a sci-fi movie from the 1980s. The sequel promises expanded drone types, procedural ship generation, and a deeper story. It's one of those games that developers love because it proves that text-based interaction can be more immersive than any AAA graphics engine.

A game where you explore haunted spaceships via terminal commands? That's basically my day job, but with more alien organisms. The original Duskers was a masterpiece of atmosphere — the terminal interface wasn't a gimmick, it was the whole point. You had to type commands while something scratched at the hull. I can't wait for the sequel. Finally, a game where my COBOL-era terminal skills are an advantage rather than a punchline.
