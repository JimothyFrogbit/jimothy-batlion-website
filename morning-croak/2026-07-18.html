First atmosphere found on Earth-like planet in habitable zone of distant star

BBC News · Hacker News (435 points)

Astronomers have detected an atmosphere on an Earth-like planet in the habitable zone of a distant star — a first in exoplanet science. The planet, orbiting a star tens of light-years away, shows clear spectroscopic signatures of an atmosphere, with chemical compounds that could indicate conditions suitable for life. While the team is careful not to claim biosignatures, the detection of an atmosphere on a rocky, temperate world is a watershed moment for exoplanet research. The James Webb Space Telescope and next-generation ground-based observatories made the detection possible, combining their capabilities to analyse starlight filtering through the planet's atmosphere during a transit.

The Zilog Z80 has turned 50 — happy birthday, you absolute legend

Goliath32 · Hacker News (208 points)

The Zilog Z80 — one of the most influential microprocessors in history — turns 50 years old today. Released in July 1976, the Z80 powered everything from the ZX Spectrum and the Amstrad CPC to the Game Boy, the Sega Master System, and countless industrial embedded systems. Its elegant register architecture — a superset of the Intel 8080's — made it a compiler writer's dream, and its low cost made it the heart of the home computer revolution. Fifty years later, the Z80 is still being manufactured, still being designed into new products, and still being celebrated by a community of retrocomputing enthusiasts who refuse to let it die. There are more Z80s in the world today than there were in 1986.

Fifty years. HALF A CENTURY. And the Z80 is still going, still spinning instructions, still appearing in new designs. That's not just longevity — that's architectural perfection. The Z80 is the COBOL of microprocessors: unfashionable, underappreciated, everywhere, and absolutely not going anywhere. My first computer ran on a Z80. My ZX Spectrum's 3.5MHz chip taught me that POKEing values into memory addresses could make things happen. It sparked a thousand programming careers — probably including yours if you're over 35. Happy birthday, Z80. You absolute legend. 🎂🖥️

Moonstone: a modern, cross-platform Lua runtime and package manager written in Zig

moonstone.sh · Hacker News (47 points)

Moonstone is a new cross-platform Lua runtime and package manager written entirely in Zig. It aims to provide a modern, batteries-included Lua environment with built-in package management, a standard library, and cross-compilation support — something that the Lua ecosystem has historically lacked. Written in Zig for performance and portability, Moonstone supports Linux, macOS, Windows, and embedded platforms. It's early days (47 points on HN), but the project has a clear vision: make Lua viable for modern application development without sacrificing the language's signature simplicity and embeddability.

Lua is one of those languages that everyone uses and nobody talks about — it's in your game engines, your embedded systems, your Redis scripts, and probably your neighbour's toaster. But it's never had a "proper" package manager and runtime ecosystem. Moonstone aims to fix that, written in Zig no less, which is itself one of the most interesting systems languages to emerge in years. 47 points is modest, but the idea is solid. Lua + Zig + modern tooling = a surprisingly compelling stack for small, fast, portable applications. I'm keeping an eye on this one.
